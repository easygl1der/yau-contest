\begin{exercise}
Let $V$ be a finite dimensional complex vector space. Let $A$, $B$ be two linear endomorphisms of $V$ satisfying $AB - BA = B$. Prove that there is a common eigenvector for $A$ and $B$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation:} This problem is a special case of Lie's Theorem, which states that any finite-dimensional representation of a solvable Lie algebra over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero has a common eigenvector. The relation $[A, B] = B$ defines a 2-dimensional solvable Lie algebra.

\textbf{Step 1: Show that $B$ is a nilpotent operator.}
Consider the adjoint operator $\text{ad}_A: \text{End}(V) \to \text{End}(V)$ defined by $\text{ad}_A(X) = AX - XA = [A, X]$. The given relation is $\text{ad}_A(B) = B$, which means $B$ is an eigenvector of $\text{ad}_A$ with eigenvalue $1$.
We claim that for any $k \in \mathbb{N}$, $\text{ad}_A(B^k) = k B^k$.
\textit{Proof by induction:} For $k=1$, it is given. Suppose $\text{ad}_A(B^{k-1}) = (k-1)B^{k-1}$. Then
\begin{align*}
\text{ad}_A(B^k) &= AB^k - B^k A = (AB - BA)B^{k-1} + B(AB^{k-1} - B^{k-1}A) \\
&= B \cdot B^{k-1} + B \cdot (k-1)B^{k-1} = k B^k.
\end{align*}
If $B^k \neq 0$ for all $k$, then $\text{ad}_A$ would have infinitely many distinct eigenvalues $1, 2, 3, \dots$. However, $\text{ad}_A$ is a linear operator on the finite-dimensional space $\text{End}(V)$ (of dimension $(\dim V)^2$), so it can have at most $(\dim V)^2$ distinct eigenvalues. Thus, we must have $B^k = 0$ for some $k$, meaning $B$ is nilpotent.

\textbf{Step 2: Find the common eigenvector.}
Since $B$ is nilpotent, its kernel $W = \ker B$ is a non-zero subspace of $V$.
We check if $W$ is invariant under $A$. For any $v \in W$, $Bv = 0$. Then
\[ B(Av) = (AB - [A, B])v = ABv - Bv = A(0) - 0 = 0. \]
Thus $A(W) \subseteq W$. Since $V$ is a complex vector space and $W$ is a non-zero $A$-invariant subspace, the restriction $A|_W$ must have at least one eigenvector $w \in W$ (by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra applied to the characteristic polynomial).
For this $w$, we have:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $Aw = \lambda w$ for some $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ (since $w$ is an eigenvector of $A|_W$).
    \item $Bw = 0$ (since $w \in \ker B$).
\end{itemize}
Thus $w$ is a common eigenvector for $A$ and $B$ (with eigenvalue $\lambda$ for $A$ and $0$ for $B$).
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M_2(\mathbb{R})$ be the ring of $2 \times 2$ matrices with real entries. Its group of multiplicative units is $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$, consisting of invertible matrices in $M_2(\mathbb{R})$.

(a) Find an injective homomorphism from the field $\mathbb{C}$ of complex numbers into the ring $M_2(\mathbb{R})$.

(b) Show that if $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$ are two such homomorphisms, then there exists a $g \in GL_2(\mathbb{R})$ such that $\phi_2(x) = g\phi_1(x)g^{-1}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{C}$.

(c) Let $h$ be an element in $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$ whose characteristic polynomial $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{R}$. Let $F \subset M_2(\mathbb{R})$ be the subring generated by $h$ and $a \cdot I$ for all $a \in \mathbb{R}$, where $I$ is the identity matrix. Show that $F$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}$.

(d) Let $h'$ be any element in $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$ with the same characteristic polynomial $f(x)$ as $h$ in (c). Show that $h$ and $h'$ are conjugate in $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$.

(e) If $f(x)$ in (c) and (d) is reducible over $\mathbb{R}$, will the same conclusion on $h$ and $h'$ hold? Give reasons.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) A complex number $z = a + bi$ acts on $\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$ by multiplication. In the basis $\{1, i\}$, multiplication by $i$ maps $1 \to i$ and $i \to -1$. Thus $i$ corresponds to the matrix $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
The map $\phi(a + bi) = aI + bJ = \begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}$ is an injective ring homomorphism.

(b) \textbf{Theorem:} This is a specific case of the Skolem-Noether Theorem, but we can prove it directly using representation theory.
Any such $\phi: \mathbb{C} \to M_2(\mathbb{R})$ makes $V = \mathbb{R}^2$ a 1-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-vector space by defining $z \cdot v = \phi(z)v$. Since $\dim_{\mathbb{R}} V = 2$ and $[\mathbb{C} : \mathbb{R}] = 2$, the $\mathbb{C}$-dimension must be $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} V = 2/2 = 1$.
Let $V_{\phi_1}$ and $V_{\phi_2}$ be the two $\mathbb{C}$-vector space structures on $\mathbb{R}^2$ induced by $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$. Since both are 1-dimensional over $\mathbb{C}$, they are isomorphic as $\mathbb{C}$-vector spaces. Let $g: V_{\phi_1} \to V_{\phi_2}$ be a $\mathbb{C}$-linear isomorphism.
Viewed as a map on $\mathbb{R}^2$, $g$ is an $\mathbb{R}$-linear automorphism, so $g \in GL_2(\mathbb{R})$. The $\mathbb{C}$-linearity condition $g(z \cdot_{1} v) = z \cdot_{2} g(v)$ means $g(\phi_1(z)v) = \phi_2(z)g(v)$ for all $z \in \mathbb{C}, v \in \mathbb{R}^2$. Thus $g \phi_1(z) = \phi_2(z) g$, or $\phi_2(z) = g \phi_1(z) g^{-1}$.

(c) The subring $F$ is the image of the evaluation map $\mathbb{R}[x] \to M_2(\mathbb{R})$ given by $x \mapsto h$. The kernel is generated by the minimal polynomial of $h$. Since $f(x)$ is degree 2 and irreducible, the minimal polynomial must be $f(x)$. Thus $F \cong \mathbb{R}[x]/(f(x))$.
Any irreducible quadratic over $\mathbb{R}$ has roots $\alpha \pm i\beta$ with $\beta \neq 0$. The field $\mathbb{R}[x]/(f(x))$ is a degree 2 extension of $\mathbb{R}$, hence isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}$.

(d) For any field $K$, two matrices in $M_n(K)$ are conjugate if and only if they have the same Rational Canonical Form. The RCF depends only on the invariant factors. Here $n=2$ and the characteristic polynomial $f(x)$ is irreducible, so it must be the only invariant factor. Thus the RCF for both $h$ and $h'$ is the companion matrix of $f(x)$. Therefore $h$ and $h'$ are conjugate in $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$.

(e) No. If $f(x)$ is reducible, it could be $(x-\lambda)^2$. There are two similarity classes of matrices with this characteristic polynomial:
1. $h = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}$ (minimal polynomial $x-\lambda$).
2. $h' = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}$ (minimal polynomial $(x-\lambda)^2$).
These are not conjugate because they have different minimal polynomials (or different Jordan forms).
Note: If $f(x)$ has distinct real roots, they are still conjugate (both diagonalizable). The irreducibility in (c) ensures we are in the "field extension" case.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $G$ be a non-abelian finite group. Let $c(G)$ be the number of conjugacy classes in $G$. Define $\overline{c}(G) := c(G)/|G|$.

(a) Prove that $\overline{c}(G) \leq \frac{5}{8}$.

(b) Is there a finite group $H$ with $\overline{c}(H) = \frac{5}{8}$?

(c) Suppose that there exists a prime number $p$ and an element $x \in G$ such that the cardinality of the conjugacy class of $x$ is divisible by $p$. Find a sharp upper bound for $\overline{c}(G)$ in terms of the smallest prime $p$ dividing $|G|$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) \textbf{Explanatory Remark:} The ratio $\overline{c}(G)$ is the probability that two randomly chosen elements of $G$ commute. This is often called the \textit{commutativity degree}.
Using the \textbf{Class Equation}: $|G| = |Z(G)| + \sum_{i=1}^{k} [G : C_G(x_i)]$, where $x_i$ are representatives of non-central conjugacy classes.
Equivalently, $c(G) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |C_G(g)|$.
For $g \in Z(G)$, $|C_G(g)| = |G|$.
For $g \notin Z(G)$, $C_G(g)$ is a proper subgroup containing $Z(G)$ and $g$. Since $G/Z(G)$ cannot be cyclic (otherwise $G$ would be abelian), $|G/Z(G)| \geq 4$, so $|Z(G)| \leq |G|/4$.
Also, for $g \notin Z(G)$, $|C_G(g)| \leq |G|/2$.
\begin{align*}
c(G) &= \frac{1}{|G|} \left( \sum_{g \in Z(G)} |G| + \sum_{g \notin Z(G)} |C_G(g)| \right) \\
&\leq \frac{1}{|G|} \left( |Z(G)| \cdot |G| + (|G| - |Z(G)|) \frac{|G|}{2} \right) \\
&= |Z(G)| + \frac{1}{2} |G| - \frac{1}{2} |Z(G)| = \frac{|G| + |Z(G)|}{2}.
\end{align*}
Since $|Z(G)| \leq |G|/4$, we have $c(G) \leq \frac{|G| + |G|/4}{2} = \frac{5|G|}{8}$. Thus $\overline{c}(G) \leq 5/8$.

(b) Yes. For the dihedral group $D_8$ or the quaternion group $Q_8$, $|G|=8$ and $Z(G) = \{1, r^2\}$ has size 2. The conjugacy classes are $\{1\}, \{r^2\}, \{r, r^3\}, \{s, sr^2\}, \{sr, sr^3\}$. Thus $c(G) = 5$ and $\overline{c}(G) = 5/8$.

(c) If $p$ is the smallest prime dividing $|G|$, and $G$ is non-abelian, then $|G/Z(G)| \geq p^2$. Thus $|Z(G)| \leq |G|/p^2$.
For $g \notin Z(G)$, $[G : C_G(g)] \geq p$, so $|C_G(g)| \leq |G|/p$.
\begin{align*}
c(G) &\leq \frac{1}{|G|} \left( |Z(G)| |G| + (|G| - |Z(G)|) \frac{|G|}{p} \right) = |Z(G)| + \frac{|G| - |Z(G)|}{p} \\
&= \frac{(p-1)|Z(G)| + |G|}{p} \leq \frac{(p-1)(|G|/p^2) + |G|}{p} = \frac{p^2 + p - 1}{p^3} |G|.
\end{align*}
The sharp upper bound is $\overline{c}(G) \leq \frac{p^2+p-1}{p^3}$. For $p=2$, this gives $5/8$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $F$ be a splitting field over $\mathbb{Q}$ of the polynomial $x^8 - 5 \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$.

(a) Find the degree $[F : \mathbb{Q}]$.

(b) Determine the Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(F/\mathbb{Q})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) The roots are $\sqrt[8]{5} \zeta_8^k$ for $k=0, \dots, 7$, where $\zeta_8 = e^{i\pi/4}$.
The splitting field is $F = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5}, \zeta_8)$.
1. $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 8$ as $x^8-5$ is irreducible (Eisenstein at $p=5$).
2. $\zeta_8 = \frac{1+i}{\sqrt{2}}$, so $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8) = \mathbb{Q}(i, \sqrt{2})$, which has degree $\phi(8) = 4$.
3. We examine $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5}) \cap \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8)$. The subfields of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8)$ are $\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{Q}(i), \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}), \mathbb{Q}(i\sqrt{2})$.
Since $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5}) \subset \mathbb{R}$, $K$ must be a subfield of $\mathbb{R} \cap \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8) = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$.
If $\sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5})$, then $x^2-2$ splits in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5})$. The subfields of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5})$ correspond to divisors of 8. The unique subfield of degree 2 is $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ (as $x^8-5 = (x^4-\sqrt{5})(x^4+\sqrt{5})$ is not quite right, rather $\mathbb{Q}((\sqrt[8]{5})^4) = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$). Since $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$, the intersection is $\mathbb{Q}$.
Thus $[F : \mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[8]{5}) : \mathbb{Q}] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_8) : \mathbb{Q}] = 8 \times 4 = 32$.

(b) Let $\alpha = \sqrt[8]{5}$ and $\zeta = \zeta_8$.
Any $\sigma \in \text{Gal}(F/\mathbb{Q})$ is determined by $\sigma(\alpha) = \zeta^k \alpha$ ($k \in \mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$) and $\sigma(\zeta) = \zeta^m$ ($m \in (\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times = \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$).
This is a semi-direct product $C_8 \rtimes (\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times$.
Let $\sigma$ be defined by $\sigma(\alpha) = \zeta \alpha, \sigma(\zeta) = \zeta$.
Let $\tau_m$ be defined by $\tau_m(\alpha) = \alpha, \tau_m(\zeta) = \zeta^m$.
The relation is $\tau_m \sigma \tau_m^{-1}(\alpha) = \tau_m \sigma(\alpha) = \tau_m(\zeta \alpha) = \zeta^m \alpha = \sigma^m(\alpha)$.
Thus $\tau_m \sigma \tau_m^{-1} = \sigma^m$.
The group is $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}) \rtimes (\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times$. Note $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong C_2 \times C_2$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $T \subset \mathbb{N}_{>0}$ be a finite set of positive integers. For each $n > 0$, define $a_n$ to be the number of finite sequences $(t_1, \ldots, t_m)$ with $m \leq n$, $t_i \in T$ and $\sum t_i = n$. Prove that $1 + \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n z^n$ is a rational function.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation:} This is a problem about generating functions for partitions where order matters (sometimes called \textit{compositions}).
Let $f(z) = \sum_{t \in T} z^t$. This is a polynomial since $T$ is finite.
The number of sequences of length exactly $m$ that sum to $n$ is the coefficient of $z^n$ in the expansion of $(f(z))^m$.
The total number of sequences of any length $m \geq 1$ summing to $n$ is $a_n = \sum_{m \geq 1} [z^n] (f(z))^m$.
(Note: the condition $m \leq n$ is always satisfied since $t_i \geq 1 \implies \sum t_i \geq m$).
Summing over $n$:
\[ \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n z^n = \sum_{n \geq 1} \sum_{m \geq 1} ([z^n] f(z)^m) z^n = \sum_{m \geq 1} f(z)^m. \]
This is a geometric series. Since $t_i \geq 1$, the constant term of $f(z)$ is zero, so $|f(z)| < 1$ for small $z$, and the series converges formally:
\[ 1 + \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n z^n = 1 + \frac{f(z)}{1 - f(z)} = \frac{1}{1 - f(z)}. \]
Since $f(z)$ is a polynomial, this is a rational function.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Describe all the irreducible complex representations of $S_4$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
The number of irreducible representations equals the number of conjugacy classes, which correspond to partitions of 4:
1. $(1^4)$: Identity.
2. $(2, 1, 1)$: Transpositions (6).
3. $(3, 1)$: 3-cycles (8).
4. $(4)$: 4-cycles (6).
5. $(2, 2)$: Double transpositions (3).
There are 5 irreps. Let their dimensions be $d_1, \dots, d_5$. We have $\sum d_i^2 = |S_4| = 24$.
- \textbf{Trivial representation ($\chi_1$)}: $d_1 = 1$.
- \textbf{Sign representation ($\chi_2$)}: $d_2 = 1$.
- $S_4$ has a normal subgroup $V_4 = \{e, (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\}$. $S_4/V_4 \cong S_3$. The irreps of $S_3$ have dimensions 1, 1, 2. These lift to $S_4$. The 1D lifts are already $\chi_1, \chi_2$. The 2D lift gives \textbf{$\chi_3$} with $d_3 = 2$.
- Remaining: $d_4^2 + d_5^2 = 24 - (1^2+1^2+2^2) = 18$. Thus $d_4 = 3, d_5 = 3$.
- \textbf{Standard representation ($\chi_4$)}: Consider the action on $\mathbb{C}^4$ by permuting coordinates. The subspace where $\sum x_i = 0$ is a 3D irreducible representation.
- \textbf{Vieri representation ($\chi_5$)}: $\chi_4 \otimes \text{sign}$.
Summary: Dimensions 1, 1, 2, 3, 3.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $a, b > 0$ be irrational such that $\frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} = 1$. Show that $\{[ax]\} \cup \{[bx]\}$ partitions $\mathbb{N}_{>0}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Theorem:} This is \textbf{Beatty's Theorem}.
Let $A = \{ \lfloor na \rfloor : n \in \mathbb{N} \}$ and $B = \{ \lfloor nb \rfloor : n \in \mathbb{N} \}$.
The number of elements in $A$ less than $N$ is the number of $n$ such that $na < N$, i.e., $n < N/a$. This is $\lfloor \frac{N-\epsilon}{a} \rfloor$ (since $a$ is irrational, $N/a$ is never an integer).
Total elements in $A \cup B$ below $N$ is $c(N) = \lfloor N/a \rfloor + \lfloor N/b \rfloor$.
Since $1/a + 1/b = 1$, we have $N/a + N/b = N$.
For any non-integer $x, y$ such that $x+y=N \in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $\lfloor x \rfloor + \lfloor y \rfloor = N-1$.
Thus $c(N) = N-1$.
This means there are exactly $N-1$ integers in $A \cup B$ in the range $[1, N-1]$.
This proves that every integer appears exactly once.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
$X^n + Y^n = Z^n$ in $\mathbb{C}[t]$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Mason-Stothers Theorem:} If $A+B=C$ in $\mathbb{C}[t]$ with $\gcd(A, B, C) = 1$, then $\max(\deg A, \deg B, \deg C) \leq n_0(ABC) - 1$, where $n_0(F)$ is the number of distinct roots of $F$.
Let $A=X^n, B=Y^n, C=Z^n$. Then $n_0(ABC) = n_0(XYZ) \leq \deg X + \deg Y + \deg Z \leq 3 \max(\deg)$.
So $n \max(\deg) \leq 3 \max(\deg) - 1$, implying $(n-3) \max(\deg) \leq -1$.
For $n \geq 3$, $\max(\deg)$ must be 0 (constant solutions).
For $n=2$, $X^2+Y^2=Z^2$ has solutions like $X=t^2-1, Y=2t, Z=t^2+1$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
(a) $\cos(\pi/7) \cos(2\pi/7) \cos(3\pi/7)$. (b) $\prod_{n=1}^{(p-1)/2} \cos(n\pi/p)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) Let $C = \cos(\pi/7) \cos(2\pi/7) \cos(3\pi/7) = -\cos(\pi/7) \cos(2\pi/7) \cos(4\pi/7)$.
Multiply by $\sin(\pi/7)$:
$\sin(\pi/7) C = -\frac{1}{2} \sin(2\pi/7) \cos(2\pi/7) \cos(4\pi/7) = -\frac{1}{4} \sin(4\pi/7) \cos(4\pi/7) = -\frac{1}{8} \sin(8\pi/7) = \frac{1}{8} \sin(\pi/7)$.
Thus $C = 1/8$.

(b) Let $L = \prod_{k=1}^{(p-1)/2} 2 \cos(k\pi/p)$. Note $2 \cos \theta = e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}$.
The values $e^{ik\pi/p}$ for $k= \pm 1, \dots, \pm (p-1)/2$ are the roots of $\frac{x^p+1}{x+1} = 0$.
The product is $1$ if $p \equiv 1, 7 \pmod 8$ and $-1$ if $p \equiv 3, 5 \pmod 8$.
Precisely, $\prod_{k=1}^{(p-1)/2} \cos(k\pi/p) = 2^{-(p-1)/2}$ if we ignore the sign. The sign is $(-1)^{(p^2-1)/8}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
$f_a = x^6 + 3ax^4 + 3x^3 + 3ax^2 + 1$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
Let $y = x + 1/x$. Then $y^2 = x^2 + 1/x^2 + 2$, $y^3 = x^3 + 1/x^3 + 3(x + 1/x)$.
$f_a/x^3 = (x^3 + 1/x^3) + 3a(x + 1/x) + 3 = (y^3 - 3y) + 3ay + 3 = y^3 + 3(a-1)y + 3$.
By Eisenstein's Criterion at $p=3$, $g(y) = y^3 + 3(a-1)y + 3$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$.
Since $[ \mathbb{Q}(x) : \mathbb{Q}(y) ] = 2$ and $[ \mathbb{Q}(y) : \mathbb{Q} ] = 3$, the degree is 6, so $f_a$ is irreducible.
The Galois group is a subgroup of $S_3 \rtimes (C_2)^3$, which is solvable.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Group of order 150 is not simple.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
$|G| = 150 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5^2$.
Sylow 5-subgroups: $n_5 \in \{1, 6\}$.
If $n_5 = 1$, it is normal.
If $n_5 = 6$, $G$ acts on the 6 subgroups. This gives $\phi: G \to S_6$.
If $G$ is simple, $\phi$ is injective. $150 = |G| \leq |S_6| = 720$.
Also $G \subseteq A_6$ (since $G$ has no subgroup of index 2 if simple, but wait, $G$ could have index 2).
However, $A_6$ has order 360. $150$ does not divide 360.
Thus $\phi(G)$ cannot be in $A_6$. If $\phi(G) \not\subseteq A_6$, then $G \cap \ker(\text{sgn})$ is a normal subgroup of index 2.
So $G$ is not simple.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
$SL_2(\mathbb{C})$ representations.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:}
The group $SL_2(\mathbb{C})$ consists of $2 \times 2$ complex matrices with determinant $1$. Its representations are deeply connected to polynomials. The standard representation $V \cong \mathbb{C}^2$ acts on 2D vectors $(x, y)^T$. The $n$-th symmetric power, $V_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n V$, can be naturally thought of as the space of homogeneous polynomials in two variables $x, y$ of degree $n$:
$$P(x, y) = c_0 x^n + c_1 x^{n-1}y + \dots + c_n y^n.$$
The dimension of $V_n$ is $n+1$.

\textbf{(a) Why is $V_n$ irreducible?}
To understand the structure of $V_n$, we look at the action of the diagonal matrices in $SL_2(\mathbb{C})$, taking the form $H = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$. 
When this matrix acts on a basis monomial $x^{n-k}y^k$, it scales $x$ by $\lambda$ and $y$ by $\lambda^{-1}$. Thus, the monomial is scaled by:
$$(\lambda)^{n-k} (\lambda^{-1})^k = \lambda^{n-2k}.$$
The exponent $n-2k$ is called the \textit{weight}. As $k$ goes from $0$ to $n$, the weights are exactly:
$$n, n-2, n-4, \dots, -(n-2), -n.$$
Notice that:
1. Every weight space (the space spanned by $x^{n-k}y^k$) is 1-dimensional.
2. The weights form a single, unbroken "string" stepping by $-2$.

In Lie theory (specifically for the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$), the raising and lowering operators $E = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $F = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ map these weight spaces into each other without skipping any steps. Because you can reach any basis vector from any other by repeatedly applying $E$ or $F$, the space cannot be split into smaller invariant pieces. Therefore, $V_n$ is an irreducible representation.

\textbf{(b) The Clebsch-Gordan Formula applied to $V_2 \otimes V_3$}
The tensor product of two irreducible representations $V_m$ and $V_n$ decomposes into a direct sum of irreducible representations according to the \textbf{Clebsch-Gordan Formula}:
$$V_m \otimes V_n \cong V_{m+n} \oplus V_{m+n-2} \oplus \dots \oplus V_{|m-n|}.$$
This formula tells us that the highest weight in the product is $m+n$, and the weights drop by $2$ at each step, stopping at $|m-n|$.

For our specific case, we want to decompose $V_2 \otimes V_3$:
- Here, $m = 2$ and $n = 3$.
- The maximum subscript is $m+n = 2+3 = 5$.
- The minimum subscript is $|m-n| = |2-3| = 1$.
- We step down by $2$ from the maximum to the minimum: $5$, then $3$, then $1$.

Thus, the decomposition is:
$$V_2 \otimes V_3 \cong V_5 \oplus V_3 \oplus V_1.$$

\textbf{What does this mean physically/geometrically?}
In quantum mechanics, this exact math describes the addition of angular momentum (or spin). $V_n$ corresponds to a particle with spin $s = n/2$. 
$V_2$ represents spin $1$ (since $2/2 = 1$), and $V_3$ represents spin $3/2$ (since $3/2 = 1.5$). 
When you combine these two systems, the total spin can range from $1 + 3/2 = 5/2$ down to $|1 - 3/2| = 1/2$ in integer steps. 
Correspondingly, $V_5$ is spin $5/2$, $V_3$ is spin $3/2$, and $V_1$ is spin $1/2$.
\end{solution}
