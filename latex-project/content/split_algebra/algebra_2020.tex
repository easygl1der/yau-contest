\section{2020 Algebra \& Number Theory}

\subsection*{Exam}
\begin{exercise}
Let $F$ be a field of characteristic zero. Consider the polynomial ring $F[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$.

(a) Prove Newton's identity over the field $F$:
$$p_k - p_{k-1}e_1 + \cdots + (-1)^{k-1}p_1e_{k-1} + (-1)^k k e_k = 0,$$
where
$$e_k(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \sum_{1 \leq i_1 < \cdots < i_k \leq n} x_{i_1} \cdots x_{i_k}$$
for $1 \leq k \leq n$, $e_0 = 1$, $e_k = 0$ when $k > n$, and
$$p_k(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = x_1^k + \cdots + x_n^k.$$

(b) Prove that over the field $F$ of characteristic zero, an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is nilpotent if and only if the trace of $A^k$ is equal to zero for all $k = 1, 2, \ldots$.

Hint: use Part (a).

(c) Prove that over the field $F$ of characteristic zero, two $n \times n$ matrices $A$ and $B$ have the same characteristic polynomial if and only if the trace of $A^k$ and trace of $B^k$ are equal for all $k = 1, 2, \ldots$.

Hint: use Part (a).
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) Consider the generating function for elementary symmetric polynomials:
$$ E(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n e_k t^k = \prod_{i=1}^n (1 + x_i t). $$
The power sum symmetric polynomials $p_k$ appear in the logarithmic derivative:
$$ \frac{E'(t)}{E(t)} = \frac{d}{dt} \sum_{i=1}^n \ln(1 + x_i t) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{x_i}{1 + x_i t} = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^\infty x_i ( -x_i t)^{j-1} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^{k-1} p_k t^{k-1}. $$
Multiplying by $E(t)$, we get:
$$ \sum_{k=1}^n k e_k t^{k-1} = \left( \sum_{m=1}^\infty (-1)^{m-1} p_m t^{m-1} \right) \left( \sum_{j=0}^n e_j t^j \right). $$
Equating the coefficients of $t^{k-1}$ on both sides:
$$ k e_k = \sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1} p_j e_{k-j}. $$
Rearranging gives the desired identity: $p_k - e_1 p_{k-1} + \cdots + (-1)^{k-1} e_{k-1} p_1 + (-1)^k k e_k = 0$.

(b) A matrix $A$ is nilpotent if and only if all its eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ are zero. This is equivalent to saying that the characteristic polynomial $\chi_A(t) = \det(tI - A) = t^n$.
The coefficients of the characteristic polynomial are (up to sign) the elementary symmetric polynomials $e_k$ of the eigenvalues.
Newton's identities allow us to express $e_k$ in terms of $p_1, \dots, p_k$ and vice versa in characteristic zero.
If $\mathrm{Tr}(A^k) = p_k(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n) = 0$ for all $k=1, \dots, n$, then $e_k = 0$ for $k=1, \dots, n$.
Thus $\chi_A(t) = t^n$, so $A$ is nilpotent.

(c) Two matrices $A$ and $B$ have the same characteristic polynomial if and only if $e_k(A) = e_k(B)$ for $k=1, \dots, n$.
Again, by Newton's identities in characteristic zero, the $e_k$ are determined by $p_1, \dots, p_k$ (where $p_k = \mathrm{Tr}(A^k)$) and vice versa.
Thus, $e_k(A) = e_k(B)$ for all $k$ if and only if $p_k(A) = p_k(B)$ for all $k$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
(a) Let $M$ be a finitely generated $R$-module and $\mathfrak{a} \subset R$ an ideal. Suppose $\phi: M \to M$ is an $R$-module map such that $\phi(M) \subseteq \mathfrak{a}M$. Prove that there is a monic polynomial $p(t) \subset R[t]$ with coefficients from $\mathfrak{a}$ such that $p(\phi) = 0$.

Hint: $p(t)$ is basically just the characteristic polynomial.

(b) If $M$ is a finitely generated $R$-module such that $\mathfrak{a}M = M$ for some ideal $\mathfrak{a} \subset R$, then there exists $x \in R$ such that $1 - x \in \mathfrak{a}$ and $xM = 0$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) Let $\{m_1, \dots, m_n\}$ be a set of generators for $M$. Since $\phi(M) \subseteq \mathfrak{a}M$, we can write:
$$ \phi(m_i) = \sum_{j=1}^n a_{ij} m_j, \quad \text{where } a_{ij} \in \mathfrak{a}. $$
This can be rewritten as:
$$ \sum_{j=1}^n (\delta_{ij} \phi - a_{ij}) m_j = 0. $$
Let $A$ be the matrix $(\delta_{ij} \phi - a_{ij})$ with entries in the ring $R[\phi]$. Let $C$ be the adjugate matrix of $A$. Then $CA = \det(A) I$.
Multiplying the vector $(m_1, \dots, m_n)^T$ by $CA = 0$ gives $\det(A) m_i = 0$ for all $i$.
Since $\{m_i\}$ generate $M$, $\det(A) M = 0$.
Expanding the determinant $p(t) = \det(t I - (a_{ij}))$ gives a monic polynomial in $t$ with other coefficients in $\mathfrak{a}$. Thus $p(\phi) = 0$ is the required relation.

(b) Assume $\mathfrak{a}M = M$. Let $\phi = \mathrm{Id}_M$. Then $\phi(M) = M = \mathfrak{a}M$.
By part (a), there exists a monic polynomial $p(t) = t^n + a_{n-1} t^{n-1} + \dots + a_0$ with $a_i \in \mathfrak{a}$ such that $p(\mathrm{Id}_M) = 0$.
This means $(1 + a_{n-1} + \dots + a_0) \mathrm{Id}_M = 0$.
Let $x = -(a_{n-1} + \dots + a_0)$. Since $a_i \in \mathfrak{a}$, we have $x \in \mathfrak{a}$.
The equation becomes $(1 - x) M = 0$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $R = F[x, y]/(y^2 - x^2 - x^3)$ for some field $F$.

(a) Prove that $R$ is an integral domain.

(b) Compute the normalization of $R$ (i.e., the integral closure of $R$ in its field of fraction).
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) To show $R$ is an integral domain, we must show that $f(x, y) = y^2 - x^2 - x^3$ is irreducible in $F[x, y]$.
$f$ is a quadratic in $y$. It is irreducible if and only if $x^2 + x^3 = x^2(1+x)$ is not a square in $F[x]$.
In the polynomial ring $F[x]$, the degree of a square is even. $x^2(1+x)$ has degree 3, so it cannot be a square.
Thus $y^2 - x^2(1+x)$ is irreducible in $F[x][y]$, so $R$ is an integral domain.

(b) Let $K$ be the field of fractions of $R$. In $K$, we have $y^2 = x^2(1+x)$.
Let $t = y/x$. Then $t^2 = 1+x$, so $x = t^2 - 1$.
Then $y = x t = (t^2 - 1) t = t^3 - t$.
The field of fractions $K = F(x, y) = F(t^2-1, t^3-t) = F(t)$ (since $t = y/x$).
The element $t \in K$ is integral over $R$ because it satisfies $t^2 - (1+x) = 0$, and $1+x \in R$.
The ring $F[t]$ is a polynomial ring, which is integrally closed.
Since $R = F[t^2-1, t^3-t] \subseteq F[t]$ and $F[t]$ is the integral closure of $F$ in $F(t)$, the normalization of $R$ is $F[t] \cong F[y/x]$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $p$ and $\ell$ be two prime numbers and $[\ell_x] = 1 + x + \cdots + x^{\ell-1}$ denote the $\ell$-th cyclotomic polynomial.

(a) Prove that $[\ell_x]$ is an irreducible element of $\mathbb{Q}[x]$.

(b) Show that $[\ell_x]$ is divisible by $x - 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ if $p = \ell$.

(c) Suppose $p \neq \ell$. Let $a$ be the order of $p$ in $\mathbb{F}_\ell$. Show that $a$ is the first value of $m$ for which the group $\mathrm{GL}_m(\mathbb{F}_p)$ of invertible $m \times m$ matrices with entries from $\mathbb{F}_p$ contains an element of order $\ell$.

Hint: Derive and use the formula for the number of elements in $\mathrm{GL}_m(\mathbb{F}_p)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) To prove $\Phi_\ell(x) = \frac{x^\ell - 1}{x - 1} = x^{\ell-1} + \dots + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$, we consider $f(x) = \Phi_\ell(x+1) = \frac{(x+1)^\ell - 1}{x} = x^{\ell-1} + \binom{\ell}{\ell-1} x^{\ell-2} + \dots + \binom{\ell}{1}$.
Since $\ell$ is prime, all binomial coefficients $\binom{\ell}{k}$ for $1 \leq k \leq \ell-1$ are divisible by $\ell$, and the constant term $\binom{\ell}{1} = \ell$ is not divisible by $\ell^2$.
By Eisenstein's Criterion at the prime $\ell$, $\Phi_\ell(x+1)$ is irreducible, hence $\Phi_\ell(x)$ is irreducible.

(b) In $\mathbb{F}_\ell[x]$, we have $(x-1)^\ell = x^\ell - 1$ because the characteristic is $\ell$.
Then $\Phi_\ell(x) = \frac{x^\ell - 1}{x - 1} = \frac{(x-1)^\ell}{x-1} = (x-1)^{\ell-1}$.
Thus $[\ell_x]$ is divisible by $x-1$ in $\mathbb{F}_\ell[x]$. (Actually, $x=1$ is a root of multiplicity $\ell-1$).

(c) An element of order $\ell$ exists in $GL_m(\mathbb{F}_p)$ if and only if $\ell$ divides the order of the group:
$$ |GL_m(\mathbb{F}_p)| = \prod_{i=0}^{m-1} (p^m - p^i) = p^{m(m-1)/2} \prod_{i=1}^m (p^i - 1). $$
By Cauchy's Theorem, $GL_m(\mathbb{F}_p)$ has an element of order $\ell$ if and only if $\ell$ divides its order. Since $\ell \neq p$, this happens if and only if $\ell$ divides $p^i - 1$ for some $1 \leq i \leq m$.
The smallest such $i$ is the order of $p$ in the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z})^\times$, which is denoted by $a = \mathrm{ord}_\ell(p)$.
Therefore, the first value of $m$ for which $GL_m(\mathbb{F}_p)$ contains an element of order $\ell$ is $m = a$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $p \geq 3$ be a prime number and let $\mathbb{Z}_p$ be the ring of $p$-adic integers.

(a) Show that an element in $1 + p\mathbb{Z}_p$ is a $p$-th power in $\mathbb{Z}_p$ if and only if it lives in $1 + p^2\mathbb{Z}_p$.

(b) Let $\mathbb{Z}_p^\times$ denote the group of units in $\mathbb{Z}_p$. Show that there exist $a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}_p^\times$ such that $a^p + b^p = c^p$ if and only if
$$\sum_{i=1}^{p-1} i^{p-2} t^i \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$$
for some integer $t \in \{2, 3, \ldots, p-1\}$. (In particular, this condition holds for $p = 7$ by taking $t = 3$. Therefore, Fermat's Last Theorem does not hold for $\mathbb{Z}_7$.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) For $p \geq 3$, the $p$-adic logarithm $\log: 1 + p\mathbb{Z}_p \to p\mathbb{Z}_p$ is an isomorphism of groups.
An element $x \in 1 + p\mathbb{Z}_p$ is a $p$-th power if and only if $\log(x) \in p \cdot (p\mathbb{Z}_p) = p^2 \mathbb{Z}_p$.
Since $x \in 1 + p^k \mathbb{Z}_p \iff \log(x) \in p^k \mathbb{Z}_p$ for $k \geq 1$, we have $x \in (1+p\mathbb{Z}_p)^p \iff x \in 1 + p^2 \mathbb{Z}_p$.
Alternatively, using binomial expansion: $(1+pu)^p = 1 + p^2 u + \binom{p}{2} p^2 u^2 + \dots \equiv 1 + p^2 u \pmod{p^3}$ for $p \geq 3$. This shows that the image of the $p$-th power map on $1+p\mathbb{Z}_p$ is $1+p^2\mathbb{Z}_p$.

(b) If $a, b, c \in \mathbb{Z}_p^\times$, then $a^p + b^p = c^p$. Modulo $p^2$, we have $a^p \equiv a_0^p \pmod{p^2}$ where $a_0$ is the unique $(p-1)$-th root of unity (Teichmüller lift) such that $a \equiv a_0 \pmod p$.
The condition $a^p + b^p = c^p$ implies $a_0^p + b_0^p \equiv c_0^p \pmod{p^2}$.
Since $c_0 \equiv a_0 + b_0 \pmod p$, let $c_0 = a_0 + b_0 + k p$. Then $c_0^p \equiv (a_0+b_0)^p \pmod{p^2}$.
Thus we need $a_0^p + b_0^p \equiv (a_0+b_0)^p \pmod{p^2}$.
Dividing by $a_0^p$ and setting $t = b_0/a_0$, we get $1 + t^p \equiv (1+t)^p \pmod{p^2}$.
Expanding $(1+t)^p = 1 + t^p + \sum_{i=1}^{p-1} \binom{p}{i} t^i$, we need $\sum_{i=1}^{p-1} \binom{p}{i} t^i \equiv 0 \pmod{p^2}$.
Since $\frac{1}{p} \binom{p}{i} \equiv \frac{(-1)^{i-1}}{i} \pmod p$, the condition is $\sum_{i=1}^{p-1} \frac{(-1)^{i-1}}{i} t^i \equiv 0 \pmod p$.
Substituting $-t$ for $t$ and using $i^{p-2} \equiv i^{-1} \pmod p$, this is equivalent to the Mirimanoff polynomial condition given in the problem.
For $p=7, t=3$, $\sum_{i=1}^6 i^5 3^i \equiv \sum_{i=1}^6 \frac{3^i}{i} \equiv 3+1+2+1+1+6 = 14 \equiv 0 \pmod 7$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Recall that a metric space is called spherically complete if any decreasing sequence of closed balls has nonempty intersection.

Let $p$ be a prime number and let $\mathbb{Q}_p$ be the field of $p$-adic numbers. For every integer $n \geq 1$, consider the finite extension $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^n})$ of $\mathbb{Q}_p$ generated by all $p^n$-th roots of unity. Let $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty}) = \cup_{n \geq 1} \mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^n})$.

Question: Which of the following are spherically complete? Explain why.

(a) $\mathbb{Q}_p$.

(b) $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^n})$.

(c) $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty})$.

(d) $\overline{\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty})}$, the completion of $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
A non-archimedean field is spherically complete if and only if it is maximally complete. For fields with a discrete valuation, spherical completeness is equivalent to completeness.

(a) $\mathbb{Q}_p$ is spherically complete. It is a complete field with a discrete valuation (and is locally compact).

(b) $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^n})$ is spherically complete. It is a finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$, thus it is also a complete field with a discrete valuation.

(c) $\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty})$ is NOT spherically complete. It is not even complete as a metric space (the union of an infinite chain of proper closed subfields is not complete). A spherically complete space must be complete.

(d) $\overline{\mathbb{Q}_p(\mu_{p^\infty})}$ is NOT spherically complete. While it is complete, its value group is $\{ p^r \mid r \in \mathbb{Q}, \text{ denom is power of } p \}$, which is not discrete. It is known that the completion of an infinite algebraic extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$ (like $\mathbb{C}_p$ or this field) is not spherically complete unless the extension is finite. Specifically, one can construct a sequence of balls whose radii tend to a non-zero limit but have an empty intersection.
\end{solution}
