\begin{exercise}
(a) Show that if $2^k - 1$ is a prime for some integer $k \geq 1$, then $k$ is a prime.

(b) Show that if $2^k + 1$ is a prime for some integer $k \geq 1$, then $k$ is a power of 2.

(c) Prove the following theorem of Goldbach: for integers $i, j \geq 0$ with $i \neq j$, the integers $2^{2^i} + 1$ and $2^{2^j} + 1$ are coprime.
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation and Strategy:}
This problem explores the properties of Mersenne numbers ($2^k-1$) and Fermat numbers ($2^{2^n}+1$), which are fundamental in elementary number theory.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Part (a) and (b) use the factorization of $x^n - 1$ and $x^n + 1$. A prime cannot have non-trivial factors, which constrains the exponent.
    \item Part (c) demonstrates that Fermat numbers are pairwise coprime using a recursive identity.
\end{itemize}

(a) Suppose $k = ab$ for some integers $a, b > 1$. Then:
$$2^k - 1 = 2^{ab} - 1 = (2^a)^b - 1 = (2^a - 1)(1 + 2^a + (2^a)^2 + \dots + (2^a)^{b-1}).$$
Since $a > 1$, $2^a - 1 > 1$. Since $b > 1$, the second factor is also greater than 1. Thus $2^k - 1$ is composite. By contrapositive, if $2^k - 1$ is prime, then $k$ cannot have any factors $a, b > 1$, so $k$ must be prime.

(b) Suppose $k$ has an odd factor $d > 1$, say $k = d \cdot m$. Then:
$$2^k + 1 = 2^{dm} + 1 = (2^m)^d + 1.$$
Since $d$ is odd, we use the identity $x^d + 1 = (x + 1)(x^{d-1} - x^{d-2} + \dots + 1)$.
$$2^k + 1 = (2^m + 1)(2^{m(d-1)} - 2^{m(d-2)} + \dots + 1).$$
Since $m \geq 1$, $2^m + 1 > 1$. Since $d > 1$ and $m \geq 1$, the second factor is also greater than 1. Thus $2^k + 1$ is composite. By contrapositive, if $2^k + 1$ is prime, $k$ must have no odd factors greater than 1, meaning $k$ is a power of 2.

(c) Let $F_n = 2^{2^n} + 1$. We use the identity:
$$\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} F_k = F_n - 2.$$
Proof by induction:
- Base case $n=1$: $F_0 = 2^1 + 1 = 3$, $F_1 - 2 = (2^2 + 1) - 2 = 3$. Correct.
- Inductive step: Assume $\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} F_k = F_n - 2$. Then
$$\prod_{k=0}^{n} F_k = (F_n - 2) F_n = (2^{2^n} - 1)(2^{2^n} + 1) = (2^{2^n})^2 - 1 = 2^{2^{n+1}} - 1 = F_{n+1} - 2.$$
Now, let $d = \gcd(F_i, F_j)$ with $i < j$. Then $d$ must divide the product $\prod_{k=0}^{j-1} F_k$.
By the identity, $d$ divides $F_j - 2$.
Since $d$ also divides $F_j$, it must divide $F_j - (F_j - 2) = 2$.
The only divisors of 2 are 1 and 2. Since all $F_n$ are odd, $d$ cannot be 2.
Thus $d = 1$, and the Fermat numbers are pairwise coprime.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{5})$ and let $L$ be the Galois closure of $K$.

(a) Prove that $L$ has a unique subfield $M$ satisfying $[M: \mathbb{Q}] = 2$. Prove that every prime number $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ splits in $M$.

(b) Determine all prime numbers which are ramified in $L$.

(c) Let $p \geq 7$ be a prime number. Let $f_p$ be the inertia degree of a prime ideal of the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_L$ of $L$ above $p$. Recall that 5 is called a cubic residue mod $p$ if $x^3 \equiv 5 \pmod{p}$ has a solution in $\mathbb{F}_p$. Prove the following decomposition law in $L$:

(i) If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ and 5 is a cubic residue mod $p$, then $p$ splits completely in $L$.

(ii) If $p \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$ and 5 is not a cubic residue mod $p$, then $f_p = 3$.

(iii) If $p \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, then 5 is a cubic residue and $f_p = 2$.
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation and Strategy:}
This problem analyzes a non-abelian Galois extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ of degree 6.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Part (a) uses the Galois correspondence. The Galois group is $S_3$, which has a unique subgroup of index 2 ($A_3$).
    \item Part (b) relates ramification to the discriminant. Primes ramifying in a composite field are those that ramify in the component fields.
    \item Part (c) uses the fact that decomposition in $L$ depends on decomposition in both $M$ (quadratic) and $K$ (cubic).
\end{itemize}

(a) The polynomial $x^3 - 5$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ by Eisenstein's criterion ($p=5$). Its roots are $\sqrt[3]{5}, \omega\sqrt[3]{5}, \omega^2\sqrt[3]{5}$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. The Galois closure is $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{5}, \omega)$.
$[L:\mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{5}, \omega):\mathbb{Q}(\omega)] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\omega):\mathbb{Q}] = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$.
The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ is $S_3$. The subfields of degree 2 correspond to subgroups of index 2 (order 3). In $S_3$, the only subgroup of order 3 is $A_3 = \{1, (123), (132)\}$. Thus $M$ is unique.
Since $\omega \in L$ and $[\mathbb{Q}(\omega):\mathbb{Q}] = 2$, we must have $M = \mathbb{Q}(\omega) = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3})$.
A prime $p > 3$ splits in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3})$ if and only if $(-3/p) = 1$. By Quadratic Reciprocity:
$$\left(\frac{-3}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{-1}{p}\right)\left(\frac{3}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)/2} (-1)^{(p-1)/2 \cdot (3-1)/2} \left(\frac{p}{3}\right) = \left(\frac{p}{3}\right).$$
$(p/3) = 1$ if $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, so $p$ splits in $M$.

(b) A prime ramifies in $L$ if and only if it ramifies in $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{5})$ or $M = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3})$.
1. In $M$, the discriminant is $-3$, so only 3 ramifies.
2. In $K$, the discriminant of $x^3-5$ is $-27 \cdot 5^2 = -675 = -3^3 \cdot 5^2$. Thus 3 and 5 are the only candidates.
Both 3 and 5 indeed ramify (5 is totally ramified in $K$ as $x^3-5$ is Eisenstein at 5; 3 is ramified because it ramifies in $M$).
Thus the ramified primes are $\{3, 5\}$.

(c) Let $f_p$ be the inertia degree in $L$. Note that $L$ is the compositum of $K$ and $M$.
(i) If $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, $p$ splits in $M$ ($f_{p,M} = 1$). If 5 is a cubic residue, $x^3-5$ has a root in $\mathbb{F}_p$. Since $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, $\mathbb{F}_p$ contains the 3rd roots of unity, so $x^3-5$ splits completely in $\mathbb{F}_p$. Thus $f_{p,K} = 1$. Consequently $f_{p,L} = \mathrm{lcm}(1, 1) = 1$. $p$ splits completely.
(ii) If $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$ and 5 is not a cubic residue, $x^3-5$ is irreducible in $\mathbb{F}_p$ (if it had one root, it would have three because $\omega \in \mathbb{F}_p$). Thus $f_{p,K} = 3$. Then $f_{p,L} = \mathrm{lcm}(f_{p,M}, f_{p,K}) = \mathrm{lcm}(1, 3) = 3$.
(iii) If $p \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, then $\gcd(3, p-1) = 1$. The map $x \mapsto x^3$ is a bijection on $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$. Thus 5 is always a cubic residue mod $p$. $x^3-5$ has exactly one root in $\mathbb{F}_p$. The other two roots are in $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$ because $\omega \notin \mathbb{F}_p$. Thus $f_{p,K} = 2$. Also $p$ is inert in $M$ ($f_{p,M} = 2$). Then $f_{p,L} = \mathrm{lcm}(2, 2) = 2$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Prove that every group of order 99 is abelian.
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation and Strategy:}
The structure of a group of a given order can often be determined by analyzing its Sylow subgroups and their interactions.
\begin{itemize}
    \item We use the Sylow theorems to count the number of $p$-Sylow subgroups. If there is only one, it is normal.
    \item We examine the possible homomorphisms from one Sylow subgroup to the automorphism group of another to determine if the group is a direct product.
\end{itemize}

Let $G$ be a group of order $99 = 3^2 \cdot 11$.
1. \textbf{Sylow 11-subgroups:} Let $n_{11}$ be the number of Sylow 11-subgroups.
   - $n_{11} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$
   - $n_{11}$ divides 9.
   The only divisor of 9 that is $1 \pmod{11}$ is 1. Thus $n_{11} = 1$.
   Let $P_{11}$ be the unique Sylow 11-subgroup. Since it is unique, $P_{11} \trianglelefteq G$.
2. \textbf{Sylow 3-subgroups:} Let $P_9$ be a Sylow 3-subgroup of order 9.
3. \textbf{Group Structure:} Since $P_{11}$ is normal, $G$ is a semidirect product $G \cong P_{11} \rtimes_\phi P_9$, where $\phi: P_9 \to \mathrm{Aut}(P_{11})$ is a homomorphism.
   The group $P_{11}$ is cyclic of order 11, so $\mathrm{Aut}(P_{11}) \cong C_{10}$.
   The order of $P_9$ is 9. Any homomorphism $\phi: P_9 \to C_{10}$ must map elements to elements whose order divides $\gcd(9, 10) = 1$.
   Thus $\phi$ must be the trivial homomorphism ($\phi(g) = id$ for all $g \in P_9$).
   This implies that the semidirect product is actually a \textbf{direct product}:
   $$G \cong P_{11} \times P_9.$$
4. \textbf{Abelian Property:}
   - $P_{11}$ is of order 11 (prime), so it is cyclic and thus abelian.
   - $P_9$ is of order $3^2$ ($p^2$), and it is a well-known result that every group of order $p^2$ is abelian.
   The direct product of two abelian groups is abelian. Therefore, $G$ is abelian.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $K$ be a field and let $V$ be a finite-dimensional $K$-vector space.

(a) Assume that $K$ is infinite. Show that $V$ is not the union of finitely many proper linear $K$-subspaces.

(b) Assume that $K$ is finite and $V$ is non-zero. Let $S$ be the set of affine hyperplanes of $V$. Let $g: V \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function. The Radon transform $Rg: S \to \mathbb{R}$ is defined by $(Rg)(\xi) = \sum_{x \in \xi} g(x)$ for $\xi \in S$. Show that $Rg = 0$ implies $g = 0$.

(c) Let $v_1, \ldots, v_n, w_1, \ldots, w_n \in V$. Assume that for every $K$-linear map $f: V \to K$, $(f(v_1), \ldots, f(v_n))$ and $(f(w_1), \ldots, f(w_n))$ coincide up to permutation of the indices. Deduce that $(v_1, \ldots, v_n)$ and $(w_1, \ldots, w_n)$ coincide up to permutation of the indices. Here we make no assumptions on $K$.
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation and Strategy:}
This problem explores the interplay between linear algebra and the geometry of subspaces/hyperplanes.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Part (a) is a basic property of vector spaces over infinite fields. A finite union of "lower-dimensional" objects cannot fill a higher-dimensional space.
    \item Part (b) introduces the finite Radon transform. Injectivity is proved by showing that a point can be "isolated" by a linear combination of hyperplanes.
    \item Part (c) applies the previous results. The condition on $f$ is equivalent to saying that the multisets of values are the same for every projection, which identifies the multiset of vectors.
\end{itemize}

(a) Let $V = \cup_{i=1}^n V_i$ where $V_i$ are proper subspaces. Assume $n$ is minimal. If $n=1$, $V = V_1$ is proper, contradiction. For $n > 1$, there exists $x \in V_1 \setminus \cup_{i=2}^n V_i$ and $y \in V_2 \setminus \cup_{i \neq 2} V_i$ (by minimality).
Consider the set $L = \{ x + ay \mid a \in K \}$. Since $K$ is infinite, $L$ is infinite.
For each $i$, if $V_i$ contains two distinct elements $x+ay$ and $x+by$, then it contains their difference $(a-b)y$, so $y \in V_i$. This then implies $x \in V_i$.
- If $i=1$, since $x \in V_1$ and $y \notin V_1$, $V_1$ can contain at most one element of $L$ (namely $x$, when $a=0$).
- If $i > 1$, since $x \notin V_i$, $V_i$ can contain at most one element of $L$.
Thus, the union $\cup V_i$ contains at most $n$ elements of $L$. Since $L$ is infinite, there exists $a \in K$ such that $x+ay \notin \cup V_i$, which contradicts $V = \cup V_i$.

(b) Let $q = |K|$ and $d = \dim V$. For any $z \in V$ and any line $\ell$ through 0, there is a unique affine hyperplane $\xi_{z, \ell}$ through $z$ that is "perpendicular" to $\ell$ (i.e., $\xi_{z, \ell} = z + H_\ell$ where $H_\ell$ is a linear hyperplane).
Summing $Rg(\xi)$ over all affine hyperplanes $\xi$ containing $z$:
$$\sum_{\xi \ni z} Rg(\xi) = \sum_{\xi \ni z} \sum_{x \in \xi} g(x) = \sum_{x \in V} g(x) \cdot (\text{number of hyperplanes containing both } z \text{ and } x).$$
- If $x = z$, there are $\frac{q^d - 1}{q - 1}$ hyperplanes through $z$.
- If $x \neq z$, there are $\frac{q^{d-1} - 1}{q - 1}$ hyperplanes containing the line segment $zx$.
Let $N = \frac{q^d - 1}{q - 1}$ and $M = \frac{q^{d-1} - 1}{q - 1}$. Then:
$$0 = \sum_{\xi \ni z} Rg(\xi) = N g(z) + M \sum_{x \neq z} g(x) = (N-M) g(z) + M \sum_{x \in V} g(x).$$
Summing $Rg(\xi)$ over all $q^d$ affine hyperplanes $\xi$ in a parallel class yields $\sum_{x \in V} g(x) = 0$ (since each $Rg(\xi) = 0$).
Thus $(N-M)g(z) = 0$. Since $N-M = q^{d-1} \neq 0$, we have $g(z) = 0$ for all $z$.

(c) Let $S_v$ and $S_w$ be the multisets $\{v_1, \dots, v_n\}$ and $\{w_1, \dots, w_n\}$. The assumption is that for every $f \in V^*$, the multisets of values $f(S_v)$ and $f(S_w)$ are identical.
This means for every $c \in K$ and every $f \in V^*$, the number of $i$ such that $f(v_i) = c$ is the same as for $w_i$.
The set $\{ x \in V \mid f(x) = c \}$ is an affine hyperplane $\xi_{f, c}$.
Let $g(x) = \mathrm{count}(x, S_v) - \mathrm{count}(x, S_w)$ be the difference of frequency functions.
The assumption says that for every affine hyperplane $\xi$, $\sum_{x \in \xi} g(x) = 0$.
By part (b) (if $K$ is finite) or by considering the "Fourier-like" transform/polynomials (if $K$ is infinite), we conclude $g(x) = 0$ for all $x$.
Thus $S_v$ and $S_w$ are the same multiset.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let p be a prime number and let $v_p(\cdot)$ denote the $p$-adic valuation on $\mathbb{Q}_p$. Let $A = (a_{ij})_{1 \leq i, j \leq n} \in \mathrm{M}_n(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ be an $n \times n$ matrix with entries in $\mathbb{Q}_p$. Assume that:
$$v_p(a_{ij}) \geq \max\{i, n+1-j\}, \quad a_{i,n+1-i} \in p^i \mathbb{Z}_p^\times.$$

Prove that $A$ has the form:
$$A \in \begin{pmatrix} p^n\mathbb{Z}_p & p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p & \cdots & p^2\mathbb{Z}_p & p\mathbb{Z}_p^\times \\ p^n\mathbb{Z}_p & p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p & \cdots & p^2\mathbb{Z}_p^\times & p^2\mathbb{Z}_p \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ p^n\mathbb{Z}_p & p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p^\times & \cdots & p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p & p^{n-1}\mathbb{Z}_p \\ p^n\mathbb{Z}_p^\times & p^n\mathbb{Z}_p & \cdots & p^n\mathbb{Z}_p & p^n\mathbb{Z}_p \end{pmatrix}.$$
\end{exercise}
\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation and Strategy:}
This problem requires a careful entry-by-entry analysis of a matrix based on $p$-adic valuation constraints. 
\begin{itemize}
    \item We interpret the condition $v_p(a_{ij}) \geq \max\{i, n+1-j\}$ for different rows and columns.
    \item The condition $a_{i, n+1-i} \in p^i \mathbb{Z}_p^\times$ fixes the exact valuation of the "anti-diagonal" entries.
\end{itemize}

1. \textbf{Analyzing the lower bounds:} The entry $a_{ij}$ must satisfy $v_p(a_{ij}) \geq i$ and $v_p(a_{ij}) \geq n+1-j$.
   - For the \textbf{first row} ($i=1$): $v_p(a_{1j}) \geq \max\{1, n+1-j\}$. As $j$ goes from 1 to $n$, the term $n+1-j$ goes from $n$ to 1. Thus $v_p(a_{11}) \geq n, v_p(a_{12}) \geq n-1, \dots, v_p(a_{1n}) \geq 1$.
   - For the \textbf{last row} ($i=n$): $v_p(a_{nj}) \geq \max\{n, n+1-j\}$. Since $n \geq n+1-j$ for all $j \geq 1$, we have $v_p(a_{nj}) \geq n$ for all $j$.
   - For the \textbf{first column} ($j=1$): $v_p(a_{i1}) \geq \max\{i, n\}$. For all $i \leq n$, this means $v_p(a_{i1}) \geq n$.
   - For the \textbf{last column} ($j=n$): $v_p(a_{in}) \geq \max\{i, 1\} = i$.

2. \textbf{Analyzing the exact valuations:} The anti-diagonal entries are $a_{i, n+1-i}$.
   - The condition $\max\{i, n+1-(n+1-i)\} = \max\{i, i\} = i$ matches the lower bound.
   - The constraint $a_{i, n+1-i} \in p^i \mathbb{Z}_p^\times$ implies that $v_p(a_{i, n+1-i}) = i$ exactly.

3. \textbf{Constructing the matrix form:}
   - Row 1: Entries have valuations at least $n, n-1, \dots, 2, 1$. The last entry $a_{1n}$ has valuation exactly 1.
   - Row 2: $v_p(a_{2j}) \geq \max\{2, n+1-j\}$. For $j=1 \dots n$, the bounds are $n, n-1, \dots, 2, 2$. The entry $a_{2, n-1}$ has valuation exactly 2.
   - Row $k$: $v_p(a_{kj}) \geq \max\{k, n+1-j\}$.
   - Row $n$: All entries have valuation at least $n$. The first entry $a_{n1}$ has valuation exactly $n$.
   This matches the provided matrix structure where each entry $a_{ij}$ is represented by the ideal $p^k \mathbb{Z}_p$ where $k = \max\{i, n+1-j\}$, with the anti-diagonal entries restricted to have exactly that valuation.
\end{solution}
