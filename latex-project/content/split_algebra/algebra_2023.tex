\section{2023 Algebra \& Number Theory}

\subsection*{Exam}
\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:1}
Let $F$ be a field. Let $n \geq 2$ be an integer. For $1 \leq i \neq j \leq n$ and for $\lambda \in F$, set $E_{ij}(\lambda)$ as the elementary matrix.

(1) Show that $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$ can be generated by the matrices $E_{ij}(\lambda)$.

(2) Assume $n \geq 3$. Show that $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ is not solvable.

(3) Assume $n = 2$ and $|F| \geq 4$. Show that $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ is not solvable either.

(4) Are $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_2)$ and $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_3)$ solvable?
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} elementary matrices, Gaussian elimination (row reduction), group solvability, commutator subgroup $[G, G]$, and derived series. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) Every matrix $A \in \mathrm{SL}_n(F)$ can be reduced to the identity matrix $I_n$ using elementary row operations. Specifically, for $A \in \mathrm{SL}_n(F)$, we only need the operation $R_i \to R_i + \lambda R_j$ (Type III). Left multiplication by $E_{ij}(\lambda)$ corresponds to this operation. Since $\det(A) = 1$, the row reduction process can be completed using only these shearing operations without needing to scale rows (Type II), which would change the determinant. Thus, $A$ is a product of elementary matrices $E_{ij}(\lambda)$. \\[2ex]

(2) For $n \geq 3$, we consider the commutator relation: $[E_{ij}(\lambda), E_{jk}(\mu)] = E_{ij}(\lambda)E_{jk}(\mu)E_{ij}(-\lambda)E_{jk}(-\mu) = E_{ik}(\lambda\mu)$ for distinct indices $i, j, k$. This formula shows that every generator $E_{ik}(\lambda)$ of $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$ is a commutator of two other elements in $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$. Therefore, the commutator subgroup $[\mathrm{SL}_n(F), \mathrm{SL}_n(F)]$ is the entire group $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$. A group that equals its own derived subgroup (and is non-trivial) cannot be solvable. Since $\mathrm{SL}_n(F) \subseteq \mathrm{GL}_n(F)$, the lack of solvability for $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$ implies $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ is not solvable. \\[2ex]

(3) For $n=2$, we use the commutator of an elementary matrix $E_{12}(\lambda)$ and a diagonal matrix $D = \text{diag}(a, a^{-1})$: 
$[E_{12}(\lambda), D] = E_{12}(\lambda(1-a^2))$. If $|F| \geq 4$, we can find a non-zero element $a \in F$ such that $a^2 \neq 1$ (since the equation $x^2-1=0$ has at most 2 roots). This allows us to express any $E_{12}(\mu)$ as a commutator. By symmetry, $E_{21}(\mu)$ is also a commutator. Thus $\mathrm{SL}_2(F) = [\mathrm{SL}_2(F), \mathrm{SL}_2(F)]$, making it non-solvable. \\[2ex]

(4) $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_2)$ is isomorphic to the symmetric group $S_3$. $S_3$ is solvable because it has the normal series $S_3 \rhd A_3 \rhd \{1\}$, where the quotients are $C_2$ and $C_3$. \\[2ex]
$\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_3)$ has order 48. Its derived series consists of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_3)$ (order 24), then the quaternion group $Q_8$ (order 8), then $V_4$ (order 4), then $C_2$, then $\{1\}$. Since this series reaches the identity, it is solvable. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} Solvability essentially means a group can be "broken down" into simpler abelian pieces. $\mathrm{SL}_n(F)$ for $n \geq 3$ or $|F| \geq 4$ is "too complex" because its elements are constantly regenerating themselves through commutators, preventing the group from ever simplifying to the identity.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:2}
Let $R$ be a ring with identity $1 \neq 0$.

(1) Assume that $R$ is commutative. Show that, if we have an isomorphism $R^n \simeq R^m$ of $R$-modules for some positive integers $n$ and $m$, then $m = n$.

(2) The analogous assertion of (1) for a non-commutative ring is not true in general as shown by the following example. Let $K$ be a non-trivial ring with identity, and $F$ be a free $K$-module with a countable basis indexed by $\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$:
$$e_1, \ldots, e_n, \ldots$$
Write $R = \mathrm{End}_K(F)$, which is a ring with the usual addition and composition of two $K$-linear endomorphisms of $F$.

(a) Show that $R$ is not commutative.

(b) Let $f_0 \in R$ (resp. $f_1 \in R$) such that $f_0(e_{2i}) = e_i$ and $f_0(e_{2i-1}) = 0$ (resp. $f_1(e_{2i}) = 0$ and $f_1(e_{2i-1}) = e_i$) for every $i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$. Show that $\{f_0, f_1\}$ is a basis of $R$ as a left $R$-module.

(c) For any integers $n, m \geq 1$, show that there exists an isomorphism $R^n \stackrel{\sim}{\to} R^m$ of $R$-modules.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} $R$-modules, maximal ideals, vector space dimension, endomorphism rings, and module bases. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) To compare $n$ and $m$, we reduce the problem to linear algebra over a field. Let $\mathfrak{m}$ be a maximal ideal of $R$. Then $k = R/\mathfrak{m}$ is a field. The isomorphism $R^n \cong R^m$ induces an isomorphism of vector spaces $(R/\mathfrak{m})^n \cong (R/\mathfrak{m})^m$ by tensoring with $k$. Since the dimension of a vector space over a field is a well-defined invariant, we conclude $n = m$. \\[2ex]

(2a) $R = \mathrm{End}_K(F)$ is not commutative because linear operators on an infinite-dimensional space typically do not commute. For example, consider a shift operator $S(e_i) = e_{i+1}$ and its adjoint. Their composition depends on the order. \\[2ex]

(2b) We need to show that every $h \in R$ can be uniquely written as $h = r_0 f_0 + r_1 f_1$ for $r_0, r_1 \in R$. Define $g_0, g_1 \in R$ by $g_0(e_i) = e_{2i}$ and $g_1(e_i) = e_{2i-1}$. Observe that $f_0 g_0 = \text{id}_F$, $f_1 g_1 = \text{id}_F$, and $f_0 g_1 = f_1 g_0 = 0$. Also, $g_0 f_0 + g_1 f_1 = \text{id}_F$ because for any $e_j$, if $j$ is even ($j=2i$), $g_0 f_0(e_{2i}) = g_0(e_i) = e_{2i}$ and $g_1 f_1(e_{2i}) = 0$; if $j$ is odd, the reverse holds. Thus $\{f_0, f_1\}$ generates $R$ as a left module, and the relations $f_i g_j = \delta_{ij}$ ensure linear independence. \\[2ex]

(2c) The fact that $R$ has a basis of 2 elements means $R \cong R^2$ as a left $R$-module. By applying this isomorphism repeatedly, $R \cong R^2 \cong R^3 \cong \dots \cong R^n$. Therefore, $R^n \cong R^m$ for any positive integers $n$ and $m$. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} In commutative rings, "size" (rank) is preserved because we can always project down to a field. In non-commutative rings, an object can be "isomorphic to two of itself" (like Hilbert's Grand Hotel), allowing different "ranks" to be equivalent.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:3}
Let $G$ be a profinite group. We say that it is topologically finitely generated if there exist finitely many elements $g_1, \ldots, g_n$ such that the closed subgroup generated by $g_1, \ldots, g_n$ is equal to $G$.

(1) Assume that $G$ is topologically finitely generated. Show that, for a fixed positive integer $n$, $G$ has only finitely many open subgroups of index $n$.

(2) Show that, for $K$ a number field (i.e., a finite field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$), its absolute Galois group $G_K$ is not topologically finitely generated.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} Profinite groups, open subgroups, group actions on cosets, homomorphisms to $S_n$, and Galois theory of quadratic extensions. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) An open subgroup $H$ of index $n$ is the stabilizer of a point in a continuous transitive action of $G$ on a set of $n$ elements. Such an action corresponds to a continuous homomorphism $\phi: G \to S_n$ such that $\phi(G)$ acts transitively. Since $G$ is topologically generated by $S = \{g_1, \dots, g_k\}$, the homomorphism $\phi$ is uniquely determined by the images of these generators $\phi(g_1), \dots, \phi(g_k) \in S_n$. There are only $(n!)^k$ possible sets of images. Thus, there are only finitely many such homomorphisms, and consequently, finitely many open subgroups of index $n$. \\[2ex]

(2) Consider $K = \mathbb{Q}$. Open subgroups of index 2 in $G_{\mathbb{Q}}$ correspond to quadratic extensions of $\mathbb{Q}$. Every such extension is of the form $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{d})$ for some square-free integer $d$. There are infinitely many square-free integers (e.g., all primes), so there are infinitely many such extensions. This implies $G_{\mathbb{Q}}$ has infinitely many open subgroups of index 2, which contradicts the property shown in part (1). Thus $G_{\mathbb{Q}}$ (and similarly $G_K$ for any number field) is not topologically finitely generated. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} If a group is "small" (finitely generated), it can only "branch out" into a finite number of directions for a given index. The absolute Galois group of a number field is "vast" because there are infinitely many independent arithmetic directions (primes) to extend into.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:4}
Let $R$ be a commutative ring with identity. An $R$-module $M$ is said to be of finite presentation if there exists an exact sequence of $R$-modules
$$R^n \longrightarrow R^m \longrightarrow M \longrightarrow 0$$
for some positive integers $m, n \geq 0$.

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(1)] Let $f: N \to M$ be a surjective morphism of $R$-modules with $N$ of finite type and $M$ of finite presentation. Show that the $R$-module $\ker(f)$ is of finite type.

\item[(2)] Let $M$ be an $R$-module of finite type. Show that the following statements are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] $M$ is flat and of finite presentation.
\item[(b)] $M$ is locally free, i.e., there exist $f_1, \dots, f_s \in R$ generating the unit ideal of $R$ such that $M_{f_i}$ is free over $R_{f_i}$.
\item[(c)] $M$ is projective as an $R$-module.
\end{enumerate}

\item[(3)] Let $S = \prod_{\mathbb{N}} R$ the product of countably many copies of $R$, and $I = \bigoplus_{\mathbb{N}} R \subset S$. Show that $S/I$ is a flat $S$-module which is not projective.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} exact sequences, finite presentation vs finite type, Schanuel's Lemma, flat/projective/locally free modules, and direct products vs direct sums. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) Since $M$ is finitely presented, it has a relation sequence $R^n \to R^m \to M \to 0$. Since $N$ is of finite type, there is a sequence $R^s \to N \to 0$. By comparing these two sequences (often using Schanuel's Lemma), we find that $\ker(f) \oplus R^m \cong \ker(R^m \to M) \oplus N$. Since the right side is a sum of finite type modules, $\ker(f)$ must also be of finite type. \\[2ex]

(2) This is a standard result in commutative algebra. (a) $\implies$ (b): Flatness plus finite presentation implies that the rank is locally constant, making it locally free. (b) $\implies$ (c): A locally free module of finite rank over a commutative ring is always projective. (c) $\implies$ (a): Projective modules are always flat, and being of finite type plus projective implies finite presentation. \\[2ex]

(3) $S/I$ is flat because it can be seen as a direct limit of flat modules (especially if $R$ is a field), or by checking that for any ideal $J \subset S$, $(S/I) \otimes J \to S/I$ is injective. However, it is not projective: if $S/I$ were projective, the sequence $0 \to I \to S \to S/I \to 0$ would split. This would mean $I$ is a direct summand of $S$. But in a product like $S$, the only direct summands are "chunks" of coordinates. $I$ (the sum) doesn't contain the "tail" of any sequence, so it cannot be a summand. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} Finite presentation means not only are there finitely many generators, but also finitely many "rules" (relations) they follow. Flatness is a geometric property (no "crunching" of dimensions), and for "well-behaved" (finitely presented) modules, this is the same as being locally free (looking like a vector bundle).
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:5}
Let $p$ be a prime number. Let $\mathbb{Z}_p$ be the ring of $p$-adic integers.

(1) Let $a \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $\lim_{r\to\infty} a^{p^r} = 1$. Show that $a$ is a $p^m$-th root of unity for some $m$.

(2) Let $A \in \mathrm{M}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{C})$ be an $n \times n$ complex matrix such that $\lim_{r\to\infty} A^{p^r} = I_n$, with $I_n$ the identity matrix. Show that $A^{p^m} = I_n$ for some integer $m$.

(3) Determine, up to isomorphisms, all the finite-dimensional continuous complex representations of $\mathbb{Z}_p$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} complex limits, roots of unity, Jordan normal form, profinite topology, and continuous homomorphisms. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) Let $a = re^{i\theta}$. The condition $a^{p^r} \to 1$ implies $r^{p^r} \to 1$, which forces $r=1$ (since $r > 0$). For the angle, $p^r \theta \pmod{2\pi} \to 0$. This means $p^r \theta = 2\pi k_r + \epsilon_r$ where $\epsilon_r \to 0$. For this to hold for all large $r$, $\epsilon_r$ must eventually be zero, meaning $\theta = 2\pi k / p^m$ for some integers $k, m$. \\[2ex]

(2) Let $J$ be the Jordan normal form of $A$. The eigenvalues of $A$ must satisfy the condition in part (1), so they are $p$-power roots of unity. For the nilpotent part, a Jordan block $J = \lambda I + N$ raised to power $p^r$ is $(\lambda I + N)^{p^r} = \lambda^{p^r} I + p^r \lambda^{p^r-1} N + \dots$. For this to converge to $I$, the term $p^r N$ must vanish as $r \to \infty$, which only happens if $N=0$. Thus $A$ is diagonalizable, and since its eigenvalues are $p^m$-th roots of unity, $A^{p^M} = I$ for some large $M$. \\[2ex]

(3) A continuous representation $\rho: \mathbb{Z}_p \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ must map the compact group $\mathbb{Z}_p$ to a compact subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$. Since $\mathbb{Z}_p$ has no small subgroups, the kernel of $\rho$ must be an open subgroup $p^k \mathbb{Z}_p$. Thus $\rho$ factors through the finite quotient $\mathbb{Z}_p / p^k \mathbb{Z}_p \cong \mathbb{Z}/p^k \mathbb{Z}$. These are direct sums of characters of the form $x \mapsto \exp(2\pi i m x / p^k)$. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} The $p$-adic world is "discrete" in a sense. Sequences that converge to 1 in this context usually have to "snap" to the value 1 rather than approaching it along a smooth path, leading to roots of unity.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{algebra:2023:6}
Let $p$ be a prime number. Let $K$ be a $p$-adic local field, i.e., a finite field extension of $\mathbb{Q}_p$. Denote by $\mathcal{O}_K$ its ring of integers, with $\pi \in \mathcal{O}_K$ a uniformizer.

(1) Show that the following logarithm map
$$\log: 1 + p\mathcal{O}_K \longrightarrow p\mathcal{O}_K, \quad 1 + x \mapsto x - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} + \cdots$$
is a well-defined isomorphism of groups, which can be extended in a unique way to a group homomorphism
$$\log_\pi: K^\times \longrightarrow K,$$
such that $\log_\pi(\pi) = 0$. Determine the kernel of $\log_\pi$.

(2) With the help of the logarithm above, show that for any integer $n \geq 1$, the group quotient $K^\times / K^{\times, n}$ is a finite group.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} $p$-adic valuation, convergence of power series, $p$-adic log and exp, and structure of $K^\times$. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Steps:} \\[2ex]
(1) The series $\log(1+x) = \sum (-1)^{n-1} x^n/n$ converges if $v_p(x) > 0$. It is a group homomorphism because $\log((1+x)(1+y)) = \log(1+x) + \log(1+y)$ as formal power series. For $v_p(x)$ large enough, the inverse $\exp(x)$ also converges, making it an isomorphism. To extend it to $\log_\pi: K^\times \to K$, we write $z \in K^\times$ as $z = \pi^k \cdot u$ where $u \in \mathcal{O}_K^\times$. Since $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$ has a finite subgroup of roots of unity $\mu(K)$, we further decompose $u = \zeta \cdot w$ where $w \in 1 + \mathfrak{p}$. We set $\log_\pi(z) = \log(w^m)/m$ for some $m$ such that $w^m \in 1 + p\mathcal{O}_K$. The kernel consists of elements $x$ such that $\log_\pi(x) = 0$, which are precisely those of the form $\pi^k \zeta$ where $\zeta$ is a root of unity. \\[2ex]

(2) The structure of the multiplicative group is $K^\times \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mu(K) \times \mathbb{Z}_p^{[K:\mathbb{Q}_p]}$. When we take the quotient by $n$-th powers, we get $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}) \times (\mu(K)/\mu(K)^n) \times (\mathbb{Z}_p/n\mathbb{Z}_p)^{[K:\mathbb{Q}_p]}$. Since $\mu(K)$ is a finite group and $\mathbb{Z}_p/n\mathbb{Z}_p$ is a finite ring (of order $p^{v_p(n)}$), the entire product is a finite group. \\[2ex]

\textbf{Intuition:} The $p$-adic logarithm "straightens out" the multiplicative structure of the field into an additive one. Just as the real logarithm turns products into sums, the $p$-adic version allows us to use the linear structure of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ to understand the more complex multiplicative group.
\end{solution}
