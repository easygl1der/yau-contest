\section{2015 Geometry & Topology}

\subsection{Individual Exam}

\begin{exercise}
Let $n$, $m$ be positive integers. Show that the product of spheres $S^{n} \times S^{m}$ has trivial tangent bundle if and only if $n$ or $m$ is odd.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} A manifold with a trivial tangent bundle is "parallelizable"—you can pick a consistent set of directions (like a compass) at every point. The Euler characteristic $\chi(M)$ measures the sum of indices of any vector field. If a manifold is parallelizable, it has many non-vanishing vector fields, so $\chi(M)$ must be zero. For spheres, only odd-dimensional ones have $\chi = 0$.

\textbf{Proof:}
The Euler characteristic of a product is the product of the Euler characteristics:
\[ \chi(S^n \times S^m) = \chi(S^n)\chi(S^m) = (1+(-1)^n)(1+(-1)^m) \]
This is zero if and only if at least one of $n, m$ is odd. If both are even, $\chi = 4 \neq 0$, so the tangent bundle cannot be trivial.
Conversely, if $n$ is odd, $S^n$ has a non-vanishing vector field. While $S^n$ itself is only parallelizable for $n=1, 3, 7$, its product with another sphere $S^m$ ($m \ge 1$) provides enough "room" to trivialize the bundle. Specifically, $S^n$ is stably parallelizable for odd $n$, and $TS^n \times TS^m$ becomes trivial.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Show that there does not exist a compact three-dimensional manifold $M$ whose boundary is the real projective space $\mathbb{R}P^{2}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is a question about "cobordism." A manifold is a boundary if its "topological twists" (measured by Stiefel-Whitney numbers) all cancel out. $\mathbb{R}P^2$ is the classic example of a "one-sided" surface that is too twisted to be the boundary of a solid 3D object.

\textbf{Proof:} A manifold is a boundary if and only if all its Stiefel-Whitney numbers are zero. For $\mathbb{R}P^2$:
The total Stiefel-Whitney class is $w(\mathbb{R}P^2) = (1+a)^3 = 1 + a + a^2$ (in $\mathbb{Z}_2$).
The Stiefel-Whitney numbers are $w_1^2[\mathbb{R}P^2] = 1$ and $w_2[\mathbb{R}P^2] = 1$.
Since these are non-zero, $\mathbb{R}P^2$ cannot be a boundary.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M^{n}$ be a smooth manifold without boundary and $X$ a smooth vector field on $M$ which does not vanish at $p \in M$. Show that there exists a local coordinate chart $(U; x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n})$ centered at $p$ such that the vector field $X$ takes the form
\[ X = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_{1}}. \]
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is the "Flow Box Theorem." It says that locally, any non-vanishing flow looks like a uniform stream in a straight pipe. You can always "straighten out" the coordinates to follow the flow lines.

\textbf{Proof:} Pick a small hypersurface $S$ through $p$ transverse to $X$. Use coordinates $(x_2, \dots, x_n)$ on $S$. Define a map $F(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \Phi_{x_1}(p(x_2, \dots, x_n))$, where $\Phi$ is the flow of $X$. By the Inverse Function Theorem, $F$ is a local diffeomorphism. In these new coordinates, moving in the $x_1$ direction is exactly flowing along $X$, so $X = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M \subset \mathbb{R}^{3}$ be a compact simply-connected closed surface. Prove that if $M$ has constant mean curvature, then $M$ is a standard sphere.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is Hopf's Theorem. A "simply-connected" closed surface is topologically a sphere. Constant mean curvature (CMC) means the surface is under uniform "tension" (like a soap bubble). For a sphere, the only way to balance this tension everywhere without any "folds" or "wrinkles" (umbilic points) is to be a round sphere.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} Define the Hopf differential $\Phi$ using the second fundamental form. If $H$ is constant, $\Phi$ is a holomorphic quadratic differential on $M$. Since $M \cong S^2$, and there are no non-zero holomorphic quadratic differentials on a sphere (Riemann-Roch), $\Phi = 0$. This implies every point is umbilic ($k_1 = k_2$). A compact connected surface of umbilic points must be a round sphere.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M$ be an $n$-dimensional compact Riemannian manifold with diameter $\pi/c$ and Ricci curvature $\geq (n-1)c^{2} > 0$. Show that $M$ is isometric to the standard $n$-sphere in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ with radius $1/c$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} Myers' Theorem says that positive Ricci curvature $(n-1)c^2$ forces the manifold to be small (diameter $\le \pi/c$). Cheng's Rigidity Theorem says that if the manifold is as "stretched out" as allowed ($\pi/c$), it must have the exact geometry of the sphere that provided that bound. It's the "most curved" a manifold of that size can be.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} The diameter equality is the extreme case of the Bishop-Gromov volume comparison theorem. If the diameter is maximal, the volume must also be maximal, and the equality case of volume comparison forces the manifold to be a constant-curvature space form, specifically the sphere $S^n(1/c)$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Suppose $(M, g)$ is a Riemannian manifold and $p \in M$. Show that the second-order Taylor series of $g$ in normal coordinates centered at $p$ is
\[ g_{ij}(x) = \delta_{ij} - \frac{1}{3}\sum_{k,l} R_{iklj}\,x_{k}x_{l} + O(|x|^{3}). \]
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} In normal coordinates, the metric is Euclidean at the origin. As you move away, curvature "bends" the space. This expansion shows that the curvature tensor $R$ is precisely the second-order correction that describes how the actual distance starts to deviate from the flat Euclidean approximation.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} In normal coordinates, $\Gamma^k_{ij}(0) = 0$, so first derivatives of $g$ vanish. The second derivatives at the origin are related to the Riemann tensor by the identity $g_{ij,kl} = -\frac{1}{3}(R_{iklj} + R_{ilkj})$. Summing over the symmetric $x_k x_l$ terms yields the result.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $SO(3)$ be the set of all $3 \times 3$ real matrices $A$ with determinant $1$ and satisfying ${}^{t}\!A\,A = I$, where $I$ is the identity matrix and ${}^{t}\!A$ is the transpose of $A$. Show that $SO(3)$ is a smooth manifold, and find its fundamental group. You need to prove your claims.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} $SO(3)$ is the space of rotations of $\mathbb{R}^3$. Any rotation can be described by an axis and an angle. This space looks like a solid ball of radius $\pi$, where antipodal points on the surface are the same (representing a $180^\circ$ rotation). This is exactly the definition of $\mathbb{R}P^3$.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:}
1. \textbf{Manifold:} Use the regular value theorem on $f(A) = A^T A$. The dimension is $3^2 - 6 = 3$.
2. \textbf{Fundamental Group:} The unit quaternions $S^3$ form a double cover of $SO(3)$ via $q \mapsto (v \mapsto qvq^{-1})$. Since $S^3$ is simply connected, $\pi_1(SO(3)) \cong \mathbb{Z}_2$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $X$ be a topological space. The suspension $S(X)$ of $X$ is the space obtained from $X \times [0, 1]$ by contracting $X \times \{0\}$ to a point and contracting $X \times \{1\}$ to another point. Describe the relation between the homology groups of $X$ and $S(X)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} Suspending a space is like adding two "poles." For example, the suspension of a circle $S^1$ is a sphere $S^2$. Each cycle in $X$ gets "stretched" into a cycle of one dimension higher in $S(X)$.

\textbf{Result:} $\tilde{H}_k(S(X)) \cong \tilde{H}_{k-1}(X)$. This is proven using Mayer-Vietoris on the two cones that make up the suspension.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $F : M \to N$ be a smooth map between two manifolds. Let $X_{1}, X_{2}$ be smooth vector fields on $M$ and let $Y_{1}, Y_{2}$ be smooth vector fields on $N$. Prove that if $Y_{1} = F_{*}X_{1}$ and $Y_{2} = F_{*}X_{2}$, then $F_{*}[X_{1}, X_{2}] = [Y_{1}, Y_{2}]$, where $[~, ~]$ is the Lie bracket.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} The Lie bracket measures the failure of two flows to commute. If two vector fields on $M$ are "pushed forward" to $N$, their flows are also pushed forward. Thus, the commutativity (or lack thereof) is preserved by the map.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} For any function $f$ on $N$, $X(f \circ F) = (Yf) \circ F$. Applying this twice and subtracting yields $[X_1, X_2](f \circ F) = ([Y_1, Y_2]f) \circ F$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M_{1}$ and $M_{2}$ be two compact convex closed surfaces in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$, and $f : M_{1} \to M_{2}$ a diffeomorphism such that $M_{1}$ and $M_{2}$ have the same inner normal vectors and Gauss curvatures at the corresponding points. Prove that $f$ is a translation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is the rigidity of the Minkowski problem. A convex shape is uniquely determined by how its surface "bends" (Gauss curvature) as a function of its orientation (normal vector). If two shapes bend the same way at every orientation, they must be the same shape.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} The support functions $h_1, h_2$ satisfy the same elliptic differential equation on $S^2$ involving the Gauss curvature. The only solutions are translates of each other.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Prove the second Bianchi identity:
\[ R_{ijkl,h} + R_{ijlh,k} + R_{ijhk,l} = 0. \]
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This identity is the "differential" version of the fact that the boundary of a boundary is zero. In physics (General Relativity), it ensures that the Einstein tensor is divergence-free, which corresponds to the conservation of energy.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} In normal coordinates at a point, $\Gamma=0$, and the identity reduces to a statement about the commutativity of second partial derivatives of the Christoffel symbols.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M_{1}, M_{2}$ be two complete $n$-dimensional Riemannian manifolds and $\gamma_{i} : [0, a] \to M_{i}$ are two arc length parametrized geodesics. Let $\rho_{i}$ be the distance function to $\gamma_{i}(0)$ on $M_{i}$. Assume that $\gamma_{i}(a)$ is within the cut locus of $\gamma_{i}(0)$ and for any $0 \leq t \leq a$ we have the inequality of sectional curvatures
\[ K_{1}\!\left(X_{1}, \frac{\partial}{\partial \gamma_{1}}\right) \geq K_{2}\!\left(X_{2}, \frac{\partial}{\partial \gamma_{2}}\right), \]
where $X_{i} \in T_{\gamma_{i}(t)}M_{i}$ is any unit vector orthogonal to the tangent $\frac{\partial}{\partial \gamma_{i}}$.
Then
\[ \operatorname{Hess}(\rho_{1})(\tilde{X}_{1}, \tilde{X}_{1}) \leq \operatorname{Hess}(\rho_{2})(\tilde{X}_{2}, \tilde{X}_{2}), \]
where $\tilde{X}_{i} \in T_{\gamma_{i}(a)}M_{i}$ is any unit vector orthogonal to the tangent $\frac{\dot{\sigma}}{\partial \gamma_{i}}(a)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} Hessian of distance measures how "curved" the distance spheres are. In higher curvature ($K_1$), geodesics converge faster, which makes the distance spheres "bend" more sharply towards the center, leading to a smaller (more negative) Hessian compared to lower curvature.

\textbf{Proof Sketch:} The Hessian satisfies a matrix Riccati equation $U' + U^2 + R = 0$. Comparison of Riccati solutions shows that $R_1 \ge R_2$ implies $U_1 \le U_2$.
\end{solution}
