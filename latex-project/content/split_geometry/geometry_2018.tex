\begin{exercise}
Let $X$ be $(S^{2} \times S^{2}) \cup_{S^{2}} D^{3}$, where we attach the 3-disk via the map
\[
S^{2} \longrightarrow S^{2} \vee S^{2}
\]
which crushes a great circle connecting the north and south poles. Compute the homology groups of $X$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} 
The space $S^2 \times S^2$ can be thought of as a CW complex with cells in dimensions 0, 2, 2, and 4. The 2-skeleton is $S^2 \vee S^2$. Attaching a 3-cell $D^3$ to the 2-skeleton means we are "filling in" a certain 2-sphere inside $S^2 \times S^2$. The attachment map is the pinch map, which "wraps" around both $S^2$ factors.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
Let $M = S^2 \times S^2$. The space $X$ is obtained from $M$ by attaching a 3-cell $e^3$ along the map $\phi: S^2 \to M$. The map $\phi$ is the composition of the pinch map $f: S^2 \to S^2 \vee S^2$ and the inclusion $i: S^2 \vee S^2 \hookrightarrow S^2 \times S^2$ onto the 2-skeleton.

The homology of $M = S^2 \times S^2$ is:
\[
H_k(M) \cong \begin{cases} \mathbb{Z} & k=0, 4 \\ \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z} & k=2 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\]
Let $a, b \in H_2(M)$ be the generators corresponding to the two $S^2$ factors. The pinch map $f$ wraps the fundamental class $[S^2]$ once around each sphere in the wedge. Thus, the induced map on homology $\phi_*: H_2(S^2) \to H_2(M)$ satisfies $\phi_*([S^2]) = a + b$.

We use the long exact sequence for the pair $(X, M)$:
\[
\dots \to H_k(M) \to H_k(X) \to H_k(X, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} H_{k-1}(M) \to \dots
\]
Since $X$ is obtained by attaching a 3-cell, $(X, M)$ is a relative CW complex with one 3-cell in dimension 3. Thus $H_3(X, M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ and $H_k(X, M) = 0$ for $k \neq 3$. The boundary map $\partial: H_3(X, M) \to H_2(M)$ is the map $\phi_*$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $k=0, 1$: $H_0(X) \cong H_0(M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ and $H_1(X) \cong H_1(M) = 0$.
    \item $k=2$: The sequence gives $H_3(X, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} H_2(M) \to H_2(X) \to 0$. Thus $H_2(X) \cong H_2(M) / \text{im}(\partial) \cong (\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}) / \langle (1, 1) \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
    \item $k=3$: $0 \to H_3(X) \to H_3(X, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} H_2(M)$ is exact. Since $\partial(1) = a+b \neq 0$ is injective, we have $H_3(X) = 0$.
    \item $k=4$: $H_4(M) \to H_4(X) \to 0$ is exact, so $H_4(X) \cong H_4(M) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Conclusion:}
The homology groups are: $H_0(X) = \mathbb{Z}, H_1(X) = 0, H_2(X) = \mathbb{Z}, H_3(X) = 0, H_4(X) = \mathbb{Z}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
(a) Let $A$ be a single circle in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$. Compute the fundamental group $\pi_{1}(\mathbb{R}^{3} - A)$.

(b) Let $A$ and $B$ be disjoint circles in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$, supported in the upper and lower half space, respectively. Compute $\pi_{1}(\mathbb{R}^{3} - (A \cup B))$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Pedagogical Note:} This problem explores the topology of complements of knots and links. Even for unknots, the complement has interesting structure.

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
Imagine $\mathbb{R}^3$ as $S^3$ minus a point at infinity. A circle $A$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ can be viewed as an unknotted circle in $S^3$. The complement of a circle in $S^3$ is another circle (they are linked in the sense of the Hopf fibration). When we remove a point (infinity) from this complement circle, it doesn't change the homotopy type from $S^1$ unless the point was on the circle itself.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
(a) Consider $S^3 = \mathbb{R}^3 \cup \{\infty\}$. The complement of a circle $A$ in $S^3$ is homotopy equivalent to $S^1$ (think of $S^3$ as two solid tori $S^1 \times D^2 \cup D^2 \times S^1$ glued along their boundary $S^1 \times S^1$; if $A$ is the core of one torus, the complement is the other torus, which retracts to its core $S^1$).
Then $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus A = S^3 \setminus (A \cup \{\infty\})$. This is homotopy equivalent to $S^1 \vee S^2$ (removing a point from a 3-manifold is like adding a 2-sphere at that point).
However, for fundamental group purposes, $\pi_1(\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus A) \cong \pi_1(S^1 \vee S^2) \cong \pi_1(S^1) * \pi_1(S^2) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \{e\} \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

(b) Let $U = \{z > - \epsilon\} \setminus A$ and $V = \{z < \epsilon\} \setminus B$. Then $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus (A \cup B) = U \cup V$. The intersection $U \cap V \cong \mathbb{R}^2 \times (-\epsilon, \epsilon)$ is contractible, so $\pi_1(U \cap V) = 0$. 
By the Seifert-van Kampen theorem:
\[
\pi_1(\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus (A \cup B)) \cong \pi_1(U) *_{\pi_1(U \cap V)} \pi_1(V) \cong \pi_1(U) * \pi_1(V) \cong \mathbb{Z} * \mathbb{Z} \cong F_2
\]
where $F_2$ is the free group on two generators. Intuitively, each circle provides one independent loop that cannot be shrunk to a point.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Consider the differential 1-form $\omega = x\, dy - y\, dx + dz$ in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$ with coordinates $(x, y, z)$. Prove that $f\omega$ is not closed for any nowhere zero function $f : \mathbb{R}^{3} \to \mathbb{R}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Pedagogical Motivation:}
This problem asks whether the distribution (plane field) defined by $\text{ker}(\omega)$ is integrable. If $f\omega$ were closed, say $d(f\omega)=0$, then locally $f\omega = dg$ for some function $g$, meaning the kernels would be the tangent planes to the level sets $g=const$. Such a 1-form is called a \textit{contact form} if $\omega \wedge d\omega$ is nowhere zero.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
A 1-form $\omega$ satisfies $d(f\omega) = 0$ for some $f \neq 0$ only if $\omega \wedge d\omega = 0$ (the Frobenius Integrability Condition).
Let's compute $d\omega$:
\[
d\omega = d(x\, dy) - d(y\, dx) + d(dz) = (dx \wedge dy) - (dy \wedge dx) + 0 = 2 dx \wedge dy
\]
Now compute $\omega \wedge d\omega$:
\[
\omega \wedge d\omega = (x\, dy - y\, dx + dz) \wedge (2 dx \wedge dy) = 2x(dy \wedge dx \wedge dy) - 2y(dx \wedge dx \wedge dy) + 2(dz \wedge dx \wedge dy)
\]
The first two terms vanish ($dx \wedge dx = 0$). We are left with:
\[
\omega \wedge d\omega = 2\, dz \wedge dx \wedge dy = 2\, dx \wedge dy \wedge dz
\]
This is $2$ times the standard volume form, which is nowhere zero.
If there existed $f \neq 0$ such that $d(f\omega) = 0$, then $df \wedge \omega + f d\omega = 0$.
Wedging with $\omega$ gives:
\[
\omega \wedge (df \wedge \omega) + f \omega \wedge d\omega = 0 \implies 0 + f \omega \wedge d\omega = 0
\]
Since $f$ is nowhere zero, this would require $\omega \wedge d\omega = 0$, a contradiction.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Show that
\[
Q^{n} := \left\{(x^{1}, \dots, x^{n+1}) \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1}; \sum_{i=1}^{n+1} (x^{i})^{4} = 1 \right\}
\]
is a differentiable manifold.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
$Q^n$ is a "squircle-like" generalization to $n$ dimensions. Just as $x^2+y^2=1$ is a circle, $x^4+y^4=1$ is a smooth closed curve that looks more like a square with rounded corners.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
Define $g: \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \to \mathbb{R}$ by $g(x) = \sum (x^i)^4$. $Q^n = g^{-1}(1)$.
$1$ is a regular value if $dg \neq 0$ for all $x \in g^{-1}(1)$.
\[
dg = \sum_{i=1}^{n+1} 4(x^i)^3 dx^i
\]
The differential $dg$ vanishes only if $4(x^i)^3 = 0$ for all $i$, i.e., at the origin $(0, \dots, 0)$.
However, $g(0, \dots, 0) = 0 \neq 1$. So $dg$ is never zero on $Q^n$.
By the Regular Value Theorem, $Q^n$ is a smooth manifold of dimension $n$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M$ be a closed surface in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$. Prove that
\[
\int_{M} |K|\, d\sigma \geq 4\pi(1 + g),
\]
where $K$, $g$ and $d\sigma$ is the Gaussian curvature, the genus and the area element of $M$, respectively.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Pedagogical Note:} 
The total absolute curvature $\int |K| d\sigma$ measures how much the surface "bends" in total. Unlike the Gauss-Bonnet integral $\int K d\sigma$ which is a topological invariant, this integral depends on the embedding.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
By Gauss-Bonnet: $\int_M K \, d\sigma = 2\pi \chi(M) = 2\pi(2 - 2g) = 4\pi(1 - g)$.
Let $M_+ = \{p \in M : K(p) \geq 0\}$ and $M_- = \{p \in M : K(p) < 0\}$.
\[
\int_{M_+} K \, d\sigma + \int_{M_-} K \, d\sigma = 4\pi(1 - g) \tag{1}
\]
The total absolute curvature is:
\[
\int_M |K| \, d\sigma = \int_{M_+} K \, d\sigma - \int_{M_-} K \, d\sigma \tag{2}
\]
From (1), $-\int_{M_-} K \, d\sigma = \int_{M_+} K \, d\sigma - 4\pi(1 - g)$.
Substitute into (2):
\[
\int_M |K| \, d\sigma = 2 \int_{M_+} K \, d\sigma - 4\pi + 4\pi g
\]
A fundamental property of compact surfaces $M \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ is that the Gauss map restricted to the region $K \geq 0$ must cover $S^2$ at least once (to "surround" the origin in some sense). Thus $\int_{M_+} K d\sigma \geq \text{Area}(S^2) = 4\pi$.
Therefore:
\[
\int_M |K| \, d\sigma \geq 2(4\pi) - 4\pi + 4\pi g = 4\pi + 4\pi g = 4\pi(1 + g)
\]
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M$ be an $n$-dimensional compact and simply connected Riemannian manifold. If the sectional curvature $K_{M}$ of $M$ satisfies
\[
\frac{1}{4} < K_{M} \leq 1,
\]
then $M$ is homeomorphic to $S^{n}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Context:} 
This is the \textit{Quarter-Pinch Sphere Theorem}. The constant $1/4$ is sharp, as $\mathbb{CP}^n$ with the Fubini-Study metric has sectional curvature in the range $[1/4, 1]$. Since $\pi_2(\mathbb{CP}^n) = \mathbb{Z}$, it is not a sphere.

\textbf{Sketch of Proof:}
1. \textbf{Injectivity Radius:} Klingenberg proved that for a simply connected manifold with $K \leq 1$, the injectivity radius $inj(M) \geq \pi$.
2. \textbf{Diameter:} By Bonnet-Myers, since $K > 1/4$, the diameter $diam(M) \leq \pi / \sqrt{1/4} = 2\pi$. 
3. \textbf{Morse Theory:} One can use the distance function from a point $p$. If $q$ is a point at maximum distance from $p$, one can show using the $1/4$-pinching and $inj(M) \geq \pi$ that $p$ and $q$ are the only critical points of a slightly smoothed distance function.
4. \textbf{Reeb's Theorem:} A manifold admitting a Morse function with exactly two critical points is homeomorphic to a sphere.
This establishes that $M \cong S^n$ homeomorphically. (The stronger \textit{Differentiable Sphere Theorem} by Brendle and Schoen shows it is diffeomorphic to $S^n$ using the Ricci flow).
\end{solution}
