\subsection{Exam}

\begin{exercise}
The topological space $X$ is obtained by gluing two tetrahedra as illustrated. The resulting complex has 2 tetrahedra, 4 triangles, 2 edges and 1 vertex. Show that $X$ cannot have the homotopy type of a compact manifold without boundary.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Pedagogical Note:} For a compact 3-manifold without boundary, the Euler characteristic must be zero. This is a direct consequence of Poincaré duality.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
Let $X$ be the CW complex formed by the gluing. The Euler characteristic is:
\[ \chi(X) = V - E + F - T \]
where $V, E, F, T$ are the number of vertices, edges, faces, and tetrahedra respectively.
Given $V=1, E=2, F=4, T=2$:
\[ \chi(X) = 1 - 2 + 4 - 2 = 1. \]
For any compact oriented 3-manifold $M$, Poincaré duality implies $b_0 = b_3$ and $b_1 = b_2$. Thus:
\[ \chi(M) = b_0 - b_1 + b_2 - b_3 = 1 - b_1 + b_1 - 1 = 0. \]
If $M$ is non-orientable, we still have $\chi(M) = 0$ (e.g., using $\mathbb{Z}_2$ coefficients or the oriented double cover).
Since $\chi(X) = 1 \neq 0$, $X$ cannot have the homotopy type of a compact manifold without boundary.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Suppose $(M, h)$ is a closed Riemannian manifold with $\sec(h) \leq -1$. Suppose $\Sigma$ is a closed minimal surface with genus $g$ in $(M, h)$. Show that $\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma) \leq 4\pi(g - 1)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} In negative curvature, surfaces must be "stretched" to close up. Minimal surfaces follow the ambient curvature but are "flatter" than the background due to their zero mean curvature.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
By the \textit{Gauss Equation} for an immersed surface $\Sigma \subset M$:
\[ K_\Sigma = K_M(T\Sigma) + \kappa_1 \kappa_2 \]
where $K_\Sigma$ is the Gaussian curvature of $\Sigma$, $K_M(T\Sigma)$ is the sectional curvature of $M$ tangent to $\Sigma$, and $\kappa_i$ are principal curvatures.
Since $\Sigma$ is minimal, $\kappa_1 + \kappa_2 = 0 \implies \kappa_2 = -\kappa_1$.
Thus $\kappa_1 \kappa_2 = -\kappa_1^2 \leq 0$.
Given $K_M \leq -1$, we have:
\[ K_\Sigma \leq -1 - \kappa_1^2 \leq -1. \]
By the \textit{Gauss-Bonnet Theorem}:
\[ 2\pi \chi(\Sigma) = \int_\Sigma K_\Sigma \, dA \leq \int_\Sigma (-1) \, dA = -\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma). \]
Since $\chi(\Sigma) = 2 - 2g$, we have:
\[ 2\pi(2 - 2g) \leq -\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma) \implies \operatorname{Area}(\Sigma) \leq 2\pi(2g - 2) = 4\pi(g - 1). \]
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Show that $\mathsf{SP}^{n}(\mathbb{CP}^{1})$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is a high-dimensional version of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. A set of $n$ points on the Riemann sphere (including infinity) uniquely determines a polynomial of degree $n$ up to scaling.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
Identify $\mathbb{CP}^1$ with $\mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}$. A point in $\mathsf{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1)$ is an unordered set of $n$ points $\{z_1, \dots, z_n\}$.
Define a map $\Phi: \mathsf{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1) \to \mathbb{CP}^n$ by mapping the set $\{z_1, \dots, z_n\}$ to the coefficients of the polynomial $P(z) = \prod_{i=1}^n (z - z_i)$.
Specifically, if we use homogeneous coordinates $[u_i : v_i]$ for each point, the product
\[ \prod_{i=1}^n (u_i z - v_i w) = a_n z^n + a_{n-1} z^{n-1} w + \dots + a_0 w^n \]
defines a point $[a_0 : a_1 : \dots : a_n] \in \mathbb{CP}^n$.
The map is well-defined because the product is symmetric in the factors. By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, every polynomial has a unique set of roots up to ordering, so the map is a bijection. Since both spaces are compact Hausdorff and the map is continuous, it is a homeomorphism.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Show that if a complete noncompact Riemannian manifold $M$ does not have the geodesic loops to infinity property, then there is a line in the universal cover $\widetilde{M}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Definition:} A complete noncompact manifold $M$ is said to have the \textit{geodesic loops to infinity property} if for every element $g \in \pi_1(M)$ and every compact set $K \subset M$, there exists a geodesic loop $\gamma$ contained in $M \setminus K$ that represents the conjugacy class of $g$.

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This property, introduced by Christina Sormani, relates the "trapping" of topological features to the geometric growth of the manifold. If a loop is "stuck" near a core and cannot be pushed to infinity without losing its "geodesic" (locally length-minimizing) status, it implies that the manifold has a certain "rigidity" at infinity that forces the existence of a line in its cover.

\textbf{Formal Proof:}
Suppose $M$ does not have the property. Then there exists an element $g \in \pi_1(M, p)$ and a compact set $K$ such that no geodesic loop representing $g$ lies entirely outside $K$.
Let $\gamma: [0, \infty) \to M$ be a ray starting at $p$. For each $r > 0$, let $c_r$ be the shortest loop based at $\gamma(r)$ that is homotopic to the loop formed by $\gamma|_{[0,r]}$, a loop at $p$ representing $g$, and the reverse of $\gamma|_{[0,r]}$.
As $r \to \infty$, the distance from the basepoint $\gamma(r)$ to $K$ increases. If the loops $c_r$ were to stay away from $K$ for large $r$, they would eventually be geodesic loops representing $g$ at infinity, contradicting our assumption.
Thus, for all large $r$, the loop $c_r$ must intersect $K$. 
Let $q_r \in c_r \cap K$. Since $K$ is compact, there exists a subsequence $r_i \to \infty$ such that $q_{r_i} \to q \in K$.
The loop $c_r$ consists of two segments of length $L_r/2$ connecting $\gamma(r)$ to $q_r$. As $r \to \infty$, these segments "stretch out" across the manifold. 
In the universal cover $\widetilde{M}$, these segments lift to a path between two points $\tilde{\gamma}(r)$ and $g\tilde{\gamma}(r)$. The fact that these paths always pass through the lift of $K$ implies that the sequence of segments converges (by Arzela-Ascoli) to a geodesic $\sigma: (-\infty, \infty) \to \widetilde{M}$ that is distance-minimizing on any interval.
Such a geodesic is a \textbf{line}. The existence of this line is a fundamental result that links the topology of $\pi_1(M)$ to the splitting properties of $\widetilde{M}$ via the Cheeger-Gromoll Splitting Theorem.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
(a) Show that $\pi_1$ of an H-space is Abelian.
(b) Show $S^{2022}$ is not an H-space.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) In an H-space $X$, the map $\mu: X \times X \to X$ induces a map $\mu_*: \pi_1(X) \times \pi_1(X) \to \pi_1(X)$.
Let $\alpha, \beta \in \pi_1(X, e)$. By the property of the identity $e$:
$\mu_*([\alpha], [e]) = [\alpha]$ and $\mu_*([e], [\beta]) = [\beta]$.
Then $[\alpha] \cdot [\beta] = \mu_*([\alpha], [e]) \cdot \mu_*([e], [\beta]) = \mu_*([\alpha] \cdot [e], [e] \cdot [\beta]) = \mu_*([\alpha], [\beta])$.
Similarly, $[\beta] \cdot [\alpha] = \mu_*([e], [\beta]) \cdot \mu_*([\alpha], [e]) = \mu_*([e] \cdot [\alpha], [\beta] \cdot [e]) = \mu_*([\alpha], [\beta])$.
Thus $[\alpha] \cdot [\beta] = [\beta] \cdot [\alpha]$.

(b) Adams' \textit{Hopf Invariant One Theorem} states that $S^{n-1}$ is an H-space if and only if $n = 1, 2, 4, 8$.
This corresponds to $n-1 = 0, 1, 3, 7$.
Since $2022$ is none of these, $S^{2022}$ is not an H-space.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
(a) Show $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$ is a shrinker. (b) Compact shrinker intersects $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
(a) For a sphere of radius $R$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, the principal curvatures are $1/R$. 
The mean curvature (sum) is $H = n/R$. 
The normal vector is $x/R$, so $\langle x, \mathbf{n} \rangle = \langle x, x/R \rangle = R^2/R = R$.
The shrinker equation is $H = \frac{1}{2} \langle x, \mathbf{n} \rangle$.
$n/R = R/2 \implies R^2 = 2n \implies R = \sqrt{2n}$.

(b) This is a consequence of the \textit{Maximum Principle}. 
The shrinker equation can be written as $H - \frac{1}{2} x \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0$.
Consider a compact shrinker $\Sigma$. Let $R_{max}$ be the maximum distance from the origin to $\Sigma$. At a point $p$ where the distance is maximal, $\Sigma$ is tangent to a sphere $S^n(R_{max})$ and lies inside it.
At this point, $H_\Sigma \geq H_{S(R_{max})} = n/R_{max}$ and $\langle x, \mathbf{n} \rangle = R_{max}$.
Substituting into the shrinker equation: $0 = H - \frac{1}{2} R_{max} \geq \frac{n}{R_{max}} - \frac{R_{max}}{2}$.
This implies $R_{max}^2 \geq 2n$, so $R_{max} \geq \sqrt{2n}$.
Similarly, for the minimum distance $R_{min}$, we get $R_{min} \leq \sqrt{2n}$.
Since $R_{min} \leq \sqrt{2n} \leq R_{max}$, the surface must intersect the sphere of radius $\sqrt{2n}$ (unless it is the sphere itself).
\end{solution}
