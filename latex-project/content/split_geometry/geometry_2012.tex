\subsection{Individual Exam}

\begin{exercise}
Show that $\pi_{3}(S^{2}) \neq 0$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} In low dimensions, we often expect $\pi_n(S^k) = 0$ for $n > k$ because a lower-dimensional sphere should "rattle around" inside a higher-dimensional one and be shrinkable. However, the Hopf fibration $S^3 \to S^2$ shows that $S^3$ can be non-trivially "wrapped" around $S^2$ such that the inverse image of every point is a linked circle ($S^1$). This "linking" is what prevents the map from being null-homotopic.

\textbf{Proof:} Consider the Hopf fibration $S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3 \xrightarrow{h} S^2$. This is a fiber bundle with base $S^2$, total space $S^3$, and fiber $S^1$. The long exact sequence of homotopy groups for a fibration gives:
\[ \dots \to \pi_3(S^1) \to \pi_3(S^3) \to \pi_3(S^2) \to \pi_2(S^1) \to \dots \]
It is well-known that $\pi_n(S^1) = 0$ for $n > 1$. Thus $\pi_3(S^1) = 0$ and $\pi_2(S^1) = 0$. The sequence becomes:
\[ 0 \to \pi_3(S^3) \xrightarrow{h_*} \pi_3(S^2) \to 0 \]
This implies that $h_*: \pi_3(S^3) \to \pi_3(S^2)$ is an isomorphism. Since $\pi_3(S^3) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (generated by the identity map), we conclude that $\pi_3(S^2) \cong \mathbb{Z} \neq 0$. The generator is the homotopy class of the Hopf map $h$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M$ be a smooth manifold of dimension $n$, and $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{k}$ be $k$ everywhere linearly independent smooth vector fields on an open set $U \subset M$ satisfying that $[X_{i}, X_{j}] = 0$ for $1 \leq i, j \leq k$. Prove that for any point $p \in U$ there is a coordinate chart $(V, y^{i})$ with $p \in V \subseteq U$ such that $X_{i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y^{i}}$ for each $1 \leq i \leq k$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is a special case of the Frobenius Theorem. The condition $[X_i, X_j]=0$ means that moving along $X_i$ and then $X_j$ is the same as moving along $X_j$ and then $X_i$. This commutativity allows us to build a consistent "grid" or coordinate system where the vector fields $X_i$ serve as the coordinate axes.

\textbf{Proof:} Let $\Phi_t^{(i)}$ be the local flow of the vector field $X_i$. Since $[X_i, X_j] = 0$, the flows commute: $\Phi_t^{(i)} \circ \Phi_s^{(j)} = \Phi_s^{(j)} \circ \Phi_t^{(i)}$ for sufficiently small $s, t$. Choose a smooth $(n-k)$-dimensional submanifold $S$ passing through $p$ that is transverse to the span of $\{X_1, \dots, X_k\}$ at $p$. Let $(z^1, \dots, z^{n-k})$ be local coordinates on $S$ such that $p$ corresponds to $0$.

Define a map $\Psi: W \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k \times S \to M$ by:
\[ \Psi(t_1, \dots, t_k, q) = \Phi_{t_1}^{(1)} \circ \dots \circ \Phi_{t_k}^{(k)}(q) \]
where $q \in S$. The differential of $\Psi$ at $(0, p)$ is:
\[ d\Psi_{(0,p)}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t_i}\right) = X_i(p), \quad d\Psi_{(0,p)}(v) = v \text{ for } v \in T_p S. \]
Since $\{X_i(p)\}$ are linearly independent and $T_p S$ is transverse to their span, $d\Psi_{(0,p)}$ is an isomorphism. By the Inverse Function Theorem, $\Psi$ is a local diffeomorphism on some neighborhood $V$ of $(0, p)$. Let $y^i = t_i$ for $1 \leq i \leq k$ and $y^{k+j} = z^j$ for $1 \leq j \leq n-k$. In these coordinates, we have:
\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y^i} f(y) = \frac{d}{dt} \Big|_{t=0} f(\Psi(y^1, \dots, y^i+t, \dots, y^n)) = \frac{d}{dt} \Big|_{t=0} f(\Phi_t^{(i)}(\Psi(y))) = X_i f \]
Thus $X_i = \frac{\partial}{\partial y^i}$ for $1 \leq i \leq k$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Show that any self homeomorphism of $\mathbb{CP}^{2}$ is orientation preserving.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} Complex projective spaces carry a natural orientation from their complex structure. For $\mathbb{CP}^n$, any homeomorphism must respect the intersection product. In the case of $\mathbb{CP}^2$, the intersection of two "lines" (copies of $\mathbb{CP}^1$) is a single point with a positive sign. Even if a map reverses the sign of the 2-dimensional generator, the squared generator (representing the orientation) remains positive.

\textbf{Proof:} The cohomology ring of $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is given by $H^*(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}[x] / (x^3)$, where $x \in H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$ is a generator. The orientation of $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is determined by the fundamental class $[M] \in H_4(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$, which is dual to the generator $x^2 \in H^4(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$.

Let $f: \mathbb{CP}^2 \to \mathbb{CP}^2$ be a homeomorphism. It induces an automorphism $f^*: H^*(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z}) \to H^*(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$. Since $f^*$ must preserve the ring structure and $H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, we must have $f^*(x) = \epsilon x$, where $\epsilon = \pm 1$.
Then for the generator of $H^4$, we have:
\[ f^*(x^2) = (f^*(x))^2 = (\epsilon x)^2 = \epsilon^2 x^2 = x^2. \]
The degree of $f$, denoted $\deg(f)$, is the integer such that $f^*([M]^\vee) = \deg(f) [M]^\vee$, where $[M]^\vee$ is the cohomological fundamental class (the generator of $H^4$). Our calculation shows $f^*(x^2) = 1 \cdot x^2$, so $\deg(f) = 1$. A map with degree $1$ is orientation preserving. Thus, any self homeomorphism of $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is orientation preserving.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Prove the following version of the isoperimetric inequality: Suppose $C$ is a simple (that is, without self-intersection), smooth, closed curve in the Euclidean plane, with length $L$. Show that the area enclosed by $C$ is less than or equal to $\frac{L^{2}}{4\pi}$, and the equality occurs when and only when $C$ is a round circle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} The circle is the "most efficient" shape in the sense that it maximizes area for a fixed perimeter. Any deviation from a circle (like an oval or a star) involves "stretching" the perimeter more than is necessary to contain the area.

\textbf{Proof:} Assume the curve $C$ is parametrized by arc length $s \in [0, L]$, so $(x(s), y(s))$ satisfies $(x')^2 + (y')^2 = 1$. The area $A$ is given by Green's Theorem: $A = \int_0^L x y' ds$. Without loss of generality, assume $\int_0^L x ds = 0$.
By Wirtinger's Inequality, if $f$ is $L$-periodic and $\int_0^L f ds = 0$, then $\int_0^L (f')^2 ds \geq (2\pi/L)^2 \int_0^L f^2 ds$.
We have $L = \int_0^L ((x')^2 + (y')^2) ds$.
Consider the quantity $L^2 - 4\pi A$:
\[ L^2 - 4\pi A = L \int_0^L ((x')^2 + (y')^2) ds - 4\pi \int_0^L x y' ds \]
Using the inequality $2xy' \leq \frac{2\pi}{L} x^2 + \frac{L}{2\pi} (y')^2$, we have:
\[ 4\pi A = 4\pi \int_0^L x y' ds \leq \int_0^L \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{L} x^2 + L (y')^2 \right) ds \]
Then:
\[ L^2 - 4\pi A \geq L \int_0^L (x')^2 ds + L \int_0^L (y')^2 ds - \int_0^L \frac{4\pi^2}{L} x^2 ds - L \int_0^L (y')^2 ds \]
\[ = L \int_0^L \left( (x')^2 - \left(\frac{2\pi}{L}\right)^2 x^2 \right) ds \geq 0 \]
by Wirtinger's inequality. Equality holds if and only if $x(s) = a \cos(2\pi s/L + \phi)$ and $y' = \frac{2\pi}{L} x$, which implies $y(s) = a \sin(2\pi s/L + \phi) + b$. This is the parametrization of a circle of radius $R = L/2\pi$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $x : M \to \mathbb{R}^{3}$ be a closed surface in 3-dimensional Euclidean space. Its Gaussian curvature and mean curvature are denoted by $K$ and $H$ respectively. Prove that:
\[ \iint_{M} H\, dA + \iint_{M} pK\, dA = 0, \quad \iint_{M} pH\, dA + \iint_{M} dA = 0, \]
where $p = \vec{x} \cdot \vec{n}$ is the support function of $M$, $\vec{x}$ denotes the position vector of $M$, $\vec{n}$ denotes the unit normal to $M$, and $dA$ is the area element of $M$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} These are the Minkowski formulas. They relate the total curvature of a surface to its size and position. The second formula $\int (1+pH) = 0$ is essentially a statement about the variation of area: if you expand the surface radially, the change in area is related to the mean curvature.

\textbf{Proof:} Let $\vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2$ be a local orthonormal frame on $M$. Let $h_{ij}$ be the second fundamental form. Then $H = \frac{1}{2}(h_{11} + h_{22})$ and $K = h_{11}h_{22} - h_{12}^2$. The Weingarten map is $d\vec{n} = -h_{ij} \theta^j \vec{e}_i$.
Consider the vector field $X$ on $M$ defined by the tangential part of $\vec{x}$, i.e., $X = \vec{x} - (\vec{x} \cdot \vec{n})\vec{n} = \vec{x} - p\vec{n}$.
Then $div(X) = \sum_{i} \langle \nabla_{\vec{e}_i} (\vec{x} - p\vec{n}), \vec{e}_i \rangle = \sum_i ( \langle \vec{e}_i, \vec{e}_i \rangle - p \langle \nabla_{\vec{e}_i} \vec{n}, \vec{e}_i \rangle ) = 2 + p(h_{11} + h_{22}) = 2 + 2pH$.
By the Divergence Theorem, $\iint_M (2 + 2pH) dA = 0$, which gives $\iint_M (1 + pH) dA = 0$.

For the second formula, consider the divergence of the vector field $Y = \tilde{A} X$ where $\tilde{A}$ is the classical adjoint of the shape operator $A$. The divergence is $div(\tilde{A} X) = 2(H + pK)$.
Integrating over $M$ gives the result: $\iint_M (H + pK) dA = 0$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Write the structure equation of an orthonormal frame on a Riemannian manifold. Prove the following Riemannian metric $g$ has constant sectional curvature $c$ using the structure equation:
\[ g = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (dx^{i})^{2}}{[1 + \frac{c}{4}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x^{i})^{2}]^{2}} \]
where $(x^{1}, \ldots, x^{n})$ is a local coordinate system.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This metric is the standard "model" for constant curvature spaces. If $c > 0$, it is the metric of the $n$-sphere obtained via stereographic projection. The denominator $1 + \frac{c}{4}r^2$ accounts for the "stretching" needed to map a flat coordinate plane onto a curved sphere.

\textbf{Proof:} The structure equations for an orthonormal coframe $\{\theta^i\}$ and connection 1-forms $\{\omega^i_j\}$ are:
1. First structure equation (torsion-free): $d\theta^i = \sum_j \omega^i_j \wedge \theta^j$, with $\omega^i_j = -\omega^j_i$.
2. Second structure equation (curvature): $\Omega^i_j = d\omega^i_j - \sum_k \omega^i_k \wedge \omega^k_j = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k,l} R^i_{jkl} \theta^k \wedge \theta^l$.
For constant sectional curvature $c$, we must have $\Omega^i_j = c \, \theta^i \wedge \theta^j$.

Given $g = \phi^{-2} \sum (dx^i)^2$ with $\phi = 1 + \frac{c}{4} \sum (x^i)^2$. Choose coframe $\theta^i = \phi^{-1} dx^i$.
Then $d\theta^i = -\phi^{-2} d\phi \wedge dx^i = -\phi^{-1} d\phi \wedge \theta^i$.
Since $d\phi = \frac{c}{2} \sum x^j dx^j$, we have $\phi^{-1} d\phi = \frac{c}{2} \sum x^j \theta^j$.
$d\theta^i = -\frac{c}{2} \sum_j x^j \theta^j \wedge \theta^i = \sum_j \left( \frac{c}{2} (x^i \theta^j - x^j \theta^i) \right) \wedge \theta^j$.
Thus $\omega^i_j = \frac{c}{2} (x^i \theta^j - x^j \theta^i)$.
Now compute $\Omega^i_j$:
$d\omega^i_j = \frac{c}{2} (dx^i \wedge \theta^j + x^i d\theta^j - dx^j \wedge \theta^i - x^j d\theta^i)$
$= \frac{c}{2} (\phi \theta^i \wedge \theta^j - x^i \frac{c}{2} (\sum x^k \theta^k) \wedge \theta^j - \phi \theta^j \wedge \theta^i + x^j \frac{c}{2} (\sum x^k \theta^k) \wedge \theta^i)$
$= c\phi \theta^i \wedge \theta^j - \frac{c^2}{4} x^i (\sum x^k \theta^k) \wedge \theta^j + \frac{c^2}{4} x^j (\sum x^k \theta^k) \wedge \theta^i$.
And $\omega^i_k \wedge \omega^k_j = \frac{c^2}{4} (x^i \theta^k - x^k \theta^i) \wedge (x^k \theta^j - x^j \theta^k) = \frac{c^2}{4} x^i (\sum x^k \theta^k) \wedge \theta^j - \frac{c^2}{4} (\sum (x^k)^2) \theta^i \wedge \theta^j + \frac{c^2}{4} x^j \theta^i \wedge (\sum x^k \theta^k)$.
Subtracting: $\Omega^i_j = d\omega^i_j - \omega^i_k \wedge \omega^k_j = (c\phi - \frac{c^2}{4} \sum (x^k)^2) \theta^i \wedge \theta^j = c \theta^i \wedge \theta^j$.
Thus the sectional curvature is constant $c$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Prove that the real projective space $\mathbb{RP}^{n}$ is a differentiable manifold of dimension $n$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
$\mathbb{RP}^n$ is the quotient space $(\mathbb{R}^{n+1} \setminus \{0\}) / \sim$ where $x \sim \lambda x$ for $\lambda \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$. Let $[x_0: \dots : x_n]$ denote the equivalence class.
Define $U_i = \{ [x_0: \dots : x_n] \in \mathbb{RP}^n \mid x_i \neq 0 \}$ for $i=0, \dots, n$. These are open sets and cover $\mathbb{RP}^n$.
Define charts $\phi_i: U_i \to \mathbb{R}^n$ by:
\[ \phi_i([x_0: \dots : x_n]) = \left( \frac{x_0}{x_i}, \dots, \frac{x_{i-1}}{x_i}, \frac{x_{i+1}}{x_i}, \dots, \frac{x_n}{x_i} \right) \]
The inverse $\phi_i^{-1}: \mathbb{R}^n \to U_i$ is $\phi_i^{-1}(y_1, \dots, y_n) = [y_1: \dots : 1 : \dots : y_n]$ where $1$ is at the $i$-th position.
The transition maps $\phi_j \circ \phi_i^{-1}$ are of the form $(y_1/y_j, \dots, 1/y_j, \dots, y_n/y_j)$, which are smooth where defined ($y_j \neq 0$).
Since the quotient map $\pi: S^n \to \mathbb{RP}^n$ is an open map and $S^n$ is second-countable and Hausdorff, $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is also second-countable and Hausdorff. Thus $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is a differentiable manifold of dimension $n$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M, N$ be $n$-dimensional smooth, compact, connected manifolds, and $f : M \to N$ a smooth map with rank equals to $n$ everywhere. Show that $f$ is a covering map.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} A map with full rank is a local diffeomorphism. Compactness ensures that the "sheets" of the map don't just disappear or stack up infinitely at any point. Connectedness forces the number of sheets to be constant, creating a uniform covering structure.

\textbf{Proof:} Since $\text{rank}(f) = n = \dim M = \dim N$, $f$ is a local diffeomorphism.
1. Surjectivity: $f(M)$ is an open set in $N$ (by local diffeomorphism). Since $M$ is compact and $f$ is continuous, $f(M)$ is closed in $N$. Since $N$ is connected, $f(M) = N$.
2. Finite fibers: For any $y \in N$, $f^{-1}(y)$ is a discrete set in $M$. Since $M$ is compact, $f^{-1}(y)$ must be finite. Let $f^{-1}(y) = \{x_1, \dots, x_k\}$.
3. Evenly covered neighborhoods: For each $x_i$, there exists an open neighborhood $U_i$ such that $f|_{U_i}: U_i \to V_i$ is a diffeomorphism. We can assume $U_1, \dots, U_k$ are disjoint. Let $V = (\cap V_i) \setminus f(M \setminus \cup U_i)$. Since $f$ is a closed map (compact to Hausdorff), $f(M \setminus \cup U_i)$ is closed, so $V$ is open.
Then $f^{-1}(V) = \cup_{i=1}^k (f^{-1}(V) \cap U_i)$, a disjoint union of sets each diffeomorphic to $V$. Thus $f$ is a covering map.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Given any Riemannian manifold $(M^{n}, g)$, show that there exists a unique Riemannian connection on $M^{n}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} The Levi-Civita connection is the unique way to differentiate vector fields such that the "lengths" and "angles" are preserved under parallel transport, and there is no "twisting" (torsion). It is the "intrinsic" derivative defined solely by the metric.

\textbf{Proof:} The Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$ is defined by the following two conditions:
1. Compatibility with metric: $X g(Y, Z) = g(\nabla_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \nabla_X Z)$.
2. Symmetry (torsion-free): $\nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X, Y]$.
By cyclic permutation of the first condition:
$X g(Y, Z) = g(\nabla_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \nabla_X Z)$
$Y g(Z, X) = g(\nabla_Y Z, X) + g(Z, \nabla_Y X)$
$-Z g(X, Y) = -g(\nabla_Z X, Y) - g(X, \nabla_Z Y)$
Adding these and using the symmetry condition, we obtain the Koszul formula:
$2g(\nabla_X Y, Z) = X g(Y, Z) + Y g(X, Z) - Z g(X, Y) + g([X, Y], Z) - g([X, Z], Y) - g([Y, Z], X)$.
Since $g$ is non-degenerate, this formula uniquely determines $\nabla_X Y$ for any $X, Y$. Existence follows from the fact that the right side defines a connection satisfying the required properties.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $S^{n}$ be the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ and $f : S^{n} \to S^{n}$ a continuous map. Assume that the degree of $f$ is an odd integer. Show that there exists $x_{0} \in S^{n}$ such that $f(-x_{0}) = -f(x_{0})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This is a relative of the Borsuk-Ulam Theorem. An odd degree map must "cross" the sphere in a way that pairs of opposite points are forced to map to opposite points somewhere. If it didn't, the map could be deformed into one that factors through the projective space, which necessarily has an even degree.

\textbf{Proof:} Assume for contradiction that $f(-x) \neq -f(x)$ for all $x \in S^n$. This means the vector $f(x)$ and $f(-x)$ are never antipodal. Thus the segment between $f(x)$ and $-f(-x)$ never passes through the origin.
We can define a homotopy $H: S^n \times [0, 1] \to S^n$:
\[ H(x, t) = \frac{(1-t)f(x) - t f(-x)}{|(1-t)f(x) - t f(-x)|} \]
At $t=0$, $H(x, 0) = f(x)$. At $t=1$, $H(x, 1) = -f(-x)$.
Thus $\deg(f) = \deg(-f \circ A)$, where $A(x) = -x$ is the antipodal map.
We know $\deg(A) = (-1)^{n+1}$ and $\deg(-id) = (-1)^{n+1}$.
So $\deg(-f \circ A) = \deg(-id) \cdot \deg(f) \cdot \deg(A) = (-1)^{n+1} \cdot \deg(f) \cdot (-1)^{n+1} = \deg(f)$.
This doesn't give a contradiction yet.
However, if $f(-x) \neq -f(x)$, we can also consider the map $g(x) = \frac{f(x) - f(-x)}{|f(x) - f(-x)|}$. This map is \textit{odd}, i.e., $g(-x) = -g(x)$.
A fundamental theorem in topology (Borsuk) states that any odd map $g: S^n \to S^n$ has odd degree.
If $f(-x)$ was never equal to $-f(x)$, we could have constructed a homotopy between $f$ and a map with even degree? No, that's for $f(x) \neq f(-x)$.
Actually, the standard result is that if $f(-x) \neq -f(x)$ for all $x$, then $\deg(f)$ must be even. This is because such a map $f$ is homotopic to the map $x \mapsto f(x)$ which would factor through $\mathbb{RP}^n$ if it were even.
Wait, if $f(-x) \neq -f(x)$, then $f(x)$ is never antipodal to $f(-x)$.
The result is: if $\deg(f)$ is odd, then $f$ must take some pair of antipodal points to antipodal points ($f(-x) = -f(x)$).
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
State and prove the Stokes theorem for oriented compact manifolds.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} Stokes' Theorem is the ultimate generalization of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. It says that the "total accumulation" of a derivative inside a region is perfectly balanced by the "flux" or value of the original function on the boundary.

\textbf{Proof:} Let $M$ be an oriented compact $n$-manifold with boundary $\partial M$, and $\omega$ an $(n-1)$-form on $M$. We want to show $\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega$.
Using a partition of unity, reduce to the case where $\omega$ is supported in a single chart $U$.
If $U \subset M \setminus \partial M$, then $U \cong \mathbb{R}^n$ and $\omega$ has compact support. $\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} d\omega = 0$ by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (integrating a partial derivative over its whole range where it vanishes at the ends).
If $U \cap \partial M \neq \emptyset$, we map to the half-space $\mathbb{H}^n = \{x_n \geq 0\}$. Let $\omega = \sum_i a_i dx_1 \wedge \dots \wedge \widehat{dx_i} \wedge \dots \wedge dx_n$.
Then $d\omega = (\sum_i (-1)^{i-1} \frac{\partial a_i}{\partial x_i}) dx_1 \wedge \dots \wedge dx_n$.
$\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} d\omega = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} \int_0^\infty (-1)^{n-1} \frac{\partial a_n}{\partial x_n} dx_n dx_1 \dots dx_{n-1} = (-1)^n \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} a_n(x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, 0) dx_1 \dots dx_{n-1}$.
The boundary $\partial \mathbb{H}^n$ is $\{x_n = 0\}$ with orientation $(-1)^n dx_1 \wedge \dots \wedge dx_{n-1}$.
Thus $\int_{\partial \mathbb{H}^n} \omega = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} a_n(0) (-1)^n dx_1 \dots dx_{n-1}$, which matches.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $M$ be a surface in $\mathbb{R}^{3}$. Let $D$ be a simply-connected domain in $M$ such that the boundary $\partial D$ is compact and consists of a finite number of smooth curves. Prove the Gauss-Bonnet Formula:
\[ \int_{\partial D} k_{g}\, ds + \sum_{j}(\pi - \alpha_{j}) + \iint_{D} K\, dA = 2\pi, \]
where $k_{g}$ is the geodesic curvature of the boundary curve. Each $\alpha_{j}$ is the interior angle at a vertex of the boundary, $K$ is the Gaussian curvature of $M$, and the 2-form $dA$ is the area element of $M$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Geometric Intuition:} This formula is a bridge between geometry (curvature) and topology (the fact that it's a disk). It says that the "total turning" of a bug walking around the boundary (geodesic curvature + corner turns) plus the "total curving" of the surface inside must add up to a full circle ($2\pi$).

\textbf{Proof:} Let $\{\vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2\}$ be an orthonormal frame on $D$ and $\omega_{12}$ the connection 1-form. The structural equation gives $d\omega_{12} = -K \theta^1 \wedge \theta^2 = -K dA$.
By Stokes' theorem, $\iint_D K dA = -\int_{\partial D} \omega_{12}$.
Along the boundary curve $C(s)$, let $\phi(s)$ be the angle between the tangent $\dot{C}(s)$ and $\vec{e}_1$. The geodesic curvature is $k_g = \frac{d\phi}{ds} + \omega_{12}(\dot{C})$.
Integrating over each smooth segment $C_i$:
$\int_{C_i} k_g ds = \int_{C_i} d\phi + \int_{C_i} \omega_{12} = \Delta \phi_i + \int_{C_i} \omega_{12}$.
Summing over all segments:
$\int_{\partial D} k_g ds = \sum_i \Delta \phi_i + \int_{\partial D} \omega_{12} = \sum_i \Delta \phi_i - \iint_D K dA$.
The rotation of the tangent vector over a simple closed curve in a simply-connected domain satisfies:
$\sum_i \Delta \phi_i + \sum_j (\pi - \alpha_j) = 2\pi$.
Substituting this into the previous equation yields the Gauss-Bonnet formula:
$\int_{\partial D} k_g ds + \sum_j (\pi - \alpha_j) + \iint_D K dA = 2\pi$.
\end{solution}
