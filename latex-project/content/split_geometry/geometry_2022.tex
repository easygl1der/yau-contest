\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:1}
The topological space $X$ is obtained by gluing two tetrahedra as illustrated by the figure. There is a unique way to glue the faces of one tetrahedron to the other so that the arrows are matched. The resulting complex has 2 tetrahedra, 4 triangles, 2 edges and 1 vertex.

Show that $X$ can not have the homotopy type of a compact manifold without boundary.

  \captionof{figure}{Two tetrahedra being glued (II)}
\end{center}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Euler Characteristic ($\chi$):}\footnote{For a finite CW complex, the Euler characteristic is defined as the alternating sum $\chi(X) = \sum_{k} (-1)^k c_k$, where $c_k$ is the number of $k$-dimensional cells. It is a topological invariant.} A fundamental numerical invariant that describes the "shape" of a space regardless of how it is bent or stretched.
    \item \textbf{CW Complex:}\footnote{A CW complex is a type of topological space constructed by inductively attaching $n$-dimensional cells (disks) to a $(n-1)$-dimensional skeleton along their boundaries.} A flexible framework for building spaces out of simple building blocks like vertices, edges, triangles, and tetrahedra.
    \item \textbf{PoincarĂ© Duality:}\footnote{PoincarĂ© Duality states that for a closed, oriented $n$-manifold $M$, the $k$-th homology group is isomorphic to the $(n-k)$-th cohomology group. A consequence for odd-dimensional closed manifolds is that their Euler characteristic must be zero.} A symmetry property between the homology and cohomology of a manifold.
    \item \textbf{Link of a Vertex:}\footnote{In a simplicial complex, the link of a vertex $v$ is the set of all simplices $\sigma$ such that $v$ is not a vertex of $\sigma$ but the join $v * \sigma$ is a simplex in the complex. For a 3-manifold, the link of every vertex must be a 2-sphere $S^2$.} A local representation of the "neighborhood" of a point.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
Imagine gluing the sides of two pyramids together. In a "normal" 3D space (a manifold), if you zoom in on any point, it should look exactly like a small piece of $\mathbb{R}^3$. However, in this specific construction, the way the edges and faces are identified at the single vertex $v$ might create a "pinch" or "singularity" that doesn't look like a standard ball. We can detect this either globally by looking at the Euler characteristic or locally by looking at the structure around the vertex.

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Calculate the Euler Characteristic of $X$:}
    The problem provides the cell counts for the complex: $c_0$ (vertices) = 1, $c_1$ (edges) = 2, $c_2$ (triangles) = 4, and $c_3$ (tetrahedra) = 2.
    \[ \chi(X) = c_0 - c_1 + c_2 - c_3 \]
    Substituting the values:
    \sidenote{Calculation: $\chi(X) = 1 - 2 + 4 - 2 = 1$.}
    
    \item \textbf{Analyze the Manifold Condition (Global):}
    If $X$ were a compact (closed) manifold without boundary of dimension $n=3$, its Euler characteristic must satisfy certain constraints. Specifically, for any odd-dimensional closed manifold $M$, $\chi(M) = 0$.
    \sidenote{PoincarĂ© Duality Consequence: For a 3-manifold $M$, $b_0 = b_3$ and $b_1 = b_2$ by symmetry. Thus $\chi(M) = b_0 - b_1 + b_2 - b_3 = b_0 - b_1 + b_1 - b_0 = 0$.}
    Since we found $\chi(X) = 1 \neq 0$, $X$ cannot be a closed 3-manifold.
    
    \item \textbf{Analyze the Manifold Condition (Local):}
    We can also examine the local structure. For a 3-dimensional simplicial complex to be a manifold, the link of every vertex must be homeomorphic to a 2-sphere $S^2$. Let $L = \operatorname{Lk}(v)$.
    The number of vertices, edges, and faces in $L$ is determined by the number of edges, triangles, and tetrahedra incident to $v$. Here, all edges, triangles, and tetrahedra contain the single vertex $v$.
    \sidenote{Link Cell Count: $V_L = 2$ (from edges), $E_L = 4$ (from triangles), $F_L = 2$ (from tetrahedra).}
    The Euler characteristic of the link is:
    \[ \chi(L) = 2 - 4 + 2 = 0. \]
    A 2-sphere has $\chi(S^2) = 2$. Since $0 \neq 2$, the link is not a sphere (it is likely a torus or Klein bottle), so $v$ is a singular point.
    
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:}
    Since $X$ violates both the global Euler characteristic requirement for odd-dimensional manifolds and the local link condition at the vertex, it cannot have the homotopy type of a compact manifold without boundary.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:2}
Suppose $(M, h)$ is a closed (i.e., compact without boundary) Riemannian manifold, and $h$ is a metric on $M$ with $\sec(h) \leq -1$, where $\sec(h)$ is the sectional curvature. Suppose $\Sigma$ is a closed minimal surface with genus $g$ in $(M, h)$. Show that
$$
\operatorname{Area} (\Sigma) \leq 4 \pi (g - 1).
$$
Remark: A minimal surface is an immersed surface with constant mean curvature 0.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sectional Curvature ($\sec$):}\footnote{Sectional curvature $\sec(P)$ is a measure of the curvature of a 2-dimensional plane $P$ in the tangent space at a point. It generalizes the Gaussian curvature of surfaces to higher-dimensional manifolds.} A quantity describing the curvature of the ambient space.
    \item \textbf{Minimal Surface:}\footnote{A minimal surface is an immersion $f: \Sigma \to M$ that is a critical point of the area functional. This is equivalent to the mean curvature vector $H$ vanishing identically. Intuitively, it is "stretched tight" like a soap film.} A surface where the average curvature from all directions is zero.
    \item \textbf{Gauss Equation:}\footnote{The Gauss equation relates the intrinsic Gaussian curvature $K_\Sigma$ of a surface to the ambient sectional curvature $\sec_M$ and the second fundamental form $A$: $K_\Sigma = \sec_M(T\Sigma) + \det(A)$.} A formula connecting internal and external geometry.
    \item \textbf{Gauss-Bonnet Theorem:}\footnote{For a closed surface $\Sigma$ of genus $g$, the total Gaussian curvature is $\int_\Sigma K_\Sigma dA = 2\pi \chi(\Sigma) = 4\pi(1-g)$.} A bridge between the geometry (curvature) and topology (genus) of a surface.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
In a manifold with negative curvature, space is "flaring out" in every direction. A minimal surface is "pulled tight." The Gauss equation tells us that a minimal surface in a negatively curved space must itself be negatively curvedâ€”even more so than the ambient space. Because negative curvature is limited by the "complexity" (genus) of the surface through the Gauss-Bonnet theorem, the area of such a surface cannot be arbitrarily large.

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Apply the Gauss Equation:}
    Let $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ be the principal curvatures of the surface $\Sigma$. The Gauss equation for a surface immersed in $M$ is:
    \[ K_\Sigma = \sec_M(T\Sigma) + \kappa_1 \kappa_2 \]
    where $K_\Sigma$ is the Gaussian curvature of the surface.
    
    \item \textbf{Use the Minimal Surface Condition:}
    By definition, a minimal surface has mean curvature $H = \kappa_1 + \kappa_2 = 0$.
    \sidenote{Curvature Relation: This implies $\kappa_2 = -\kappa_1$, so the product $\kappa_1 \kappa_2 = -\kappa_1^2$.}
    Substituting this into the Gauss equation:
    \[ K_\Sigma = \sec_M(T\Sigma) - \kappa_1^2 \]
    
    \item \textbf{Incorporate the Curvature Bound:}
    We are given $\sec(h) \leq -1$. Since $\kappa_1^2 \geq 0$, we have:
    \[ K_\Sigma = \sec_M(T\Sigma) - \kappa_1^2 \leq -1 - \kappa_1^2 \leq -1. \]
    This shows that the Gaussian curvature $K_\Sigma$ is at most $-1$ everywhere on the surface.
    
    \item \textbf{Integrate over the Surface:}
    Integrate the inequality $K_\Sigma \leq -1$ over the entire surface $\Sigma$:
    \[ \int_\Sigma K_\Sigma \, dA \leq \int_\Sigma (-1) \, dA = -\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma). \]
    
    \item \textbf{Apply Gauss-Bonnet Theorem:}
    The Gauss-Bonnet theorem states that $\int_\Sigma K_\Sigma \, dA = 4\pi(1 - g)$.
    Substituting this into our integral inequality:
    \sidenote{Inequality: $4\pi(1 - g) \leq -\operatorname{Area}(\Sigma)$.}
    Multiplying both sides by $-1$ reverses the inequality:
    \[ \operatorname{Area}(\Sigma) \leq -4\pi(1 - g) = 4\pi(g - 1). \]
    This completes the proof. Note that for this to be possible with positive area, we must have $g \geq 1$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:3}
For any topological space $X$, the $n$-th symmetric product of $X$ is the quotient of the Cartesian product $(X)^n$ by the action of the symmetric group $S_n$, which permutes the factors in $(X)^n$. This space is denoted by $\mathrm{SP}^n(X)$, and the topology is the natural quotient topology induced from $(X)^n$.

Show that $\mathrm{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1)$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{CP}^n$. Here $\mathbb{CP}^1$ and $\mathbb{CP}^n$ are equipped with the manifold topology.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Symmetric Product ($\mathrm{SP}^n$):}\footnote{The symmetric product $\mathrm{SP}^n(X)$ is the space of unordered multisets of $n$ points in $X$. It is the quotient space $X^n / S_n$ where $S_n$ acts by permuting the coordinates.} A space that treats collections of points as unlabeled.
    \item \textbf{Complex Projective Space ($\mathbb{CP}^n$):}\footnote{$\mathbb{CP}^n$ is the space of all lines passing through the origin in $\mathbb{C}^{n+1}$. It can be viewed as the set of ratios $[z_0 : z_1 : \dots : z_n]$ where not all $z_i$ are zero.} A fundamental space in complex geometry.
    \item \textbf{Fundamental Theorem of Algebra:}\footnote{The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra states that every non-zero single-variable polynomial of degree $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$ has exactly $n$ complex roots, counted with multiplicity.} The link between roots and coefficients.
    \item \textbf{Homeomorphism:}\footnote{A homeomorphism is a continuous, bijective map between two topological spaces that has a continuous inverse. It represents a "topological equivalence."} A way to say two spaces are "the same" topologically.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
Think of $\mathbb{CP}^1$ as the Riemann sphere (the complex plane plus a point at infinity). A point in $\mathrm{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1)$ is just a collection of $n$ points on this sphere. On the other hand, a point in $\mathbb{CP}^n$ can be viewed as the coefficients of a degree $n$ polynomial. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra says that every such polynomial is uniquely determined by its roots. Thus, the space of roots (points on the sphere) is "the same" as the space of coefficients (points in projective space).

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Define the Map $\Phi$:}
    Consider a multiset of $n$ points $\{[u_i : v_i]\}_{i=1}^n$ in $\mathrm{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1)$. Each $[u_i : v_i]$ corresponds to a linear factor $L_i(z, w) = v_i z - u_i w$.
    Define the map $\Phi: \mathrm{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1) \to \mathbb{CP}^n$ by multiplying these linear factors:
    \[ P(z, w) = \prod_{i=1}^n (v_i z - u_i w) = a_0 z^n + a_1 z^{n-1} w + \dots + a_n w^n. \]
    The map is $\Phi(\{[u_i : v_i]\}) = [a_0 : a_1 : \dots : a_n]$.
    
    \item \textbf{Verify Well-definedness:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Permutations:} Since the product of complex numbers is commutative, changing the order of the points $\{[u_i : v_i]\}$ does not change the coefficients $a_k$.
        \item \textbf{Scaling:} If we scale one point $[u_j : v_j]$ to $[\lambda u_j : \lambda v_j]$, the whole polynomial $P(z, w)$ is scaled by $\lambda$, which means the vector $[a_0 : \dots : a_n]$ represents the same point in $\mathbb{CP}^n$.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Check Bijectivity:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Injectivity:} By the unique factorization of polynomials over $\mathbb{C}$, the coefficients of $P(z, w)$ uniquely determine its roots (as an unordered set).
        \item \textbf{Surjectivity:} Any point $[a_0 : \dots : a_n] \in \mathbb{CP}^n$ defines a non-zero homogeneous polynomial of degree $n$, which by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra factors into $n$ linear terms, providing a multiset of $n$ roots.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Verify Homeomorphism:}
    The coefficients $a_k$ are elementary symmetric polynomials in the root coordinates $(u_i, v_i)$, making $\Phi$ continuous.
    \sidenote{Algebraic Relation: For $n=2$, $a_0 = v_1 v_2$, $a_1 = -(v_1 u_2 + v_2 u_1)$, $a_2 = u_1 u_2$. These are clearly continuous functions.}
    Since $\mathrm{SP}^n(\mathbb{CP}^1)$ is the quotient of a compact space $(\mathbb{CP}^1)^n$, it is compact. Since $\mathbb{CP}^n$ is Hausdorff, any continuous bijection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:4}
Let $M$ be a complete noncompact Riemannian manifold. $M$ is said to have the geodesic loops to infinity property if for any $[\alpha] \in \pi_1(M)$ and any compact subset $K \subset M$, there is a geodesic loop $\beta \subset M \setminus K$ such that $\beta$ is homotopic to $\alpha$.

Show that if a complete noncompact Riemannian manifold $M$ does not have the geodesic loops to infinity property, then there is a line in the universal cover $\widetilde{M}$.

Remark: A line is a geodesic $\gamma : (-\infty, \infty) \to M$ such that $\operatorname{dist}(\gamma(s), \gamma(t)) = |s - t|$; a geodesic loop is a curve $\beta : [0, 1] \to M$ that is a geodesic and $\beta(0) = \beta(1)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Complete Manifold:}\footnote{A Riemannian manifold is complete if its geodesics are defined for all time, or equivalently if it is a complete metric space. The Hopf-Rinow theorem guarantees that any two points in a complete manifold can be joined by a distance-minimizing geodesic.} A space where you can "walk forever" in any direction.
    \item \textbf{Line:}\footnote{A line is an infinite geodesic that minimizes distance between any two of its points. It is a very "straight" and "efficient" path.} The straightest possible path in a manifold.
    \item \textbf{Universal Cover ($\widetilde{M}$):}\footnote{The universal cover is a simply-connected space $\widetilde{M}$ that "unrolls" all the loops in $M$. Geodesic loops in $M$ lift to segments in $\widetilde{M}$ connecting points in the same fiber.} A space that removes all the holes and loops of the original manifold.
    \item \textbf{ArzelĂ -Ascoli Theorem:}\footnote{In this context, the ArzelĂ -Ascoli theorem allows us to take a limit of a sequence of geodesic segments to obtain an infinite geodesic, provided the segments are "tethered" to a compact region.} A tool for finding "limits" of curves.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
If the "loops to infinity" property fails, it means there's a specific "hole" (homotopy class) that cannot escape a central region $K$. As we try to push the basepoint of such a loop further and further out toward infinity, the loop must "stretch" longer and longer to reach back and wrap around that hole. In the limit, this stretched loop becomes so long and straight that it turns into an infinite "line" passing through the center.

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Negate the Property:}
    If $M$ does not have the property, there exists a class $[\alpha] \in \pi_1(M)$ and a compact set $K$ such that every geodesic loop in class $[\alpha]$ must intersect $K$.
    
    \item \textbf{Construct a Sequence of Points:}
    Let $\{p_j\}$ be a sequence of points in $M$ such that $\operatorname{dist}(p_j, K) \to \infty$. For each $p_j$, let $\beta_j$ be the \textit{shortest} geodesic loop based at $p_j$ in the class $[\alpha]$.
    
    \item \textbf{Tether to the Compact Set:}
    By assumption, each $\beta_j$ must pass through some point $q_j \in K$.
    \sidenote{Length Property: The length $L(\beta_j) \geq 2 \operatorname{dist}(p_j, q_j) \geq 2 \operatorname{dist}(p_j, K)$. Thus $L(\beta_j) \to \infty$.}
    
    \item \textbf{Lift to the Universal Cover:}
    Lift $\beta_j$ to a geodesic segment $\tilde{\beta}_j$ in $\widetilde{M}$ starting at $\tilde{q}_j$ (a lift of $q_j$) and ending at $g \cdot \tilde{q}_j$, where $g$ is the deck transformation corresponding to $[\alpha]$.
    Since $q_j \in K$, all $\tilde{q}_j$ lie in a compact region of $\widetilde{M}$.
    
    \item \textbf{Take the Limit:}
    Each $\tilde{\beta}_j$ is a minimizing segment in its homotopy class. As the distance to the "ends" $p_j$ goes to infinity, we use the ArzelĂ -Ascoli theorem to extract a subsequence of geodesics that converges to an infinite geodesic $\gamma: (-\infty, \infty) \to \widetilde{M}$.
    
    \item \textbf{Verify the Line Condition:}
    Because each $\tilde{\beta}_j$ was a shortest path in its class, the limit $\gamma$ must be distance-minimizing between any two of its points.
    \sidenote{Distance Property: For any $s, t$, $\operatorname{dist}(\gamma(s), \gamma(t)) = |s - t|$. This is because $\gamma$ is the limit of minimizing segments whose endpoints are going to infinity in both directions.}
    Thus, $\gamma$ is a line in $\widetilde{M}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:5}
A topological space $X$ is called an H-space if there exist $e \in X$ and $\mu : X \times X \to X$ such that $\mu(e, e) = e$ and the maps $x \mapsto \mu(e, x)$ and $x \mapsto \mu(x, e)$ are both homotopic to the identity map.

(a) Show that the fundamental group of an H-space is Abelian.
(b) Show that the sphere $S^{2022}$ is not an H-space.

Historic Remark: "H" was suggested by Jean-Pierre Serre in recognition of the contributions in Topology by Heinz Hopf.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{H-space:}\footnote{An H-space is a generalization of a topological group. It has a multiplication and an identity, but the group laws (like associativity and inverses) only need to hold "up to homotopy." Examples include Lie groups and loop spaces.} A space with a group-like multiplication.
    \item \textbf{Fundamental Group ($\pi_1$):}\footnote{The fundamental group $\pi_1(X, e)$ is the set of equivalence classes of loops based at $e$, with the group operation being path concatenation.} A group that tracks how loops can be formed in a space.
    \item \textbf{Cohomology Ring ($H^*$):}\footnote{The cohomology ring is a graded ring $H^*(X; \mathbb{Z})$ where the multiplication is given by the cup product. For a sphere $S^n$, the ring is $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2)$ where $x$ has degree $n$.} An algebraic structure that captures the "holes" of a space and how they interact.
    \item \textbf{Abelian Group:}\footnote{A group is Abelian if the order of the operation doesn't matter: $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$.} A group where elements commute.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
(a) In a normal space, you multiply loops by following one after the other. In an H-space, you can also multiply loops "point-by-point" using the space's own multiplication $\mu$. The existence of these two ways to combine loops forces them to commute.
(b) On a sphere, the cohomology has a very simple structure ($x^2=0$). If a sphere were an H-space, the multiplication would force its "topology" to be compatible with algebraic "cloning" (the cup product). This only works in specific dimensions (1, 3, 7).

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}

\textbf{Part (a): $\pi_1$ is Abelian}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Two Operations:}
    Let $f, g$ be loops in $X$ based at $e$. We have two operations:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Concatenation}: $f * g$ (follow $f$ then $g$).
        \item \textit{H-multiplication}: $f \odot g$ defined by $(f \odot g)(t) = \mu(f(t), g(t))$.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Use Eckmann-Hilton:}
    The identity loop $c_e$ acts as the identity for both operations.
    \sidenote{Interchange Law: $(f * g) \odot (h * k) \simeq (f \odot h) * (g \odot k)$. This comes from the continuity of $\mu$.}
    Apply the interchange law with $g = c_e$ and $h = c_e$:
    \[ (f * c_e) \odot (c_e * k) \simeq (f \odot c_e) * (c_e \odot k) \implies f \odot k \simeq f * k. \]
    Similarly, using $f = c_e$ and $k = c_e$:
    \[ (c_e * g) \odot (h * c_e) \simeq (c_e \odot h) * (g \odot c_e) \implies g \odot h \simeq h * g. \]
    \sidenote{Conclusion: $f * g \simeq f \odot g \simeq g * f$. Thus the fundamental group is Abelian.}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Part (b): $S^{2022}$ is not an H-space}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Induced Map on Cohomology:}
    Let $x \in H^{2022}(S^{2022})$ be the generator. The H-space multiplication $\mu: S^k \times S^k \to S^k$ induces a map $\mu^*$ on cohomology.
    By the identity property, $\mu^*(x) = x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x$.
    
    \item \textbf{Calculate the Square:}
    In $H^*(S^{2022})$, we know $x^2 = 0$ because the dimension is $2k$.
    So $\mu^*(x^2) = (\mu^*(x))^2 = (x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)^2$ must be zero.
    
    \item \textbf{Apply Graded Commutativity:}
    $(x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)^2 = (x^2 \otimes 1) + (x \otimes 1)(1 \otimes x) + (1 \otimes x)(x \otimes 1) + (1 \otimes x^2)$.
    Since $x^2 = 0$:
    \sidenote{Algebraic Step: $(x \otimes 1)(1 \otimes x) = x \otimes x$ and $(1 \otimes x)(x \otimes 1) = (-1)^{|x||x|} x \otimes x$. For $k=2022$, $(-1)^{k^2} = 1$.}
    So, $\mu^*(x^2) = 2(x \otimes x)$.
    
    \item \textbf{Contradiction:}
    For $S^{2022}$ to be an H-space, we need $2(x \otimes x) = 0$, which is impossible in $H^{4044}(S^{2022} \times S^{2022}; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. Thus, $S^{2022}$ is not an H-space.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
\label{geometry:2022:6}
A hypersurface $\Sigma \subset \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ is called a shrinker if it satisfies the equation
$$
H(x) = \frac{1}{2} \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle.
$$
Here $H$ is the mean curvature, which is $-\langle \operatorname{tr} A, \vec{n} \rangle$ where $A$ is the second fundamental form, $x$ is the position vector, and $\vec{n}$ is outer unit normal vector.

(a) Show that $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$, the sphere with radius $\sqrt{2n}$, is a shrinker.
(b) Show that any compact shrinker without boundary must intersect with $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites \& Definitions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shrinker:}\footnote{Shrinkers are self-similar solutions to the Mean Curvature Flow (MCF). They represent the shapes that a surface might take just before it disappears into a point under the flow.} A surface that shrinks toward the origin while keeping its shape.
    \item \textbf{Mean Curvature ($H$):}\footnote{The mean curvature $H$ is the sum of the principal curvatures. For a sphere of radius $R$ in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, $H = n/R$.} A measure of how "curved" a surface is on average.
    \item \textbf{Second Fundamental Form ($A$):}\footnote{The second fundamental form describes how the normal vector of the surface changes as you move along the surface.} A tensor encoding the extrinsic curvature.
    \item \textbf{Maximum Principle:}\footnote{The maximum principle states that a smooth function on a compact manifold cannot have its Laplacian be strictly positive at a maximum point. It's a powerful tool for proving bounds in geometry.} A tool for analyzing the behavior of functions at their peaks.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Geometric Intuition:}
(a) For a sphere, the curvature is constant and points inward. The position vector points outward. The shrinker equation just sets a specific "sweet spot" for the radius where these two forces balance out.
(b) Think of $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$ as a "natural size" for shrinkers. If a shrinker were entirely inside this sphere, it would have to be "too curved" to satisfy the equation. If it were entirely outside, it would be "too flat." Thus, any compact shrinker must "cross" this special radius.

\textbf{Formal Proof/Detailed Steps:}

\textbf{Part (a): Verify the Sphere}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Calculate Geometric Terms:}
    For a sphere $S^n(R)$, at any point $x$, the outer unit normal is $\vec{n} = x/R$.
    The mean curvature is $H = n/R$.
    
    \item \textbf{Substitute into the Equation:}
    The right side of the shrinker equation is $\frac{1}{2} \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \langle x, x/R \rangle = \frac{|x|^2}{2R} = \frac{R^2}{2R} = \frac{R}{2}$.
    The left side is $H = \frac{n}{R}$.
    
    \item \textbf{Solve for $R$:}
    \sidenote{Equation: $n/R = R/2 \implies R^2 = 2n \implies R = \sqrt{2n}$.}
    This shows $S^n(\sqrt{2n})$ satisfies the shrinker equation.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Part (b): Intersection Proof}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Consider the Distance Function:}
    Let $f(x) = |x|^2$ be the squared distance from the origin. We compute the Laplacian of $f$ on the surface $\Sigma$.
    \sidenote{Identity: $\Delta_\Sigma |x|^2 = 2n - 2H \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle$. This is a standard identity for hypersurfaces.}
    
    \item \textbf{Apply the Shrinker Condition:}
    Substitute $H = \frac{1}{2} \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle$ into the identity:
    \[ \Delta_\Sigma |x|^2 = 2n - 2\left(\frac{1}{2} \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle\right) \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle = 2n - \langle x, \vec{n} \rangle^2. \]
    
    \item \textbf{Use the Maximum Principle:}
    At the point $x_{max}$ where $\Sigma$ is furthest from the origin, $|x|^2$ is a maximum, so $\Delta_\Sigma |x|^2 \leq 0$. At this point, $x$ must be parallel to $\vec{n}$, so $\langle x, \vec{n} \rangle^2 = |x|^2 = R_{max}^2$.
    \sidenote{Bound: $2n - R_{max}^2 \leq 0 \implies R_{max} \geq \sqrt{2n}$.}
    Similarly, at the minimum point $x_{min}$, $\Delta_\Sigma |x|^2 \geq 0$, which implies $2n - R_{min}^2 \geq 0 \implies R_{min} \leq \sqrt{2n}$.
    
    \item \textbf{Conclusion:}
    The surface $\Sigma$ has points with $|x| \leq \sqrt{2n}$ and points with $|x| \geq \sqrt{2n}$. By continuity, it must intersect the sphere $|x| = \sqrt{2n}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{solution}
