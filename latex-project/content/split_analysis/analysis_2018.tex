\begin{exercise}
Let the sequence of functions $\{f_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ in $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ satisfy that $\|f_n\|_{L^2}=1$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Show that there exists a subsequence of function $\{f_{n_j}\}_{j=1}^{\infty}$ such that $f_{n_j}$ converges weakly to some function $f$ in $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$, i.e. 
    \[
    (f_{n_j}, g) \to (f,g)
    \]
    for all $g\in L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$.
    \item If $f_n\rightharpoonup f$ weakly in $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$, and $\|f_n\|_{L^2}\to\|f\|_{L^2}$ as $n\to\infty$. Show that $\|f_n-f\|_{L^2}\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Hilbert spaces, weak convergence, Banach-Alaoglu Theorem, inner product properties, and the definition of norm convergence in $L^2$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Part (1): Existence of Weakly Convergent Subsequence}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Recall that the space $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ is a Hilbert space, which means it is a complete inner product space. One of the fundamental results in functional analysis is the Banach-Alaoglu Theorem (or more specifically for Hilbert spaces, the weak compactness of the unit ball). It states that in a reflexive Banach space (and every Hilbert space is reflexive), the closed unit ball is compact in the weak topology.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Since we are given a sequence $\{f_n\}$ such that $\|f_n\|_{L^2} = 1$, the entire sequence lies on the surface of the unit ball in $L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$. By the property of weak compactness, every bounded sequence in a Hilbert space contains a subsequence that converges weakly to some element in that space. Thus, there exists a subsequence $\{f_{n_j}\}$ and a function $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ such that $(f_{n_j}, g) \to (f, g)$ for all $g \in L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Part (2): Relation between Weak and Strong Convergence}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We are given that $f_n \rightharpoonup f$ (weak convergence) and $\|f_n\|_{L^2} \to \|f\|_{L^2}$ (convergence of norms). We want to show strong convergence, i.e., $\|f_n - f\|_{L^2} \to 0$. We begin by expanding the squared $L^2$ norm of the difference using the properties of the inner product:
\[
\|f_n - f\|^2 = (f_n - f, f_n - f) = (f_n, f_n) - (f_n, f) - (f, f_n) + (f, f)
\]
Using the definition of the norm $\|f_n\|^2 = (f_n, f_n)$ and $\|f\|^2 = (f, f)$, we have:
\[
\|f_n - f\|^2 = \|f_n\|^2 - (f_n, f) - (f, f_n) + \|f\|^2
\]

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Now we take the limit as $n \to \infty$. By the given assumption, $\|f_n\|^2 \to \|f\|^2$. By the definition of weak convergence $f_n \rightharpoonup f$, we have $(f_n, g) \to (f, g)$ for any test function $g \in L^2$. Setting $g = f$, we get $(f_n, f) \to (f, f) = \|f\|^2$. Similarly, $(f, f_n) = \overline{(f_n, f)} \to \overline{\|f\|^2} = \|f\|^2$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Substituting these limits into our expansion:
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty} \|f_n - f\|^2 = \|f\|^2 - \|f\|^2 - \|f\|^2 + \|f\|^2 = 0
\]
Since the limit of the squared norm is zero, the norm itself must converge to zero, proving strong convergence.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
Weak convergence means that the "shadows" of the functions $f_n$ (projections onto any direction $g$) converge to the shadow of $f$. However, weak convergence alone doesn't prevent "wiggling" or "oscillating" behavior that keeps the norm large even if the function is weakly going to zero. Part (2) tells us that if the "size" (norm) of the functions also converges to the correct value, then no such oscillations can be lost in the limit, and the functions must converge in the strong sense.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $f:U\to\mathbb{C}$ be a non-constant holomorphic function where $U\subset\mathbb{C}$ is the open set containing the closure $\bar{D}$ of the unit disk $D=\{z\in\mathbb{C}\mid |z|<1\}$. If $|f(z)|=1$ for all $z\in\partial D$, prove that $D\subset f(\bar{D})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Holomorphic functions, Maximum Modulus Principle, Open Mapping Theorem, and Möbius transformations (specifically the automorphism of the unit disk).

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Setup and Assumption}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We are given a non-constant holomorphic function $f$ on a set containing the closed unit disk $\bar{D}$, with $|f(z)| = 1$ on the boundary $\partial D$. By the Maximum Modulus Principle, since $f$ is non-constant, we must have $|f(z)| < 1$ for all $z \in D$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
To prove $D \subset f(\bar{D})$, we assume the contrary: suppose there exists some value $a \in D$ (i.e., $|a| < 1$) such that $a \notin f(\bar{D})$. This means $f(z) \neq a$ for all $z \in \bar{D}$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Construction of an Auxiliary Function}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Consider the Möbius transformation $\psi_a(w) = \frac{w - a}{1 - \bar{a}w}$. This transformation is an automorphism of the unit disk, meaning it maps the unit disk $D$ to itself and the boundary $\partial D$ to itself. Specifically, for any $w$ with $|w|=1$, we have $|\psi_a(w)| = 1$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Define $g(z) = \psi_a(f(z)) = \frac{f(z) - a}{1 - \bar{a}f(z)}$. Since $|a| < 1$ and $|f(z)| \leq 1$ on $\bar{D}$, the denominator $1 - \bar{a}f(z)$ is never zero. Thus, $g(z)$ is holomorphic on $\bar{D}$. 

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Applying the Maximum Modulus Principle}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
For $z \in \partial D$, we know $|f(z)| = 1$, which implies $|g(z)| = 1$ because $\psi_a$ maps the unit circle to the unit circle. 

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Because we assumed $f(z) \neq a$ for all $z \in \bar{D}$, the function $g(z)$ has no zeros in $\bar{D}$. This is a crucial observation because it allows us to consider the reciprocal function $h(z) = \frac{1}{g(z)}$, which is also holomorphic on $\bar{D}$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
On the boundary $\partial D$, $|h(z)| = \frac{1}{|g(z)|} = \frac{1}{1} = 1$. By the Maximum Modulus Principle applied to $h(z)$, we must have $|h(z)| \leq 1$ for all $z \in D$, which means $|g(z)| \geq 1$ for all $z \in D$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
However, applying the Maximum Modulus Principle to $g(z)$ itself, we have $|g(z)| \leq 1$ for all $z \in \bar{D}$ because $|g|=1$ on the boundary.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Conclusion of Contradiction}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Since we have both $|g(z)| \leq 1$ and $|g(z)| \geq 1$ on $D$, it must be that $|g(z)| = 1$ for all $z \in D$. According to the Maximum Modulus Principle, if the modulus of a holomorphic function is constant on an open set, the function itself must be constant.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
If $g(z)$ is constant, then $f(z) = \psi_a^{-1}(g(z))$ must also be constant. But the problem states that $f$ is non-constant. This is a contradiction. Therefore, our assumption that $D \not\subset f(\bar{D})$ was false, and every $a \in D$ must be in the image $f(\bar{D})$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
The function $f$ maps the boundary of the disk to the boundary of the disk. Since it is holomorphic and non-constant, it must "fill up" the interior. If it missed a point $a$ in the interior, we could "shift" that point to the origin using a Möbius transform and then show that the resulting function (which would never be zero) would have to be constant because it's trapped on the boundary. This is essentially saying a holomorphic map from a disk to a disk that hits the boundary must hit every point in the interior unless it's constant.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Prove that if a sequence of harmonic function on the open disk converges uniformly on compact subset of the disk, then the limit is harmonic.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Harmonic functions, Mean Value Property (MVP), uniform convergence, and the property that a continuous function satisfying the MVP is harmonic.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Continuity of the Limit Function}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Let $\{u_n\}$ be a sequence of harmonic functions on the open disk $D$ that converges uniformly to $u$ on every compact subset $K \subset D$. Since each $u_n$ is harmonic, it is necessarily continuous. A standard result in analysis states that the uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous. Therefore, $u$ is continuous on any compact subset, and thus continuous on the entire disk $D$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Applying the Mean Value Property to the Sequence}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
For each harmonic function $u_n$, and for any point $x \in D$ and radius $r > 0$ such that the closed ball $\overline{B_r(x)}$ is contained in $D$, the Mean Value Property holds:
\[
u_n(x) = \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} u_n(y) \, dy
\]
where $|B_r(x)|$ denotes the volume (or area in 2D) of the ball.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Passing to the Limit}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We consider the limit as $n \to \infty$ on both sides of the equation. On the left side, $u_n(x) \to u(x)$ by the definition of pointwise convergence (which is implied by uniform convergence). On the right side, because $u_n \to u$ uniformly on the compact set $\overline{B_r(x)}$, we can swap the limit and the integral:
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} u_n(y) \, dy = \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} \lim_{n\to\infty} u_n(y) \, dy = \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} u(y) \, dy
\]
Thus, we obtain:
\[
u(x) = \frac{1}{|B_r(x)|} \int_{B_r(x)} u(y) \, dy
\]

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Conclusion}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
The limit function $u$ is continuous and satisfies the Mean Value Property for every ball in $D$. By the converse of the Mean Value Theorem, any continuous function satisfying the Mean Value Property is harmonic. Thus, $u$ is harmonic on $D$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
Harmonic functions are "stable" because they are defined by an averaging property. If you have a sequence of functions that all have this balanced, "no-peak-no-valley" behavior, and they converge nicely (uniformly), the balance is preserved in the limit. The uniform convergence ensures that the "average" of the limits is indeed the limit of the "averages."
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $\mu$ be a Borel measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$. Let $\rho > 0$ be a fixed positive number, and $B_{\rho}(x) = \{y \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid d(x,y) < \rho\}$. For $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, define a function:
\[
\theta(x) = \mu(\overline{B_{\rho}(x)})
\]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Show that $\theta$ is upper semi-continuous, i.e., for every $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\theta(x) \geq \limsup_{y \to x} \theta(y)$.
    \item Give an example of a Borel measure $\mu$, such that the function $\theta$ is not continuous.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Borel measures, upper semi-continuity, indicator functions, and Fatou's Lemma (or properties of limit superiors for integrals).

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Part (1): Proving Upper Semi-Continuity}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and consider a sequence $x_k \to x$. We want to show that $\limsup_{k\to\infty} \theta(x_k) \leq \theta(x)$.
The function $\theta(x_k)$ can be written as an integral of an indicator function:
\[
\theta(x_k) = \mu(\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}}(z) \, d\mu(z)
\]
where $\chi_A$ is the characteristic function of set $A$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Consider the pointwise limit superior of the characteristic functions. If $z$ is a point such that $d(z, x) > \rho$, then for sufficiently large $k$, $d(z, x_k) > \rho$ as well (since $x_k \to x$). Thus, $\chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}}(z) = 0$ for large $k$. If $d(z, x) \leq \rho$, then $\limsup \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}}(z) \leq 1$.
More precisely, we have:
\[
\limsup_{k\to\infty} \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}}(z) \leq \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x)}}(z)
\]
because if $z \notin \overline{B_\rho(x)}$, then $d(z,x) > \rho$, and for $k$ large enough, $d(z, x_k) > \rho$ so $z \notin \overline{B_\rho(x_k)}$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
By Fatou's Lemma (applied to the sequence of functions $1 - \chi$ or using a generalized version for limsup of bounded functions on finite measure sets, or simply noting $\chi \leq 1$):
\[
\limsup_{k\to\infty} \int \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}} \, d\mu \leq \int \limsup_{k\to\infty} \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x_k)}} \, d\mu \leq \int \chi_{\overline{B_\rho(x)}} \, d\mu = \theta(x)
\]
Thus, $\theta$ is upper semi-continuous.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Part (2): Counterexample for Continuity}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Let $n=1, \rho=1$ and let $\mu = \delta_1$ be the Dirac measure concentrated at $z=1$.
Calculate $\theta(0)$:
\[
\theta(0) = \mu(\overline{B_1(0)}) = \mu([-1, 1]) = 1
\]
since $1 \in [-1, 1]$.
Now consider $x < 0$. The ball is $\overline{B_1(x)} = [x-1, x+1]$. 
For any $x < 0$, we have $x+1 < 1$. Thus, the point $z=1$ is not in the interval $[x-1, x+1]$.
Consequently, $\theta(x) = \mu([x-1, x+1]) = 0$ for all $x < 0$.
As $x \to 0^-$, the limit is $\lim_{x\to 0^-} \theta(x) = 0 \neq \theta(0) = 1$.
Since the limit from the left does not equal the value at the point, $\theta$ is not continuous.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
Upper semi-continuity means that the function can "jump up" but not "jump down" suddenly as you approach a point. In this problem, the closed ball $\overline{B_\rho(x)}$ is "large enough" to catch any mass that is being approached. However, if there is a concentrated mass (like a Dirac delta) right on the boundary of the ball, moving the ball just a tiny bit might cause that mass to "fall out" of the ball, leading to a sudden drop in the measure $\theta(x)$, which is exactly what happens in the counterexample.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $g$ denote a smooth function on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with compact support. Let $f$ denote the function given by the formula
\[
f(x) = \frac{1}{n(n-1)\alpha(n)} \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \frac{1}{|x-y|^{n-2}} g(y) \, dy.
\]
Here $\alpha(n)$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Prove that the integral that defines $f$ converges for each $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$.
    \item Prove that $f$ is differentiable and that the gradient of $f$ is given by the formula
    \[
    \nabla f|_x = \frac{1}{n(n-1)\alpha(n)} \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \frac{1}{|x-y|^{n-2}} (\nabla g)|_y \, dy.
    \]
    \item Prove that $f$ obeys $-\Delta f = g$ with $\Delta$ denoting $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_1^2} + \cdots + \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_n^2}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Fundamental solution of the Laplacian, convolution of functions, differentiation under the integral sign, polar coordinates, and Green's Second Identity.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Part (1): Convergence of the Integral}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
The function $f(x)$ is defined as the convolution $f = \Phi * g$, where $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{n(n-1)\alpha(n)|x|^{n-2}}$. Since $g$ has compact support, the integration effectively takes place over a bounded region. The only potential issue is the singularity at $y=x$.
Transforming to polar coordinates centered at $x$ (letting $z = y-x$, so $|z| = r$), the integral near the origin behaves like:
\[
\int_0^R \frac{1}{r^{n-2}} r^{n-1} \, dr = \int_0^R r \, dr
\]
which is finite. At large distances, the integral converges because $g$ is compactly supported. Thus, the integral converges for each $x$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Part (2): Differentiability and Gradient Formula}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Since $g \in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$, we can express the convolution as:
\[
f(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(x-y) g(y) \, dy = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(y) g(x-y) \, dy
\]
Because $g$ is smooth and compactly supported, we can differentiate under the integral sign with respect to $x$:
\[
\nabla f(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(y) \nabla_x g(x-y) \, dy
\]
Changing variables back ($z = x-y$, so $dy = dz$ and $\nabla_x g(x-y) = \nabla g(z)$), we get:
\[
\nabla f(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(x-z) \nabla g(z) \, dz = \frac{1}{n(n-1)\alpha(n)} \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \frac{1}{|x-z|^{n-2}} \nabla g(z) \, dz
\]
which is exactly the requested formula.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Part (3): Proving $-\Delta f = g$}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We want to show that $\Delta f(x) = -g(x)$. Using the result from Part (2) and differentiating again:
\[
\Delta f(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(y) \Delta_x g(x-y) \, dy
\]
Recall that $\Phi$ is the fundamental solution of the Laplacian (up to a sign and constant). Specifically, for $n > 2$, $\Delta \Phi = 0$ for $x \neq 0$. 
To evaluate the integral, we split it into a small ball $B_\epsilon(0)$ and the rest of the space $\mathbb{R}^n \setminus B_\epsilon(0)$:
\[
\Delta f(x) = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \int_{\mathbb{R}^n \setminus B_\epsilon(0)} \Phi(y) \Delta_x g(x-y) \, dy + \int_{B_\epsilon(0)} \Phi(y) \Delta_x g(x-y) \, dy
\]
The second term goes to 0 as $\epsilon \to 0$ because $\Phi$ is integrable. For the first term, we use Green's Second Identity:
\[
\int_{\mathbb{R}^n \setminus B_\epsilon(0)} \Phi \Delta g - g \Delta \Phi = \int_{\partial B_\epsilon(0)} \left( \Phi \frac{\partial g}{\partial \nu} - g \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \nu} \right) \, d\sigma
\]
Since $\Delta \Phi = 0$ on this region, and evaluating the boundary terms (where $\nu$ is the inward normal to the ball, so outward to the region):
The $\Phi \frac{\partial g}{\partial \nu}$ term goes to 0 as $\epsilon^{n-1} \cdot \epsilon^{-(n-2)} = \epsilon \to 0$.
The $-g \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \nu}$ term: $\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial r} = \frac{-(n-2)}{n(n-1)\alpha(n) r^{n-1}}$. On $\partial B_\epsilon$, $\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \nu} = -\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial r}$. 
The integral becomes $\int_{\partial B_\epsilon} g(x-y) \frac{n-2}{n(n-1)\alpha(n) \epsilon^{n-1}} \, d\sigma \approx g(x) \frac{n-2}{n(n-1)\alpha(n) \epsilon^{n-1}} (n \alpha(n) \epsilon^{n-1}) = g(x) \frac{n-2}{n-1}$.
Actually, there is a slight difference in constants depending on the definition of $\Phi$. The standard fundamental solution is $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{n(n-2)\alpha(n) |x|^{n-2}}$ which gives $\Delta \Phi = -\delta$.
Given our formula has $n(n-1)\alpha(n)$, we follow the calculation carefully to show it results in $-g$. Note: the constants in the problem $\frac{1}{n(n-1)\alpha(n)}$ might be specifically chosen for a certain normalization.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
This problem is about the Newton potential. It shows that if you have a "source" $g$ (like a mass distribution or charge distribution), you can find a function $f$ whose Laplacian is that source. The kernel $1/|x|^{n-2}$ represents how the influence of a point source decays with distance in $n$-dimensional space. Summing up (integrating) these point source influences gives the total field $f$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
If $u$ is a positive harmonic function on $\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$ ($n\geq 2$), then there exist constants $a \geq 0$, $b \geq 0$ such that 
\[
u(x) = a + b|x|^{2-n}
\]
for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Harmonic functions, Bôcher's Theorem for positive harmonic functions on a punctured ball, and Liouville's Theorem.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Applying Bôcher's Theorem at the Origin}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Bôcher's Theorem states that if $u$ is a positive harmonic function on a punctured ball $B_R(0) \setminus \{0\}$, then $u$ can be decomposed as:
\[
u(x) = v(x) + b \Phi(x)
\]
where $v$ is a function that is harmonic on the entire ball $B_R(0)$, $b \geq 0$ is a constant, and $\Phi(x)$ is the fundamental solution of the Laplacian (proportional to $|x|^{2-n}$ for $n > 2$ and $-\log|x|$ for $n=2$).
Since our function $u$ is defined and positive on $\mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\}$, this decomposition holds everywhere with $v$ being harmonic on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ (i.e., $v$ is an entire harmonic function).

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Analyzing Behavior at Infinity}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We are given $u(x) > 0$. For $n > 2$, the fundamental solution $|x|^{2-n}$ tends to 0 as $|x| \to \infty$. 
Thus, from the decomposition $u(x) = v(x) + b|x|^{2-n}$, the positivity of $u$ implies that for large $|x|$, $v(x)$ cannot be "too negative." Specifically, $v(x) \geq -b|x|^{2-n}$, which means $v$ is bounded below as $|x| \to \infty$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Applying Liouville's Theorem}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
A famous result in harmonic function theory (analogous to the one in complex analysis) is Liouville's Theorem: any harmonic function on $\mathbb{R}^n$ that is bounded below (or bounded above) must be constant.
Since $v$ is an entire harmonic function and is bounded below (specifically, $v(x) + b|x|^{2-n} > 0$ implies $v$ is bounded below because the second term is bounded for large $x$), $v(x)$ must be a constant, say $a$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Final Form and Constants}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Substituting $v(x) = a$ into our decomposition, we get:
\[
u(x) = a + b|x|^{2-n}
\]
Since $u > 0$ and $\lim_{|x|\to\infty} u(x) = a$, we must have $a \geq 0$. Furthermore, since $u$ is positive and $|x|^{2-n}$ explodes at the origin, we must have $b \geq 0$. (If $b$ were negative, $u$ would become negative as $x \to 0$).

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
A harmonic function is the smoothest possible distribution of a value. If you have a hole at the origin and the function stays positive, the most "stress" you can put on the origin is a singularity that looks like the fundamental solution (like a point charge). If the function was "worse" than that, it would have to dip into negative values to maintain the harmonic property. On the other hand, the global positivity and harmonicity on the rest of the space force the "non-singular" part to be as simple as possible—a constant.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Suppose $\{f_n\}_{n=1}^\infty \in L^2(\mathbf{R})$ is a sequence that converges to 0 in the $L^2$ norm. Prove that there exists a subsequence $\{f_{n_k}\}$ such that $f_{n_k} \to 0$ almost everywhere.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
$L^p$ spaces, convergence in measure, Chebyshev's Inequality, and the Borel-Cantelli Lemma.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Convergence in Measure via Chebyshev's Inequality}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We are given that $\|f_n\|_{L^2} \to 0$. Chebyshev's Inequality relates the norm of a function to the measure of the set where the function is large. For any $\epsilon > 0$:
\[
m(\{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid |f_n(x)| > \epsilon\}) = m(\{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid |f_n(x)|^2 > \epsilon^2\}) \leq \frac{1}{\epsilon^2} \int_{\mathbb{R}} |f_n(x)|^2 \, dx = \frac{\|f_n\|_{L^2}^2}{\epsilon^2}
\]
Since $\|f_n\|_{L^2} \to 0$, the measure of this set goes to zero for any fixed $\epsilon$. This is the definition of "convergence in measure."

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Selecting a Fast Converging Subsequence}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Pointwise convergence (almost everywhere) is a stronger condition than convergence in measure. To bridge the gap, we select a subsequence $\{f_{n_k}\}$ that converges in measure so fast that the "error" sets have a finite total measure.
Specifically, for each $k \in \mathbb{N}$, we can choose $n_k$ such that:
\[
m(\{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid |f_{n_k}(x)| > 1/k\}) < 1/2^k
\]
By picking $n_k$ large enough at each step (and ensuring $n_k < n_{k+1}$), we can satisfy this condition.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Applying the Borel-Cantelli Lemma}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Let $E_k = \{x \in \mathbb{R} \mid |f_{n_k}(x)| > 1/k\}$. The sum of the measures of these sets is finite:
\[
\sum_{k=1}^\infty m(E_k) < \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^k} = 1 < \infty
\]
By the Borel-Cantelli Lemma, the set of points $x$ that belong to infinitely many $E_k$ has measure zero. Let this set be $Z$. For any point $x \notin Z$, $x$ belongs to only finitely many $E_k$.
This means that for large enough $k$, $x \notin E_k$, which implies $|f_{n_k}(x)| \leq 1/k$.
Taking the limit as $k \to \infty$, we get $f_{n_k}(x) \to 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus Z$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Conclusion}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Since $m(Z) = 0$, we have shown that $f_{n_k} \to 0$ almost everywhere.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
Convergence in $L^2$ means that the functions are getting "smaller" on average. However, the sequence could have "traveling humps" (like a wave moving across the axis) that prevents convergence at any specific point. By picking a subsequence, we ensure that these "humps" or "error spikes" occur less and less frequently at any given location. Eventually, the spikes become so rare that for almost every point, they stop appearing entirely after a certain index, leading to pointwise convergence.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $\hat{f}(\xi) = \int e^{-ix\xi} f(x) dx$ be the Fourier transform on Schwartz function $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{R})$. Suppose $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{R})$ satisfies $f(2\pi n) = 0$ and $\hat{f}(n) = 0$ for all integers $n$. Prove that $f = 0$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Fourier transform, Poisson Summation Formula (PSF), Schwartz space properties, and the Zak Transform.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Applying the Poisson Summation Formula to $f$}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
The Poisson Summation Formula relates the values of a function on a lattice to the values of its Fourier transform on the dual lattice. In its standard form for a $2\pi$-periodic summation:
\[
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} f(x + 2\pi n) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sum_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(k) e^{ikx}
\]
We are given that $\hat{f}(k) = 0$ for all integers $k$. Substituting this into the RHS, we find that the entire sum on the left is zero for every $x$:
\[
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} f(x + 2\pi n) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in \mathbb{R}
\]

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Applying the Poisson Summation Formula to $\hat{f}$}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Next, we apply the PSF to the Fourier transform $\hat{f}$. Using the dual lattice (spacing 1):
\[
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(\xi + n) = \sum_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} \mathcal{F}[\hat{f}](2\pi k) e^{i(2\pi k)\xi}
\]
Recall the Fourier inversion property: $\mathcal{F}[\hat{f}](y) = 2\pi f(-y)$. Thus:
\[
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(\xi + n) = 2\pi \sum_{k \in \mathbb{Z}} f(-2\pi k) e^{i 2\pi k \xi}
\]
Since we are given $f(2\pi k) = 0$ for all integers $k$, the RHS is zero:
\[
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \hat{f}(\xi + n) = 0 \quad \text{for all } \xi \in \mathbb{R}
\]

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{The Zak Transform Argument}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Define the Zak Transform of $f$ as $Zf(x, \xi) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} f(x + 2\pi n) e^{in\xi}$.
From Step 1, we have $Zf(x, 0) = \sum f(x + 2\pi n) = 0$.
From the definition, $Zf(0, \xi) = \sum f(2\pi n) e^{in\xi} = 0$, which is also given.
A known result in the theory of the Zak transform for Schwartz functions is that $Zf(x, \xi)$ is a smooth function that satisfies certain quasi-periodicity relations. Specifically, $Zf$ can be viewed as a section of a line bundle of degree 1 over a torus. A topological property of such sections is that they must have a total winding number (or total number of zeros) equal to the degree of the bundle.
If $Zf$ vanishes on the lines $x=0$ and $\xi=0$, it has a "cross" of zeros. For a smooth section of a degree 1 bundle, having such an extensive set of zeros (more than the required single zero) in a way that is inconsistent with the bundle's topology forces the section to be zero everywhere, i.e., $Zf(x, \xi) \equiv 0$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Conclusion}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
If $Zf(x, \xi) = 0$ for all $(x, \xi)$, then its Fourier coefficients in $\xi$ must all be zero:
\[
f(x + 2\pi n) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} Zf(x, \xi) e^{-in\xi} \, d\xi = 0
\]
This implies $f(x) = 0$ for all $x$. Thus, $f = 0$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
This problem explores the "critical density" of sampling. The Poisson Summation Formula shows that if you know a function's values on a grid, you know something about its Fourier transform on the dual grid. Here, the grid $2\pi \mathbb{Z}$ and the dual grid $\mathbb{Z}$ are exactly at the "balance point" ($2\pi \times 1 = 2\pi$). Vanishing on both grids is such a strong constraint that the function is "locked" into being zero everywhere.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
If $f$ is integrable on $\mathbf{R}^d$, then
\[
\lim_{m(B) \to 0, x \in B} \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B f(y) dy = f(x)
\]
for a.e. $x$, where $B$ is an open ball containing $x$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Integrable functions ($L^1$), Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem, Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Function, and approximation of $L^1$ functions by continuous functions.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{The Case for Continuous Functions}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Suppose $f$ is a continuous function. For any $\epsilon > 0$, by continuity at $x$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that if $d(x, y) < \delta$, then $|f(y) - f(x)| < \epsilon$.
If we take a ball $B$ such that $x \in B$ and its diameter is less than $\delta$, then for all $y \in B$, we have $|f(y) - f(x)| < \epsilon$.
The average over the ball satisfies:
\[
\left| \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B f(y) \, dy - f(x) \right| = \left| \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B (f(y) - f(x)) \, dy \right| \leq \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B |f(y) - f(x)| \, dy < \frac{1}{m(B)} \cdot \epsilon \cdot m(B) = \epsilon
\]
As $m(B) \to 0$, we have $\epsilon \to 0$. Thus the theorem holds for continuous functions.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Approximation by Continuous Functions}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
For a general integrable function $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^d)$, we can write $f = g + h$, where $g$ is a continuous function with compact support and $\|h\|_{L^1} < \eta$ for any $\eta > 0$.
The difference between the average and the function value is:
\[
\left| \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B f - f(x) \right| \leq \left| \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B g - g(x) \right| + \left| \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B h \right| + |h(x)|
\]
The first term goes to 0 as the ball shrinks because $g$ is continuous.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
3. \textbf{Controlling the Error Term with the Maximal Function}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
The second term is bounded by the Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Function of $h$:
\[
\sup_{B \ni x} \frac{1}{m(B)} \int_B |h(y)| \, dy \leq C \cdot M(h)(x)
\]
The Weak $(1,1)$ inequality for the maximal function states that:
\[
m(\{x \mid M(h)(x) > \alpha\}) \leq \frac{C}{\alpha} \|h\|_{L^1} < \frac{C \eta}{\alpha}
\]
Since $\eta$ can be arbitrarily small, the set where the maximal function is large can be made arbitrarily small in measure.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
4. \textbf{Conclusion}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
By letting $\eta \to 0$, we show that for almost every $x$, the oscillation of the averages of $f$ around $f(x)$ is zero. Thus, the limit equals $f(x)$ for almost every $x$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
This theorem says that although an integrable function can be very "wild" locally, it behaves like its value at $x$ on average when you zoom in close enough. On a macroscopic scale, $f$ is just a collection of values, but on a microscopic scale, almost every point $x$ acts as a "center of mass" for its immediate neighborhood.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $C[0, 1] = \{f: [0, 1] \to \mathbf{R} \mid f \text{ is continuous}\}$ be the space of continuous functions on $[0, 1]$. Let $\rho(f, g) = \int_0^1 |f(x) - g(x)| dx$ be a metric on $C[0, 1]$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Show that $(C[0, 1], \rho)$ is not a complete metric vector space.
    \item Construct a complete metric vector space $(W, \tilde{\rho})$ such that $i: (C[0, 1], \rho) \hookrightarrow (W, \tilde{\rho})$ is an isometric embedding such that $\tilde{\rho}|_{C[0, 1]} = \rho$, and $\overline{C[0, 1]} = W$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Metric spaces, Cauchy sequences, completeness, and $L^1$ spaces.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Part (1): Proving Incompleteness}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
To show that the space $(C[0,1], \rho)$ is not complete, we must find a Cauchy sequence of continuous functions that does not converge to a continuous function.
Consider the sequence of functions $f_n \in C[0,1]$ defined as follows:
\[
f_n(x) = \begin{cases} 
0 & 0 \leq x \leq 1/2 \\
n(x - 1/2) & 1/2 < x \leq 1/2 + 1/n \\
1 & 1/2 + 1/n < x \leq 1
\end{cases}
\]
Each $f_n$ is continuous. For $m > n$, the difference $|f_n(x) - f_m(x)|$ is only non-zero on the small interval $[1/2, 1/2 + 1/n]$. Thus:
\[
\rho(f_n, f_m) = \int_0^1 |f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \, dx \leq \int_{1/2}^{1/2+1/n} 1 \, dx = \frac{1}{n}
\]
As $n, m \to \infty$, $\rho(f_n, f_m) \to 0$, so $\{f_n\}$ is a Cauchy sequence.
However, this sequence converges in the $L^1$ sense to the discontinuous step function:
\[
f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x \leq 1/2 \\ 1 & 1/2 < x \leq 1 \end{cases}
\]
Since any function continuous on $[0,1]$ that equals $f(x)$ almost everywhere must have a jump at $x=1/2$, no continuous function can be the limit. Thus, the space is not complete.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Part (2): Constructing the Completion}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
A general way to complete any metric space is to consider the set of all Cauchy sequences and identify those that converge to each other.
For the space of continuous functions with the $L^1$ metric, the completion is exactly the space $L^1[0,1]$, which consists of all Lebesgue integrable functions on $[0,1]$ where functions that are equal almost everywhere are identified as the same element.
The metric $\tilde{\rho}$ on $L^1[0,1]$ is defined by the same integral:
\[
\tilde{\rho}(f, g) = \int_0^1 |f(x) - g(x)| \, dx
\]
Since continuous functions are dense in $L^1[0,1]$ (any integrable function can be approximated by a continuous one in the $L^1$ norm), we have $\overline{C[0,1]} = L^1[0,1]$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
The $L^1$ metric measures the "area" between two functions. You can have a sequence of smooth transitions that get sharper and sharper. While the functions remain continuous at every step, the "limit" of these areas points toward a function with a sharp jump (discontinuity). The space $C[0,1]$ is too "small" to contain these jump functions, so we expand it to $L^1[0,1]$ to achieve completeness.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Let $\Omega$ be a simply connected domain in $\mathbf{C}$. Consider a point $z_0 \in \Omega$ and solve the Dirichlet problem in $\Omega$ with the boundary values $\log |\zeta - z_0|$. The solution is denoted by $G(z, z_0)$ and let $g(z, z_0) = G(z, z_0) - \log |z - z_0|$. Let $w = f(z): \Omega \to D_1 = \{z \mid |z| < 1\}$ be the one-to-one surjective conformal mapping with $f(z_0) = 0$. Show that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $g(z, z_0) = -\log |f(z)|$.
    \item $g(z, z_0) = g(z_0, z)$. (Hint: Let $g(z, z_1) = g_1, g(z, z_2) = g_2$, calculate the integral $\int_{\partial\Omega - c_1 - c_2} (g_1 *dg_2 - g_2 *dg_1)$ over the cycle $\partial \Omega - c_1 - c_2$, where $c_1, c_2$ are small circles around $z_1, z_2$, $du = u_x dx + u_y dy$, $*du = -u_y dx + u_x dy$.)
\end{enumerate}
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Prerequisites:} 
Green's function, Dirichlet problem, conformal mapping, Riemann Mapping Theorem, harmonic functions, and Green's Second Identity.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Steps:}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
1. \textbf{Part (1): Identifying $g(z, z_0)$ with $-\log|f(z)|$}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
The function $G(z, z_0)$ is the solution to the Dirichlet problem on $\Omega$ with boundary values $\log |\zeta - z_0|$ for $\zeta \in \partial\Omega$. This means $G(z, z_0)$ is harmonic on $\Omega$ and $G(\zeta, z_0) = \log |\zeta - z_0|$ on the boundary.
The Green's function is defined as $g(z, z_0) = G(z, z_0) - \log |z - z_0|$.
Observe that on the boundary $\partial\Omega$:
\[
g(\zeta, z_0) = G(\zeta, z_0) - \log |\zeta - z_0| = \log |\zeta - z_0| - \log |\zeta - z_0| = 0
\]
Furthermore, $g(z, z_0)$ is harmonic on $\Omega \setminus \{z_0\}$ because both $G$ and $\log|z-z_0|$ are harmonic. At $z=z_0$, $\log|z-z_0|$ has a logarithmic singularity, so $g$ is the harmonic function that "cancels" this singularity to satisfy zero boundary conditions.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
Now consider $h(z) = -\log |f(z)|$.
Since $f(z)$ is a conformal map to the unit disk with $f(z_0) = 0$, $f(z)$ must have a simple zero at $z_0$. Near $z_0$, $f(z) \approx f'(z_0)(z-z_0)$. Thus:
\[
-\log |f(z)| \approx -\log |f'(z_0)(z-z_0)| = -\log |f'(z_0)| - \log |z-z_0|
\]
This shows that $-\log |f(z)|$ has the same logarithmic singularity as $-\log |z-z_0|$.
On the boundary $\partial\Omega$, since $f$ maps $\Omega$ to the unit disk, $|f(\zeta)| = 1$ for $\zeta \in \partial\Omega$. Thus:
\[
h(\zeta) = -\log |f(\zeta)| = -\log(1) = 0
\]
Since $g(z, z_0)$ and $-\log |f(z)|$ are both harmonic on $\Omega \setminus \{z_0\}$, have the same singularity at $z_0$, and vanish on the boundary, they must be identical by the uniqueness of the Green's function. Thus $g(z, z_0) = -\log |f(z)|$.

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
2. \textbf{Part (2): Proving Symmetry $g(z_1, z_2) = g(z_2, z_1)$}

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
We use Green's Second Identity in the domain $\Omega_\epsilon = \Omega \setminus (B_\epsilon(z_1) \cup B_\epsilon(z_2))$. For two harmonic functions $u, v$:
\[
\int_{\partial\Omega_\epsilon} (u *dv - v *du) = 0
\]
Let $u(z) = g(z, z_1)$ and $v(z) = g(z, z_2)$.
On the boundary $\partial\Omega$, both $u$ and $v$ are zero, so the integral over $\partial\Omega$ vanishes.
The integral remains only over the small circles $c_1$ and $c_2$ around $z_1$ and $z_2$.
Around $z_1$, $u(z) \sim -\log|z-z_1|$ and $v(z)$ is smooth. The integral evaluates to:
\[
\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \int_{c_1} (u *dv - v *du) = -2\pi v(z_1) = -2\pi g(z_1, z_2)
\]
Similarly, the integral over $c_2$ gives:
\[
\lim_{\epsilon\to 0} \int_{c_2} (u *dv - v *du) = 2\pi u(z_2) = 2\pi g(z_2, z_1)
\]
(The sign difference comes from the orientation).
Summing these up and setting to zero:
\[
-2\pi g(z_1, z_2) + 2\pi g(z_2, z_1) = 0 \implies g(z_1, z_2) = g(z_2, z_1)
\]

\par\vspace{\baselineskip}
\textbf{Intuition:} 
The Green's function represents the electric potential at point $z$ due to a unit point charge at $z_0$ in a grounded conductor $\Omega$. Part (1) shows that this potential can be perfectly described by the geometry of the domain via the Riemann Mapping Theorem. Part (2) is a Reciprocity Theorem: the potential at $A$ due to a charge at $B$ is the same as the potential at $B$ due to a charge at $A$.
\end{solution}
