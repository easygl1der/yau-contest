\section{2025 Mathematical Physics}

\begin{exercise}
A bead of mass $m$ is constrained to move on a hoop of radius $R$ rotating with constant angular velocity $\omega$ around the vertical axis.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
The "bead on a rotating hoop" is a classic example of a system exhibiting a pitchfork bifurcation driven by a parameter, the rotation frequency $\omega$. When the hoop is stationary or spinning slowly, the bead's lowest energy state is sitting stably at the very bottom, just like a simple pendulum. However, as the hoop spins faster, the bead experiences an outward "centrifugal force" in the rotating frame. At a critical angular velocity $\Omega$, this outward push exactly balances the inward pull of gravity at the bottom. For any $\omega > \Omega$, the bottom position becomes unstable, and the bead is pushed outwards to a new stable equilibrium at a non-zero angle. This sudden change in the qualitative behavior of the system's equilibria is a macroscopic manifestation of spontaneous symmetry breaking.

\textbf{1.} Let $\theta$ be the polar angle from the bottom of the hoop. The velocity in spherical coordinates is $\mathbf{v} = R\dot{\theta}\hat{\theta} + R\omega\sin\theta\hat{\phi}$.
The Lagrangian is:
\[ L = \frac{1}{2}mR^2(\dot{\theta}^2 + \omega^2\sin^2\theta) + mgR\cos\theta \]
Equations of motion: $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\theta}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta}$:
\[ mR^2\ddot{\theta} = mR^2\omega^2\sin\theta\cos\theta - mgR\sin\theta \]
\[ \ddot{\theta} = \sin\theta(\omega^2\cos\theta - g/R) \]

\textbf{2.} The Hamiltonian is $H = p_\theta \dot{\theta} - L = \frac{1}{2}mR^2\dot{\theta}^2 - \frac{1}{2}mR^2\omega^2\sin^2\theta - mgR\cos\theta$.
$H$ is conserved since $\partial L/\partial t = 0$.
The fixed-frame energy is $E = \frac{1}{2}mR^2(\dot{\theta}^2 + \omega^2\sin^2\theta) - mgR\cos\theta$.
$H = E - mR^2\omega^2\sin^2\theta$. $E$ is not conserved because the constraint is time-dependent.

\textbf{3.} Equilibrium occurs at $\sin\theta(\omega^2\cos\theta - g/R) = 0$.
The bottom is $\theta = 0$. Stability requires $V_{\text{eff}}''(0) > 0$ where $V_{\text{eff}} = -\frac{1}{2}mR^2\omega^2\sin^2\theta - mgR\cos\theta$.
$V_{\text{eff}}' = -mR^2\omega^2\sin\theta\cos\theta + mgR\sin\theta$.
$V_{\text{eff}}'' = -mR^2\omega^2\cos(2\theta) + mgR\cos\theta$.
At $\theta=0$, $V_{\text{eff}}''(0) = mR(g - R\omega^2)$. Stable if $\omega < \Omega \equiv \sqrt{g/R}$.
For $\omega > \Omega$, a new stable equilibrium appears at $\cos\theta_0 = g/(R\omega^2)$.

\textbf{4.} For $\omega < \Omega$, $\omega_{\text{osc}}^2 = \frac{V_{\text{eff}}''(0)}{mR^2} = \frac{g}{R} - \omega^2$.
For $\omega > \Omega$, $\omega_{\text{osc}}^2 = \frac{V_{\text{eff}}''(\theta_0)}{mR^2} = \frac{-mR^2\omega^2(2\cos^2\theta_0-1) + mgR\cos\theta_0}{mR^2} = \omega^2\sin^2\theta_0 = \omega^2(1 - (g/R\omega^2)^2)$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Wave packet propagation in the $z$ direction.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
When we discuss a light beam or a wave packet (like a laser pointer's beam) propagating in the $z$-direction, it is physically impossible for the wave to be perfectly confined in the transverse (xy) plane while having no longitudinal ($z$) electric or magnetic field components. This is a direct consequence of Gauss's law ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$). If the amplitude drops to zero as you move away from the center of the beam (transverse profile), there must be a compensating spatial variation in the $z$-direction. In this exercise, we see how a finite transverse profile necessarily generates a small longitudinal field $E_z$. This further connects to the spin and orbital angular momentum of light, where the "swirling" of the phase and polarization state gives the wave mechanical angular momentum along the direction of propagation.

\textbf{1.} For $\omega=ck$, $E = E_0 e^{i(kz-\omega t)}$ satisfies $\nabla^2 E - \frac{1}{c^2}\ddot{E} = 0$ if $\nabla_\perp E_0$ is neglected.
$\mathbf{B}^{(0)} = \hat{\mathbf{z}} \times \mathbf{E}^{(0)}$ follows from $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{1}{c}\dot{\mathbf{B}}$.

\textbf{2.} Energy density $u = \frac{1}{8\pi}(|E|^2 + |B|^2) = \frac{1}{4\pi}E_0^2$.
$\langle U \rangle = \int d^2x u = \frac{1}{4\pi} \int E_0^2 dx dy = \frac{\mathcal{A}^2}{4\pi} (2\pi \sigma^2) = \frac{\mathcal{A}^2 \sigma^2}{2}$.

\textbf{3.} From $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0 \implies \partial_x E_x + \partial_y E_y + \partial_z E_z = 0$.
$ik E_z = -(\partial_x E_x + \partial_y E_y) \implies E_z = \frac{i}{k}(\partial_x E_x + \partial_y E_y)$.
For circular polarization $\mathbf{E}_\perp \propto (\hat{x}+i\hat{y})E_0$, $E_z = \frac{i}{k \sqrt{2}}(\partial_x + i\partial_y)E_0$.

\textbf{4.} Superposition of plane waves with slightly different $\mathbf{k}$ vectors. Each plane wave has $\mathbf{E} \perp \mathbf{k}$. If $\mathbf{k}$ is not along $\hat{z}$, $\mathbf{E}$ must have a $z$-component.

\textbf{5.} $\langle L^z \rangle = \int d^2x (\mathbf{r} \times \langle \mathbf{S} \rangle / c^2)^z$.
The orbital angular momentum part comes from the phase gradient, and the spin part from polarization.
Result: $\langle L^z \rangle = \langle U \rangle / \omega$.

\textbf{6.} Ratio is $1/\omega$. Since $E = \hbar\omega$ and $L = \hbar$ for a circularly polarized photon, the ratio $L/E = 1/\omega$ is expected.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Shifted harmonic oscillator $H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2 - q\mathcal{E}x$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
The shifted harmonic oscillator models a particle in a parabolic potential well subjected to an additional constant force, such as a charged particle in a uniform electric field $\mathcal{E}$ or a mass on a spring under gravity. Physically, a constant force doesn't change the stiffness of the spring (the $\omega$ parameter); it only pulls the equilibrium position of the particle to a new location. In quantum mechanics, we can handle this elegantly without solving a completely new differential equation. By introducing a spatial translation operator, we shift the coordinates so the new Hamiltonian looks exactly like the old, unshifted one, plus a constant energy offset. This shift operator is directly related to coherent states, which are the closest quantum states to classical particle motion.

\textbf{1.} Complete the square: $H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 (x - \frac{q\mathcal{E}}{m\omega^2})^2 - \frac{q^2\mathcal{E}^2}{2m\omega^2}$.
Let $x_0 = \frac{q\mathcal{E}}{m\omega^2}$. $H = e^{-A} H_0 e^A + B$ where $e^{-A}x e^A = x - x_0$.
This is a translation operator $A = \frac{i}{\hbar} x_0 p$. $B = -\frac{q^2\mathcal{E}^2}{2m\omega^2}$.
The spectrum is $E_n = \hbar\omega(n+1/2) + B$.

\textbf{2.} $A = \frac{i}{\hbar} x_0 p = \frac{x_0}{l\sqrt{2}}(a^\dagger - a)$.
Probability is $|\langle 0 | e^{A} | 0 \rangle|^2 = e^{-|\alpha|^2}$ where $\alpha = x_0/(l\sqrt{2})$.

\textbf{3-5.} Transitions are governed by the displacement operator. The dipole moment oscillates at frequency $\omega$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Ising model on a triangle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
The Ising model represents interacting magnetic dipoles (spins) that can point either "up" or "down". In this microscopic framework, neighboring spins want to align with each other if the interaction is ferromagnetic ($J > 0$), or anti-align if it is antiferromagnetic ($J < 0$). A triangle is the simplest geometry where we encounter "frustration." If $J < 0$, spin 1 wants to be opposite to spin 2, and spin 2 opposite to spin 3. But then spin 3 cannot simultaneously be opposite to both spin 1 and spin 2! Because there are only 3 spins here, we can simply write down all $2^3 = 8$ possible configurations to calculate exactly the partition function $Z$. The partition function gives us a complete statistical description, allowing us to find macroscopic properties like heat capacity, magnetization, and susceptibility purely from the microscopic rules.

\textbf{1.} $Z = \sum_{\sigma_i = \pm 1} e^{-\beta E} = e^{3\beta J + 3\beta h} + e^{3\beta J - 3\beta h} + 3e^{-\beta J + \beta h} + 3e^{-\beta J - \beta h}$.
$Z = 2e^{3\beta J} \cosh(3\beta h) + 6e^{-\beta J} \cosh(\beta h)$.

\textbf{2.} $F = -T \ln Z$. $S = - \frac{\partial F}{\partial T}$.

\textbf{3.} For $h=0$, $Z = 2e^{3\beta J} + 6e^{-\beta J}$. $U = - \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} = - \frac{6Je^{3\beta J} - 6Je^{-\beta J}}{2e^{3\beta J} + 6e^{-\beta J}} = -3J \frac{e^{4\beta J}-1}{e^{4\beta J}+3}$.
Specific heat $C = dU/dT$.

\textbf{4.} $M = \frac{1}{\beta} \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial h}$. At low $T$ ($J > 0$), $M \approx 3\tanh(3\beta h)$. $\chi \approx 9\beta \sim 1/T$.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Gravitational waves from binary black holes.
\end{exercise}

\begin{center}

  \\
  \captionof{figure}{Two black holes in circular motion}
\end{center}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
In General Relativity, moving masses cause ripples in the fabric of spacetime, just as a boat causes ripples when moving through water. However, because gravity is a tensor field, you need a changing "quadrupole moment" (an asymmetric mass distribution moving in specific ways) to generate these waves. The classic and most powerful source is a binary system of two black holes spiraling into one another. As they orbit, they continuously radiate energy and angular momentum outward in the form of gravitational waves. Losing energy causes their orbit to shrink, making them orbit faster, which in turn causes them to radiate even more intensely. This run-away process creates a characteristic "chirp" signal: both the amplitude and the frequency of the gravitational waves increase rapidly right before the two black holes violently merge.

\textbf{1.} Trajectory: $x = r_1 \cos \Omega t$, $y = r_1 \sin \Omega t$.
$x^2 - y^2 = r_1^2 \cos 2\Omega t$. Second derivative gives $-4\Omega^2 r_1^2 \cos 2\Omega t$.
$h_+ = -\frac{4G\mu \Omega^2 r^2}{c^4 L} \frac{1+\cos^2\theta}{2} \cos(2\Omega t)$.
Frequency $f = \Omega/\pi$ (twice the orbital frequency).

\textbf{2.} $P = \frac{32}{5} \frac{G^4}{c^5} \frac{m_1^2 m_2^2 (m_1+m_2)}{r^5}$.

\textbf{3.} $\frac{df}{dt} = \frac{96}{5} \pi^{8/3} \left(\frac{G \mathcal{M}}{c^3}\right)^{5/3} f^{11/3}$ where $\mathcal{M}$ is chirp mass.

\textbf{4.} Coalescence time $T_C \propto r_0^4$.

\textbf{5.} Estimation leads to $r_0 \approx 0.01$ au.
\end{solution}

\begin{exercise}
Conformal scalar field.
\end{exercise}

\begin{solution}
\textbf{Motivation \& Intuition:} 
In curved spacetime, the geometry of the universe itself can interact with fields (like the scalar field). "Conformal invariance" is a special symmetry where the physics remains unchanged if you locally rescale the spacetime metric—equivalent to stretching or shrinking your rulers and clocks point by point. A standard massless scalar field does not have this symmetry in curved space. However, by adding a specific "non-minimal coupling" term between the field and the Ricci scalar curvature $R$ of the spacetime, we can restore this conformal symmetry. Remarkably, in a cosmologically expanding universe (like de Sitter space), this curvature coupling acts mathematically like an effective mass for the scalar field. This means the geometry itself gives the otherwise massless particles an apparent mass!

\textbf{1.} $\Delta = -1$.

\textbf{2-4.} The action becomes invariant if $\xi = 1/6$.

\textbf{5.} The metric is de Sitter in conformal coordinates. The term $\xi R \phi^2$ acts as a mass term with $m^2 = \xi R$. For de Sitter $R = 12H^2$, so $m^2 = 2H^2$.
\end{solution}
